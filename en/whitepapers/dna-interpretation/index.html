Abstract

This paper gives a deterministic, reproducible account of how a one-dimensional DNA sequence is interpreted into structure and function. The account rests on one asymmetry, which we state plainly and then test on real genomes: a genome strongly encodes position — where structural features sit and what shape the molecule locally takes — while the quantity of any given output (how much, how often, how fast) is set largely by the cellular runtime rather than by the letters of the sequence.

We organize this as two layers. Layer 1 is a generative specification written in the sequence: a small number of universal physical and recognition rules (“two grammars”) act on position-specific values to fix a four-dimensional arrangement of structural constraints — stiffness shells, anchors, motors, and loops (the A4 arrangement). Among the arrangements a sequence admits, the one realized is the one that minimizes a residual-stress measure under fixed physics; this is why a particular structure forms rather than an equally plausible alternative. Layer 2 is the cellular runtime — ion environment, supercoiling, molecular crowding, cell-cycle state, and competition for limited shared resources — which executes the specification and sets quantities.

We make the account operational and verifiable, and we read the structures the sequence specifies as phase-separated condensates: the “stiffness shell” of the framework is the firm interface of such a domain, with two settable properties — an interfacial tension γ\gamma that Layer 1 (sequence composition) sets, and a fill fraction ϕ\phi that Layer 2 (runtime) sets. We test this on real genomes, tagging every claim as confirmed, refined, or hypothesis. Sequence to chromatin phase. In budding yeast a sequence-only signal is organized around measured nucleosome-depleted regions (1.66×1.66\times enrichment, z=−38.8z=-38.8) and the packing units sit at a preferred, helically quantized spacing (≈171\approx\!171 bp peak, ≈11\approx\!11 bp period) exactly as the framework’s capacity identity predicts. In human, GC composition predicts which Hi-C compartment a region occupies with AUROC up to 0.840.84 (and 0.920.92 on cross-sample consensus), on its own merits rather than as a gene-density proxy (partial correlation −0.52-0.52). The same picture makes a falsifiable negative prediction that we confirm: compartment boundaries do not sit at sharp sequence transitions (AUROC 0.520.52, chance), because an interface location is emergent, not encoded. Importance predicts fixedness. Across four independent, non-circular measures of functional importance in E. coli — codon position, functional category, pangenome presence, and codon-usage bias — conservation rises monotonically with importance, and the relationship is exponential: pN/pS≈exp(−5.91+0.087ENC)\mathrm{pN}/\mathrm{pS}\approx\exp(-5.91+0.087\,\mathrm{ENC}), with aggregate R2=0.975R^2=0.975 and the same form on a second independent axis. The deepest valleys (genes with zero nonsynonymous change) are ∼9×\sim\!9\times enriched for the most central machinery, and 18S ribosomal RNA holds its functional core across ∼1\sim\!1 billion years of divergence while its peripheral segments drift freely.

What is established and what is new. The individual biological observations on which we build are established in the literature. What this paper contributes is (a) the organizing framework and its formalization, including the reading of the stiffness shell as a viscoelastic condensate interface; (b) a falsifiable two-layer decomposition that states, for each output, which part is written in the sequence and where the rest comes from, sharpened into element-position (encoded) versus boundary-position (emergent); (c) a reproducible, multi-organism set of analyses, every result tagged by status; and (d) an exponential conservation law and six falsifiable rules that point to a geometric (landscape) account of why important things are fixed — which we present, honestly, as a hypothesis whose direct tests require external structural, network, and fitness data we specify. The framework does not replace high-accuracy black-box predictors; it makes their predictions decomposable and points to where the remaining signal must lie. All code and data manifests are released as a separate reproducibility bundle; the core interpretation pipeline is embedded in the text.

The framework

Introduction: what it means to interpret DNA

This paper is written so that a reader meeting these ideas for the first time can follow them end to end and, with the code we provide, carry out the same interpretation on a genome of their own. We therefore build every concept from the ground up and define each term where it first appears, including terms that specialists treat as obvious. Nothing in the argument requires the reader to take a claim on authority: each quantitative statement is tied to a procedure that can be run and checked.

Two ways to read a genome

A genome is a long text written in a four-letter alphabet — the bases adenine, cytosine, guanine, and thymine, written A, C, G, T. The two strands of the double helix pair these bases in a fixed way (A with T, G with C), so one strand determines the other and we may speak of “the sequence” as a single string. To read or interpret that string can mean two very different things, and most confusion about “what DNA does” comes from running the two together.

The first kind of reading recovers the rules written in the string itself. Some stretches of sequence resist being bent and wrapped around the spool-like proteins that package DNA; others bend easily. Some short patterns are recognized by the protein machines that copy or read the genome; most are not. Some regions pull open into single strands at a low energy cost; others hold shut. These regularities live in the letters, and they hold wherever those letters appear — in any cell, of any tissue, on any day. A rule of this kind is valuable precisely because it compresses: one statement accounts for many positions at once.

The second kind of reading asks how a particular stretch of sequence is actually used in a particular cell at a particular moment: how often a given starting point for DNA copying is used in one round of replication; how much of a gene’s product is made; how early or late, in the cell’s internal schedule, some event occurs. This information lives only partly in the letters. Most of it lives in the state of the cell — the concentrations of ions and proteins, the mechanical strain on the DNA, how crowded the interior is, where in its life cycle the cell sits.

Throughout, we will need two standard words for “where a value comes from.” A value is cis (Latin for “on the same side”) when it is determined by the local sequence itself — its composition, the short patterns it contains, its length, its local shape. A value is trans (“across”) or environmental when it is set by something acting from elsewhere — a diffusing protein, the ionic environment, the cell-cycle clock. The distinction is not a matter of opinion: one can test whether a quantity is cis by asking how well it is predicted from sequence alone.

Conflating the two readings produces two opposite errors. If one expects the sequence to explain everything, then every measurement the sequence fails to predict is dismissed as “noise.” If one expects every measured quantity to be a hidden message in the sequence, then values the cell sets on the fly are mistaken for grammar that was never there. The discipline of this paper is to keep the two readings apart and to measure, for each output, how much of it is written in the letters and where the rest comes from.

The two-layer proposition

We compare the genome to source code and the living cell to a running program. Source code does not act on its own; it is a specification that a runtime executes. The same code, run on different machines or with different inputs, produces different behavior, yet the program’s structure is fixed by the code. We propose that DNA relates to the cell in the same way, and we separate two layers.

  • Layer 1 — the generative specification. This is the position-determined information written in the one-dimensional sequence: the specification of what structural feature goes where. It is produced by applying a few universal physical and chemical rules (the “two grammars” of Section 2.2, to come) to position-specific cis values. Layer 1 fixes where and what.

  • Layer 2 — the cellular runtime. This is the temporal and environmental state of the cell that executes the specification: ion concentrations, supercoiling (the over- or under-winding of the helix), molecular crowding, the cell-cycle clock, competition for limited resources, and the large-scale three-dimensional arrangement of the genome in the nucleus. Layer 2 sets how much and when.

The central proposition is deliberately simple, and the rest of the paper tests it: the genome strongly encodes position (Layer 1), while the runtime sets quantity (Layer 2). The two layers are not in tension; they are different jobs. Position is the kind of thing a fixed text can specify well. Quantity — how often, how much, how fast — depends on conditions that the text cannot know in advance, and so it is set at run time.

There is a physical form of this division, developed in full in Section 4, that we state here so the reader carries it through Part I. The structures the sequence specifies are phase-separated condensates, and such a domain has exactly two properties to set: how firm its boundary is — an interfacial tension γ\gamma — and how much material fills it — a fill fraction ϕ\phi. Layer 1 (the sequence composition) sets the interfacial-tension propensity γ\gamma: which phase a region prefers. Layer 2 (the runtime) sets the fill fraction ϕ\phi: the quantity. “Position as grammar” is the γ\gamma-knob and “quantity as runtime” is the ϕ\phi-knob. One consequence is worth flagging now because it sharpens the proposition rather than softening it: “position” is really two things. Element-position — where a gene or a recognized site sits — is written in the sequence. Boundary-position — where the interface between two domains falls — is emergent from the competing phases at run time, and is not promised by the local letters. The two-layer claim, made precise, is that the sequence strongly encodes phase propensity and element-position, while the runtime sets fill and the boundary locations follow from the physics. Part II tests both halves, including the negative one.

A construction analogy makes the division concrete, and we use it only to fix intuition; the quantitative claims below stand on data, not on the metaphor. A blueprint fixes how many rooms a house has and where the load-bearing frame goes; it does not, by itself, build the house. The construction site supplies materials — without them nothing is built — and the same blueprint yields a somewhat different house in the tropics than at the pole, with reinforcement added on site where conditions demand. Yet the large structure (the number of rooms, the placement of the frame) is fixed by the blueprint and is robust to site conditions, while finishes, timing, and amounts are set on site. In the same spirit, Layer 1 fixes the structural plan and Layer 2 supplies the materials and sets the amounts. Figure 1 summarizes the whole picture.

From prediction to explanation

Modern sequence-to-output predictors are very good at one thing: given a sequence, they return a number, often with high accuracy on data drawn from the same distribution they were trained on. They do not, however, say why a sequence yields one structure rather than another that looks equally plausible on energetic grounds. The protein-folding problem is the classic case: a single one-dimensional chain reliably folds to one three-dimensional shape, and accurate predictors can now anticipate that shape, yet this sharpens rather than answers the question of why that shape is the necessary outcome.

This paper is built to answer the “why.” For a given sequence it states, in order: the physical and recognition tendencies the sequence carries (the two grammars, Section 2.2); the arrangement of structural constraints those tendencies produce (the A4 arrangement, Section 3.1); and the principle that selects, among the arrangements the sequence admits, the one actually realized — minimum residual stress under fixed physics (Section 3.4). Where a quantity is not fixed by the sequence, the framework says so explicitly and points to the runtime measurement that does fix it.

This stance does not compete with high-accuracy predictors; it composes with them. The framework partitions each output into a part that explicit rules already account for and a residual that a flexible learner — supplied with the right inputs, namely sequence plus runtime plus multiple conditions — can close. The result is a hybrid that is both high-reaching and interpretable, and that remains decomposable: one can always ask which part of a prediction came from a stated rule and which from the learned residual. The practical payoff is that interpretation becomes a program with named parts rather than a single opaque number.

What is established, and what is new

We state our position precisely rather than with a blanket disclaimer. The individual biological observations this paper builds on are established in the literature: that the local sequence influences how DNA bends and how easily it opens; that specific short patterns mark the sites where DNA copying begins; that those sites compete for a limited supply of the factors needed to fire; that one value can be used by many downstream processes at once; and that the spacing of packaging proteins differs between species. None of these facts is claimed here as a discovery.

What is new is threefold. First, the organizing framework and its formalization: the explicit two grammars, the four-dimensional arrangement objects (shells, anchors, motors, loops), the residual-stress selection principle, and the treatment of fixedness as relative to a scope (Section 2.6, to come). Second, the falsifiable two-layer decomposition: for each output we state which part is written in the sequence and where the remainder comes from, and we report the measured split. Third, a reproducible, multi-organism set of analyses and the results they produce — the sequence-to-chromatin-phase results (nucleosome packing and the compartment-scale phase prediction), the importance-to-fixedness results and the exponential conservation law, the deep-time core-conservation result, and the runtime (quantity) results — each of which ships with code. Some of these analyses produce findings not present in the original single-organism study, for example the exponential form of the conservation law across four independent constraint axes and the predicted negative result that compartment boundaries are not placed by sequence.

What this paper claims — and does not

The claims are concrete and testable.

  1. Position — which physical phase a region prefers, where its structural elements sit, and how locally it is shaped — is strongly encoded by the sequence (Layer 1), and we report the measured strength on real data.

  2. Quantity — how much, how often, how fast — is dominated by the cellular runtime (Layer 2); sequence alone explains little of it until runtime measurements are added.

  3. The mapping from a sequence to its structural arrangement can be made a deterministic, auditable pipeline that, for any input region, either produces the arrangement and an event record or returns an explicit, labeled failure.

  4. What is fixed and what varies is not absolute but relative to a chosen reference population (a “scope”); the scope at which a value begins to vary identifies the biological unit it distinguishes (individual, species, or life itself).

We state the boundaries once, as facts rather than as ritual hedging. The paper does not claim to predict whole-organism phenotype from sequence; that requires the runtime and developmental information of Layer 2, which we do not model in full. The residual-stress measure (Section 3.4) ranks candidate structures under a fixed parameter set; it is a selection criterion, not a measured free energy, and a structure shown to be lowest among an enumerated set of alternatives is not thereby proven to be the global optimum over all conceivable configurations. Finally, we do not assert that the event-to-event variation seen at the single-molecule level is reducible; whether it reflects unmeasured higher-scope state or something irreducible is left open, because the data does not settle it. Leaving this open is a statement about the evidence, not a posture.

Reproducibility and how each result can be checked

Because the point of this paper is that DNA interpretation can be done, not merely described, every quantitative result carries a tag stating how a reader can reproduce it. We use three tiers.

Analyses we run from public sequence data using code shipped in the reproducibility bundle: the E. coli openability and stiffness results, the human chromosome-21 variation counts, the multi-genome composition and dinucleotide signatures, the TP53 translation and positional-precision demonstrations, the small-subunit ribosomal phylogeny, and the B-form helix coordinates. A reader can regenerate every number in these from the inputs.

Yeast results whose source data are publicly downloadable from code repositories: nucleosome-position classification, replication-origin (recognition-pattern) classification, and the genome-wide limiting-resource model. These can be re-run from their public sources.

Yeast quantitative results whose source data sit in archives not used for the present runs (the messenger-loading and replication-timing measurements). These are reported with full citation to their original sources and marked as carried over; supplying those datasets promotes them to Tier A.

The whole pipeline is governed by a simple contract we call Lock–Derive–Gate. Lock freezes the assumptions, parameters, and algorithm versions for a run. Derive produces all outputs from the locked inputs by fixed rules, with no hidden manual steps. Gate checks that each output is admissible (for example, that an irreversible step releases rather than absorbs energy, and that the residual-stress measure does not increase during relaxation) and records a verdict of pass, fail, or inconclusive. The core pipeline — the mapping from sequence to the A4 arrangement — is given in full inside this paper (Section 6); the complete script set, input hashes, and gate tables are in the separate bundle.

Conceptual framework

This section fixes the vocabulary used throughout the paper. It is offered as a proposed way of organizing the phenomena, to be tested against the data of Part III; where a statement is framework (a choice of how to carve things up) rather than a claim about data, we say so. Every term is defined where it first appears, and the reader is not expected to bring prior familiarity with any of them.

Two axes: what exists, versus what it means

The most common cause of confusion in the question “what does this piece of DNA do?” is that two different questions are run together. Separating them removes most of the ambiguity.

Axis 1 — the structural and functional inventory answers “what exists here?” It is a parts list. There are four kinds of part, and we define each from scratch.

  • Shell (the structural casing). A shell is a stretch of DNA described by how stiff or how easily opened it is. DNA is not uniformly rigid: some segments bend and pull apart at a low energy cost, others stay straight and stay shut. The reason is physical and simple. An A–T base pair is held together by two hydrogen bonds; a G–C pair by three. A region rich in A and T — especially one containing long runs of A or of T — is therefore less tightly held: it is more flexible and it opens more readily. A region rich in G and C is more stable and stiffer. A soft, AT-rich shell is where the helix can breathe, bend, or be pried open; a stiff, GC-rich shell resists. We make “soft” and “stiff” quantitative in Section 3.1; for now a shell is simply a contiguous segment with a characteristic stiffness.

  • Anchor (a fixed site). An anchor is a fixed reference point along the DNA. Two kinds matter: the boundary between one shell and the next (where the stiffness changes sharply), and the position of a recognized short pattern (a handle that a specific protein grips). Anchors are the nails on which the rest of the structure hangs; their defining feature is that the sequence fixes where they are.

  • Motor (an active machine). A motor is the site of an active molecular machine — for example a transcription start site, where the machinery that reads a gene docks and begins to move. We use “motor” for the location and action of such a machine. The machines themselves (the polymerases and the like) are universal hardware, essentially identical in every cell.

  • Loop (a three-dimensional contact). A loop is a contact in space: two points that lie far apart along the one-dimensional sequence are brought close together in three dimensions, typically by tethering a motor to an anchor. Loops are how a linear text folds into a working three-dimensional object.

The two grammars

Section 1.1 promised that the rules written in the sequence can be stated. There are, we argue, essentially two of them, and almost everything else commonly called a “rule” turns out to be one of these two applied to a different local value.

G1 — DNA physics. The double helix is a physical object, and its local behavior follows from the bases it is made of. The bases stack on one another like coins in a roll; the backbone carries negative charge; the two strands are zipped by hydrogen bonds. From these facts follow the local shape of the helix, how stiff it is, how it curves and so folds from a one-dimensional string into a three-dimensional path, how readily it melts (its two strands separate), and when it flips into alternative (“non-B”) forms under the twisting stress of supercoiling or under particular ionic conditions. The single most useful fact in G1 is the one already introduced: because an A–T pair has two hydrogen bonds and a G–C pair has three, AT-rich DNA is less stable, more flexible, and more easily opened than GC-rich DNA. This one statement compresses an enormous number of individual observations, which is exactly what qualifies it as grammar.

G2 — molecular recognition. Certain short sequence patterns are handles: a specific protein grips them and not the surrounding DNA. The pattern that marks where DNA copying begins, the sites that transcription factors recognize, and the signals that help position packaging proteins are all of this kind. G2 is the rule that maps a sequence (and the shape it implies) to the propensity for a particular protein to bind there.

A discipline: “understood” is not the same as “grammar.” Having measured or understood a quantity does not make it grammar. We reserve the word grammar for a predictive rule that compresses many instances into a single statement. If we merely know a value, it is a parameter (whose upstream cause may or may not be known); if it is a physical part (a helix, a loop, a positioned nucleosome), it is substrate or output. The test is mechanical and is the same test used throughout Part III: regress the candidate quantity on the sequence (and on the rules already known). The part that is predicted is grammar; the part that is not is a genuine parameter, on which one recurses to find its upstream cause; the part that differs from event to event under identical conditions is per-event variation, whose reducibility we leave open. Applied honestly, this test collapses a long list of supposed “rules” — positioning preferences, the width of A-tracts, origin anchors, non-B propensity, local shape — into instances of G1, with recognition folded into G2, acting on different cis values. The conclusion is that there are about two grammars; the rest are parameters and outputs.

Four classes of variability

A parameter can be classified by what it varies with. Two yes/no questions — does it change with the genetics (the sequence)? does it change with the environment (the cell’s state)? — give a two-by-two scheme with four classes, shown in Table 2.

Detection difficulty rises down the table: a fixed value is trivial to read off; a cis value can be found by regression on the sequence; an environmental value requires an assay of the cell’s state; and a value that depends on the interaction of genetics and environment is the hardest of all to pin down. There is a key asymmetry here, which Part III demonstrates with measurements: a fixed parameter is easy to measure, but precisely because it is constant it explains none of the variation between sites. Predictive power comes not from the fixed baseline but from local deviations away from it. The tension is central to the whole enterprise — the easy parameters carry no information, while the information-rich ones (those that also vary with the environment) are the hard ones to find.

Shared parameters, and pleiotropy

Parameters are often shared: a single value is read, at the same time, by many different processes. The concentration of calcium ions, for instance, is used at once by processes as different as egg formation, bone deposition, signaling, and muscle contraction. It is not that there is one calcium value “for” one tissue and a different one “for” another; a common number is read in many places. There is no central processor that gathers all the values and decides what happens. Each local process reads the shared values it needs, and the outcomes emerge — a dataflow or reaction network, not a single controller. (“Pleiotropy” is the standard word for one cause having many effects; a shared parameter is its mechanism.)

This has a sharp consequence for detection. A parameter dedicated to one output is easy to find by studying that output. But a shared, global, or upstream parameter is hard to find in any single output, because its effect there is distributed, indirect, and redundant with the proximal values it has already influenced. Such parameters are often global variables of the cell (ions, metabolites, master regulators, limited resources) rather than local sites. Part III shows a concrete case: a shared activator that makes replication origins markedly more efficient — a real, large biological effect — yet adds almost nothing to a prediction once the proximal parameters it acts through are already included.

The scope of a parameter

Parameters also differ in their range of action — their scope — and much of the part of an output that the local sequence fails to explain consists of larger-scope parameters that are invisible at the local level. Figure 3 arranges the levels.

A local parameter acts at one site — a single recognized pattern, say. A regional one acts over tens of kilobases, like a domain whose copying is scheduled together. A global parameter is one number shared by the whole cell — the total amount of a limiting resource, for example — and because it is the same for every site in a given condition, it is invisible to a regression that compares sites within that condition: it contributes a constant, and a constant has no explanatory power across sites. Such a global value reveals itself only in one of two ways: by fitting a model to the whole genome at once (so that the single shared number becomes a fitted parameter), or by comparing across conditions or individuals. An individual-scope quantity, like a person’s height, is a sum over thousands of sites plus global signals, and is likewise invisible from any single site.

The same ladder can be read along time rather than space, and the two readings coincide. The scope of a value is equivalently the slowest timescale on which it stays invariant: a local, individual-level value changes on fast (ecological, expression, generational) timescales, while a value shared across a whole clade changes only on slow (phylogenetic) ones. Reading scope as an invariance timescale is what lets the same axis carry both “where a parameter acts” and “how deeply fixed it is,” and it is the axis on which identity (deep, slow) separates from adaptation (shallow, fast). Section 2.6 turns this into the fixedness claim, and Part II measures the fixedness directly.

Fixedness is relative to a scope

“Fixed” and “variable” are not absolute properties. They are relative to a chosen reference population — a scope — and this is the hinge of the whole framework: the scope at which a value begins to vary identifies the biological unit that the value distinguishes. Three levels make the point.

  • Fixed across all of life. Base pairing, the genetic code, the chemistry of the helix, the core machinery — these are the same in every living thing. They therefore define life itself. Breaking one is lethal (the largest possible consequence), and because all organisms share them, they do not distinguish one species from another.

  • Fixed within a species, variable between species. A value that is constant in every healthy member of one species but differs between species distinguishes species or clades. The spacing of packaging proteins along the DNA is a clean example: it is essentially constant within one organism but differs across organisms.

  • Variable within a species. A value that differs among the members of one species distinguishes individuals: the copy number of repeated genes, single-base differences, larger structural differences, expression levels.

It follows that “how catastrophic it is to break a value” scales with the depth of its fixedness: breaking a universally fixed value is lethal; changing a species-fixed value tends toward inviability or toward the boundary between species; varying an individual-level value is simply normal diversity. This single idea binds together the four variability classes (Section 2.3), the scope axis (Section 2.5), and the familiar observation that the more important a piece of the genome is to function, the more strongly it is conserved. Part III measures examples at each level.

Part II makes this scaling quantitative rather than merely ordinal. Across four independent, non-circular measures of functional importance — codon position, functional category, pangenome presence, and codon-usage bias — conservation rises monotonically with importance, and the relationship takes a specific functional form: substitution falls exponentially in a constraint proxy, pN/pS≈exp(a+bENC)\mathrm{pN}/\mathrm{pS}\approx \exp(a + b\,\mathrm{ENC}) with a strongly linear log–constraint relation at the aggregate level (Section 4). We flag the status carefully: the exponential form is supported on two independent constraint axes within a species; the stronger claim that a single decay constant governs all scales (Rule 5) remains a hypothesis pending external data. The qualitative statement of this subsection — the more important a piece of the genome, the more deeply fixed — is the ordinal shadow of that law.

The formal objects

Section 2 gave the vocabulary as intuition. This section makes the four objects precise and computable, defines the stiffness signal that turns a sequence into shells, states the dynamics by which an arrangement settles into a structure, and gives the principle that selects which structure forms. The definitions are written so that the core pipeline of Part II is a direct transcription of them; nothing here is left informal.

The A4 arrangement

The pipeline operates on a region, and produces an A4 arrangement from it.

Definition 1 (Region). A region is a triple ℛ=(S,𝒜,θ)\mathcal{R}=(S,\mathcal{A},\theta), where SS is a DNA sequence of length LL over the alphabet {𝙰,𝙲,𝙶,𝚃,𝙽}\{\texttt{A},\texttt{C},\texttt{G},\texttt{T},\texttt{N}\} (the symbol N marks a base that was not determined), 𝒜\mathcal{A} is an optional set of annotations restricted to the region (for example, transcript coordinates), and θ\theta is a parameter dictionary fixing the window length WW, the step Δ\Delta, algorithm versions, and any random seed. The pipeline is a function of ℛ\mathcal{R} alone: same inputs, same output.

Definition 2 (A4 arrangement). The A4 arrangement of a region is the tuple A4=(shells,anchors,motors,loops,meta)\mathrm{A4}=(\text{shells},\text{anchors},\text{motors},\text{loops},\text{meta}), where meta records the versions and provenance needed to reproduce it. When no annotation is supplied, motors and loops are empty; this is permitted, but it changes what can be claimed (there is then no transcript-driven structure).

The four components are the precise versions of the Axis-1 parts of Section 2.1.

Definition 3 (Stiffness shell). A shell is a maximal contiguous run of windows that share a coarse stiffness label (defined in Section 3.2). Each shell stores its coordinate interval [s,e)[s,e) and the mean of its normalized stiffness signal, z‾\bar z.

Definition 4 (Anchor). An anchor is a tuple (id,p,k,α)(\mathrm{id},p,k,\alpha): a position pp in base pairs, a kind k∈{region edge,shell boundary,shell core}k\in\{\text{region edge},\ \text{shell boundary},\ \text{shell core}\}, and a non-negative strength α\alpha that, for a boundary, equals the size of the stiffness discontinuity there. Positions lie in [0,L][0,L] and identifiers are unique within a run.

Definition 5 (Motor and loop). A motor is a tuple (id,p,strand)(\mathrm{id},p,\text{strand}) giving the position of a transcript-driven feature — a transcription start — in local coordinates. A loop is a tuple (id,motor_id,anchor_id,d)(\mathrm{id},\text{motor\_id},\text{anchor\_id},d) pairing a motor with an anchor at sequence distance d=|pmotor−panchor|d=|p_{\text{motor}}-p_{\text{anchor}}|. Loops are generated by joining each motor to its nearest kk anchors under a fixed rule, so the loop set is determined by the region and θ\theta.

The stiffness signal: turning a sequence into shells

This is the step that delivers the “soft” and “stiff” of Section 2.1 as numbers. The pipeline slides a window of length WW along the sequence and, for each window, computes three quantities.

Definition 6 (Stiffness signal). For each window the pipeline computes the GC fraction GC\mathrm{GC} (the share of bases that are G or C), the CpG density CpG\mathrm{CpG} (the frequency of adjacent CG pairs), and the AT-tract density AT6\mathrm{AT}_6 (the frequency of runs of at least six As or six Ts). The stiffness signal is the fixed linear combination stiff=wGCGC+wCpGCpG+wATAT6,\mathrm{stiff} \;=\; w_{\mathrm{GC}}\,\mathrm{GC} \;+\; w_{\mathrm{CpG}}\,\mathrm{CpG} \;+\; w_{\mathrm{AT}}\,\mathrm{AT}_6, with weights (wGC,wCpG,wAT)(w_{\mathrm{GC}},w_{\mathrm{CpG}},w_{\mathrm{AT}}) fixed in the Lock.

Each ingredient has a physical reading, and we state it in the phase language of Section 4 rather than as a bending rigidity. The GC fraction tracks the base-pairing energy through the three-versus-two hydrogen-bond fact of Section 2.2, and thereby a region’s propensity to demix into a firmer-bounded phase (a higher interfacial-tension propensity γ\gamma). The CpG density singles out a dinucleotide that marks regulatory regions in many genomes, sharpening the signal where regulation lives. The AT-tract density captures something the GC fraction alone misses: long runs of A or T have a distinctive local structure that tends to exclude packaging proteins, marking regions whose phase behavior differs from their GC content alone. We read the resulting shell labels as phase-propensity labels — which coexisting phase a region tends to join (Definition 10) — not as a ranking of how rigid or how compact the helix is. We state plainly that the weights are a modeling choice recorded in the Lock, and that the signal is a deterministic, auditable summary of the sequence — not a complete physical model of chromatin mechanics. Its job is to produce a reproducible segmentation, and Part III tests how well that segmentation lines up with biology.

The raw signal is then put on a common scale and smoothed. Rather than a mean and standard deviation, which a few extreme windows would distort, the pipeline uses a robust standardization built from the median mm and the median absolute deviation d=median(|xi−m|)d=\mathrm{median}(|x_i-m|) (the typical distance of a value from the median): zi=xi−m1.4826max(d,ϵ),z_i \;=\; \frac{x_i-m}{1.4826\,\max(d,\epsilon)}, with a small ϵ\epsilon to avoid division by zero (the factor 1.48261.4826 makes the scale agree with a standard deviation for ordinary data). A short moving average then removes single-window noise without introducing long-range dependence.

Finally the smoothed signal is cut into shells. Let q1q_1 and q2q_2 be its 33rd and 66th percentiles; each window gets a label of 00 (soft), 11 (intermediate), or 22 (stiff) according to which tercile it falls in, and a shell is a maximal run of windows with the same label. Very short shells are merged into the neighbor whose mean is closer, to avoid unstable fragments. At each junction between two shells the boundary strength is the size of the jump, σ=|z‾b−z‾a|\sigma=|\bar z_b-\bar z_a|, and an anchor is placed there with strength σ\sigma. How an arrangement settles: the four-activity dynamics

The shells, anchors, motors, and loops describe what the sequence offers. To turn that offer into a settled structure, the framework uses a fixed grammar of four activities, and no others; any variety in behavior must come from the arrangement, not from inventing new kinds of activity.

  • INIT sets up the starting state from the A4 arrangement.

  • SCONSERV is a conservative step: it projects the current state onto the set of configurations that satisfy the hard constraints (no two parts occupying the same space, no buried charges, the stiffness ordering between rigid and flexible elements). It conserves energy and is, in principle, reversible.

  • SDISSIP is a dissipative step: it relaxes the configuration by descending on the residual-stress measure of Section 3.4, shedding strain. It increases entropy and is not reversible. In the condensate reading of Section 4, SDISSIP is demixing and coarsening: at the runtime-set fill fraction ϕ\phi, the region separates into phases and its interfaces relax toward the partition of least interfacial energy, so the Lyapunov property below is the statement that a phase-separation free energy is being minimized.

  • JEVENT is an irreversible discrete event — a cleavage, a bond formation, a topological locking — applied when a local energy barrier is crossed. It advances time and writes a row to an event ledger.

This is the coarse-grained form of a standard split in non-equilibrium physics into a conservative part (which preserves energy) and a dissipative part (which produces entropy), with the irreversible events treated as discrete jumps. One further ingredient is worth naming because it explains handedness: an interaction built from a triple product of orientations changes sign under reflection, and so it is what gives a structure a fixed chirality or a direction of rotation — the right-handedness of a fibrous helix, or the single turning direction of a rotary motor. The relaxation has a guarantee attached: the residual-stress measure behaves like a Lyapunov function — a quantity that does not increase as the system relaxes — so the dynamics settles rather than wanders. The admissibility gates of the Lock–Derive–Gate contract enforce exactly these properties: every JEVENT must release energy (ΔE<0\Delta E<0) rather than absorb it, and the residual stress must not rise during a relaxation phase. A run that violates a gate is flagged, not quietly accepted.

The selection principle: minimum residual stress

We can now answer the “why this structure” question of Section 1.3. Among the configurations a region admits, the one realized is the one that settles to the lowest residual stress while satisfying every hard constraint.

Definition 7 (Residual stress). The residual stress of a configuration Ψ\Psi is the dimensionless sum Pres(Ψ)=∑kλkδk(Ψ)+∑ℓμℓσℓ(Ψ),P_{\mathrm{res}}(\Psi) \;=\; \sum_k \lambda_k\,\delta_k(\Psi) \;+\; \sum_\ell \mu_\ell\,\sigma_\ell(\Psi), where each δk\delta_k measures a violation of a hard constraint (a steric clash, a buried charge), each σℓ\sigma_\ell measures a softer strain (excess bending), and the non-negative weights obey a fixed hierarchy λk≫μℓ\lambda_k \gg \mu_\ell recorded in the Lock. Small PresP_{\mathrm{res}} means the configuration nearly satisfies its constraints; large PresP_{\mathrm{res}} means it is strongly frustrated.

In practice the candidate configurations are not an unstructured continuum but a finite, explicitly listed set — the topologically distinct, geometrically feasible arrangements the region admits — and the pipeline relaxes each, records its settled PresP_{\mathrm{res}}, and selects the minimum among those that satisfy all hard constraints. Two honesty points belong here, and we state each once. First, what matters is the ordering of candidates, not the absolute numbers; the scale of PresP_{\mathrm{res}} depends on the weights, while the ranking does not. Second, a configuration shown to be lowest within an enumerated set is not thereby proven to be the global optimum over all conceivable configurations; the strength of the claim is the strength of the candidate set. PresP_{\mathrm{res}} is a selection criterion, not a measured free energy.

There is a clean geometric reading of why a configuration is frustrated, and it is the law of the fill knob ϕ\phi of Section 4.3. Picture a condensate — the core of a folded chain, the interior of a chromatin domain — that holds NN units of effective size veffv_{\mathrm{eff}} at fill fraction ϕ\phi. The configurations that sit at low residual stress are those that nearly satisfy the capacity identity Nveff≈ϕVcore,N\,v_{\mathrm{eff}} \;\approx\; \phi\,V_{\mathrm{core}}, without overshooting it. Overfill it (Nveff>ϕVcoreN\,v_{\mathrm{eff}}>\phi\,V_{\mathrm{core}}) and the interior clashes; underfill it and the domain is dilute and its interface cannot hold a stable shape. Both deviations raise PresP_{\mathrm{res}} (Figure 5), for the same reason a droplet has a preferred size at a given amount of material. Here γ\gamma (set by Layer 1) fixes how costly a misshapen interface is, and ϕ\phi (set by Layer 2) fixes how full the interior is; the capacity identity is where the two knobs meet. The same reading applies to any container the framework treats this way — the core of a folded chain, the cross-section of a fibre, the circumference of a ring: each needs the right amount of empty space, neither crammed nor hollow, a point that recurs throughout the case studies.

Position is a three-dimensional coordinate

A final point closes the gap between “where in the sequence” and “where in space.” The B-form double helix has a fixed local geometry: each base pair is raised by a rise of h≈3.4h\approx 3.4 Å along the axis and rotated by a twist of φ≈34.29∘\varphi\approx 34.29^\circ relative to the one before, so that the helix completes one full turn every 10.510.5 base pairs, and the bases sit at a radius ρ≈10\rho\approx 10 Å from the axis. The position of base pair nn is therefore literally a point in space, 𝐫(n)=(ρcosnφ,ρsinnφ,nh).\mathbf{r}(n) \;=\; \big(\rho\cos n\varphi,\ \rho\sin n\varphi,\ n\,h\big). The one-dimensional order of the sequence is a three-dimensional coordinate on the helix. As a worked instance, the first base pair sits near (8.3,5.6,3.4)(8.3,\,5.6,\,3.4) Å and the twenty-first near (10.0,0,71.4)(10.0,\,0,\,71.4) Å— the same angular position as the first, because twenty-one base pairs make almost exactly two turns, but raised by 71.471.4 Å along the axis (Figure 6). We are precise about the scope of this statement: it is the local, intrinsic coordinate of the helix itself. The higher-order folding — the loops of Section 3.1, and the large-scale arrangement of the genome in the nucleus — is a separate matter, and it is exactly where measurements of three-dimensional contact (which we do not have here) would enter.

The stiffness shell, reframed: a viscoelastic condensate interface

This section revises the physical reading of the stiffness signal of Section 3.2. The computational object is unchanged: the pipeline still slides a window along the sequence, forms the deterministic signal from GC\mathrm{GC}, CpG\mathrm{CpG}, and AT6\mathrm{AT}_6, standardizes it, and cuts it into shells at the tercile thresholds (Section 3.2). That segmentation remains the operational tool, and nothing downstream of it needs to be recomputed. What changes is what a shell means. In v2 a shell was read as a stretch of helix that is intrinsically stiff or intrinsically bendable, and “soft” was used interchangeably with “easy to open” and “easy to bend.” That reading conflates three different things and, as the compartment-scale data will show (Section 2), it has the sign of the effect backwards at large scales. We replace it with a single, sharper picture: a shell is a region’s propensity to occupy a particular physical phase, and a shell boundary is the interface of a phase-separated domain. The rest of this section makes that statement precise and states plainly where it is on firm ground and where it is a modeling commitment to be tested.

What “firmness” is, and is not

The word “stiffness” invites a mechanical-engineering reading — a beam’s resistance to bending, the persistence length of the double helix — and that reading is the wrong one for what the framework needs. The firmness at issue is not the bending rigidity of a rod; it is the firmness of a surface, the way a droplet’s skin resists a quick poke yet gives way to a slow steady push.

Definition 8 (Firmness of a shell). The firmness of a shell is its interfacial and viscoelastic character, not a bending modulus. It has two parts that act on different timescales. On fast timescales the shell resists deformation elastically, and the strength of that resistance is set by an interfacial tension γ\gamma — the energy cost per unit area of the boundary between the shell’s phase and its surroundings. On slow timescales the shell flows viscously and yields. Concretely: strike it and it holds; push it and it moves.

Remark 1. This is exactly why a single scalar “stiff/soft” label was ambiguous. It tried to summarize in one number a response that is genuinely two-faced: an elastic (fast) response governed by γ\gamma and a flow (slow) response governed by viscosity. A material can be firm to a strike and soft to a push at the same time; the two are not in contradiction, they are the two regimes of one viscoelastic interface. The stiffness signal of Section 3.2 is best understood not as “how rigid the helix is” but as “which phase this region prefers, and how firm the resulting interface tends to be.”

The physical home: chromatin as a viscoelastic condensate

The object that has precisely these two properties — an interfacial tension and a frequency-dependent mechanical response — is a biomolecular condensate formed by liquid–liquid phase separation (LLPS). This is not an analogy imported for convenience; it is the now-standard physical description of how chromatin and its associated proteins organize. Heterochromatin domains form by phase separation of HP1 proteins, which demix into droplets with a measurable surface tension (Strom et al., Nature, 2017; Larson et al., Nature, 2017). Nucleoli behave as multiphase liquid droplets (Brangwynne et al., 2011; Feric et al., Cell, 2016), and chromatin itself shows intrinsic and regulated phase-separation behavior (Gibson et al., Cell, 2019); the broader principle of condensates as organizers of cellular biochemistry is reviewed by Banani et al. (Nat. Rev. Mol. Cell Biol., 2017).

Two measured features of these condensates are the two parts of Definition 8. First, they have an interfacial tension γ\gamma: a droplet rounds up, fuses with neighbors, and resists having its surface stretched, all of which are surface-tension behaviors. Second, micro-rheology of chromatin reports frequency-dependent storage and loss moduli G′(ω)G'(\omega) and G″(ω)G''(\omega) — the signature of a viscoelastic, Kelvin–Voigt-like material that is elastic at high frequency and viscous at low frequency. The long-running “is chromatin a liquid or a solid?” debate is, on this reading, not a contradiction to be resolved but the rate- and scale-dependence itself: probed quickly or on small scales it looks solid, probed slowly or on large scales it flows. That is the macroscopic statement of “strike it and it holds, push it and it moves.”

Remark 2 (The molecular-scale cousin). The same “firm boundary” logic appears one level down, inside the double helix, as the cooperativity of the helix–coil (melting) transition. A melted bubble does not open base by base independently; opening is cooperative because creating a helix–coil junction costs a boundary free energy (the Poland–Scheraga picture). That junction free energy is the one-dimensional analogue of the interfacial tension γ\gamma: in both cases the system pays to make a boundary, and that payment is what makes the boundary firm. The stiffness shell at the chromatin scale and the cooperative melting unit at the base-pair scale are the same kind of object — a phase boundary with a tension — at two scales.

Two knobs: composition sets the tension, runtime sets the fill

A condensate carries exactly the two quantities the framework has emphasized from the start: how much material is packed into it — a fill fraction ϕ\phi — and how firm its boundary is — an interfacial tension γ\gamma. The fill fraction is already in the paper: it is the ϕ\phi of the capacity identity of Section 3.4, Nveff≈ϕVcoreN\,v_{\mathrm{eff}}\approx\phi\,V_{\mathrm{core}}. The interfacial tension is what the stiffness signal has been tracking all along. This gives the two-layer proposition a clean physical statement.

Definition 9 (The two knobs). The two layers act on two separate properties of a condensate.

  • Layer 1 (the sequence composition; the stiffness signal of Section 3.2) sets the interfacial-tension propensity γ\gamma — which phase a region prefers, and how firm the resulting interface tends to be.

  • Layer 2 (the cellular runtime) sets the fill fraction ϕ\phi — how much is actually packed into the region, the quantity.

Position (grammar) is read here as the γ\gamma-knob; quantity (runtime) as the ϕ\phi-knob.

Settling is phase separation

With the two knobs named, the four-activity dynamics of Section 3.3 and the selection principle of Section 3.4 acquire a concrete physical meaning. The dissipative relaxation SDISSIP, which descends on the residual stress PresP_{\mathrm{res}}, is demixing and coarsening: at the runtime-set fill fraction ϕ\phi, the region separates into phases and the boundaries relax toward the partition of least interfacial energy. The Lyapunov property of PresP_{\mathrm{res}} (it does not increase during relaxation, Section 3.3) is the statement that this is a free energy being minimized, which is what phase separation is. The settled shell is the resulting condensate; the anchor at a shell boundary is its interface.

The capacity identity reads naturally in this language. Overfilling a condensate (Nveff>ϕVcoreN\,v_{\mathrm{eff}}>\phi\,V_{\mathrm{core}}) crowds its interior and forces clashes; underfilling leaves it dilute and the interface cannot hold a stable shape; only the balanced fill sits at low interfacial and elastic energy. Both deviations raise PresP_{\mathrm{res}} for the same reason a droplet has a preferred size at a given amount of material.

Remark 3 (An honesty point, stated once). The pipeline implements the segmentation half of this picture — composition →\to signal →\to shells (Section 3.2) — and does not implement the settling dynamics as a phase-separation simulation. SDISSIP, the PresP_{\mathrm{res}} descent, and the coarsening of interfaces are specified as the intended physics, not run as code. We therefore do not claim to have computed settled condensates from sequence. What we test instead, and directly, is the outcome: whether a region’s sequence predicts which phase it occupies in measured chromatin (Section 2). Predicting the outcome is the falsifiable content; simulating the trajectory is future work.

Stiffness is phase, not compactness

The reframing earns its keep by dissolving a contradiction that the v2 reading could not. At the nucleosome scale, GC-rich sequence is associated with more occupancy and tighter local packing; at the compartment scale, GC-rich sequence marks the open, active A compartment (Section 2). The sign of “GC-rich →\to more compact” flips between the two scales. Under the v2 reading — stiffness as a monotone stand-in for compactness or for ease of opening — this is a straight contradiction.

It is not a contradiction once the signal is read as phase propensity. The composition sets which phase a region prefers; whether that phase happens to be dense or sparse is a property of the level’s physics, not of the signal. A high-γ\gamma region is one that demixes firmly into its own phase; that phase can be a compact nucleosome array at one scale and an open, gene-rich compartment at another.

Definition 10 (Reading rule for the stiffness signal). The stiffness signal is a map from sequence to phase identity (which coexisting phase a region tends to join), not a map from sequence to density (how tightly it is packed). Treat a high signal as “prefers a firmly demixed phase,” never as “more compact” simpliciter.

What the sequence fixes at an interface — and what it does not

A phase picture makes a prediction that a stiffness-as-property picture does not, and the prediction is partly negative. If a region’s sequence sets only its phase propensity, then sequence should determine which phase a region is in, but the location of the boundary between two phases need not be written in the sequence at all: an interface position is an emergent, collective outcome of the competing phases under the runtime conditions, the same way the surface of a settling emulsion is not encoded in any one molecule. This forces a distinction the framework did not previously draw.

Definition 11 (Element-position versus boundary-position). “Position,” the grammar half of the two-layer proposition, splits into two kinds. Element-position — where a gene, a binding site, or a structural feature sits — is written in the sequence (grammar; strongly encoded). Boundary-position — where the interface between two phases or domains falls — is emergent: it is set by the collective phase physics at the runtime-determined fill, not fixed by the local letters.

This is a testable, and falsifiable, consequence. It predicts that the identity of a region’s phase will be strongly sequence-predictable while the sharp location of a domain boundary will not sit at any sharp sequence feature. Part II reports exactly this pattern at the compartment scale: sequence composition predicts which compartment a region occupies with high accuracy, yet compartment boundaries do not coincide with sharp compositional transitions (Section 2, including the boundary-localization null result). The negative half is not a failure of the framework; it is the phase picture being right where the property picture would have been wrong.

The core pipeline

This section keeps the promise of Section 1.6: the mapping from a region to its A4 arrangement is given here in full, as runnable code, so that interpretation can be done and checked rather than only described. The module below is a direct transcription of Section 3; the bulky analysis scripts that produce the results of Part III live in the separate bundle, but this core — the part everything else depends on — is in the paper.

What it does. Given a sequence (and, optionally, a GFF annotation for one identifier), the pipeline computes the windowed observables and the stiffness signal of Section 3.2, standardizes and smooths it, cuts it into shells, places anchors at the shell boundaries with a strength equal to the size of the stiffness jump, reads transcription starts from the annotation as motors, joins each motor to its nearest anchors as loops, and finally runs the admissibility gates. It depends only on numpy and the standard library, and it is deterministic: the same region and the same Lock give the same A4 arrangement.

The Lock. The parameters are fixed at the top of the module in a dictionary named LOCK: the window length and step (W=2000W=2000, step=500\mathrm{step}=500 base pairs), the stiffness-signal weights (wGC=1.0w_{\mathrm{GC}}=1.0, wCpG=0.5w_{\mathrm{CpG}}=0.5, wAT=−0.5w_{\mathrm{AT}}=-0.5; the negative AT-tract weight encodes that poly-A/poly-T tracts mark locally open, nucleosome-disfavoring DNA), the smoothing half-width, the minimum shell length, and the number of nearest anchors per motor. Changing any value defines a new version, never a silent edit of an old result.

The gates. Four invariants are checked on every run and reported as pass/fail: the shells cover the whole region, the shells are contiguous (no gaps or overlaps), every anchor lies within the region, and anchor identifiers are unique. A failed gate is surfaced, not hidden.

import numpy as np

# ---- LOCK: fixed parameters. Changing any of these defines a new version. ----
LOCK = dict(
    W=2000, step=500,                 # window length and step, in base pairs
    w_gc=1.0, w_cpg=0.5, w_at=-0.5,   # stiffness-signal weights (Section 3.2)
    smooth_radius=2,                  # moving-average half-width, in windows
    min_shell_bp=5000,                # merge shells shorter than this
    loop_k=2,                         # number of nearest anchors per motor
    eps=1e-9,
)


def read_fasta(path):
    seqs, name, buf = {}, None, []
    with open(path) as fh:
        for line in fh:
            if line.startswith('>'):
                if name is not None:
                    seqs[name] = ''.join(buf)
                name, buf = line[1:].split()[0], []
            else:
                buf.append(line.strip().upper())
    if name is not None:
        seqs[name] = ''.join(buf)
    return seqs


def windowed_observables(seq, W, step):
    """Return (centers, GC, CpG, AT6) per window using cumulative sums."""
    a = np.frombuffer(seq.encode('ascii'), dtype=np.uint8)
    n = len(a)
    A, C, G, T = 65, 67, 71, 84
    is_a, is_c, is_g, is_t = (a == A), (a == C), (a == G), (a == T)
    is_acgt = is_a | is_c | is_g | is_t
    is_gc = is_g | is_c
    cg = np.zeros(n, dtype=bool)            # CpG: C at i, G at i+1
    cg[:-1] = is_c[:-1] & is_g[1:]
    at6 = np.zeros(n, dtype=bool)           # start of a run of >=6 A or >=6 T
    if n >= 6:
        ca = np.concatenate([[0], np.cumsum(is_a)])
        ct = np.concatenate([[0], np.cumsum(is_t)])
        run_a = (ca[6:] - ca[:-6]) == 6
        run_t = (ct[6:] - ct[:-6]) == 6
        at6[:len(run_a)] = run_a | run_t
    cum_gc = np.concatenate([[0], np.cumsum(is_gc)])
    cum_ac = np.concatenate([[0], np.cumsum(is_acgt)])
    cum_cg = np.concatenate([[0], np.cumsum(cg)])
    cum_a6 = np.concatenate([[0], np.cumsum(at6)])
    starts = np.arange(0, max(1, n - W + 1), step)
    ends = starts + W
    denom = cum_ac[ends] - cum_ac[starts]
    keep = denom > 0
    starts, ends, denom = starts[keep], ends[keep], denom[keep]
    gc = (cum_gc[ends] - cum_gc[starts]) / denom
    cpg = (cum_cg[ends] - cum_cg[starts]) / max(1, W - 1)
    a6f = (cum_a6[ends] - cum_a6[starts]) / max(1, W - 5)
    centers = starts + W // 2
    return centers, gc, cpg, a6f


def stiffness_signal(gc, cpg, at6, lock=LOCK):
    return lock['w_gc'] * gc + lock['w_cpg'] * cpg + lock['w_at'] * at6


def robust_z(x, eps):
    m = np.median(x)
    d = np.median(np.abs(x - m))
    return (x - m) / (1.4826 * max(d, eps))


def smooth(z, radius):
    if radius <= 0 or len(z) == 0:
        return z
    pad = np.pad(z, radius, mode='edge')
    ker = np.ones(2 * radius + 1) / (2 * radius + 1)
    return np.convolve(pad, ker, mode='valid')


def segment_shells(centers, z, L, min_shell_bp):
    """Tercile-label, run-length into contiguous shells, merge short ones."""
    n = len(z)
    if n == 0:
        return [[0, L, 1, 0.0]]
    q1, q2 = np.percentile(z, 33), np.percentile(z, 66)
    lab = np.where(z <= q1, 0, np.where(z <= q2, 1, 2))
    mids = [0] + [int((centers[i] + centers[i + 1]) // 2) for i in range(n - 1)] + [int(L)]
    shells, start = [], 0
    for i in range(1, n + 1):
        if i == n or lab[i] != lab[start]:
            shells.append([mids[start], mids[i], int(lab[start]),
                           float(np.mean(z[start:i]))])
            start = i
    changed = True
    while changed and len(shells) > 1:
        changed = False
        for i, sh in enumerate(shells):
            if sh[1] - sh[0] < min_shell_bp:
                if i > 0 and i < len(shells) - 1:
                    left, right = shells[i - 1], shells[i + 1]
                    tgt = i - 1 if abs(left[3] - sh[3]) <= abs(right[3] - sh[3]) else i + 1
                elif i > 0:
                    tgt = i - 1
                else:
                    tgt = i + 1
                ln_t = shells[tgt][1] - shells[tgt][0]
                ln_s = sh[1] - sh[0]
                mean = (shells[tgt][3] * ln_t + sh[3] * ln_s) / max(1, ln_t + ln_s)
                if tgt == i - 1:
                    shells[i - 1] = [shells[i - 1][0], sh[1], shells[i - 1][2], mean]
                else:
                    shells[i + 1] = [sh[0], shells[i + 1][1], shells[i + 1][2], mean]
                del shells[i]
                changed = True
                break
    for sh in shells:                       # relabel by final mean after merging
        sh[2] = 0 if sh[3] <= q1 else (1 if sh[3] <= q2 else 2)
    return shells


def build_anchors(shells, L):
    anchors = [dict(id=0, pos=0, kind='region_edge', strength=0.0)]
    aid = 1
    for i in range(1, len(shells)):
        strength = abs(shells[i][3] - shells[i - 1][3])
        anchors.append(dict(id=aid, pos=int(shells[i][0]),
                            kind='shell_boundary', strength=float(strength)))
        aid += 1
    anchors.append(dict(id=aid, pos=int(L), kind='region_edge', strength=0.0))
    return anchors


def read_gff_tss(path, seqid, L):
    motors, mid = [], 0
    with open(path) as fh:
        for line in fh:
            if line.startswith('#'):
                continue
            f = line.rstrip('\n').split('\t')
            if len(f) < 8 or f[0] != seqid or f[2] not in ('gene', 'mRNA', 'transcript'):
                continue
            start, end, strand = int(f[3]), int(f[4]), f[6]
            tss = start if strand == '+' else end
            if 0 <= tss <= L:
                motors.append(dict(id=mid, pos=int(tss), strand=strand))
                mid += 1
    return motors


def build_loops(motors, anchors, k):
    loops, lid = [], 0
    apos = np.array([a['pos'] for a in anchors])
    for m in motors:
        d = np.abs(apos - m['pos'])
        for j in np.argsort(d)[:k]:
            loops.append(dict(id=lid, motor_id=m['id'],
                              anchor_id=anchors[int(j)]['id'], d=int(d[int(j)])))
            lid += 1
    return loops


def gates(A4, L):
    sh, an = A4['shells'], A4['anchors']
    checks = [
        ('shells_cover_region', bool(sh) and sh[0][0] == 0 and sh[-1][1] == L),
        ('shells_contiguous', all(sh[i][1] == sh[i + 1][0] for i in range(len(sh) - 1))),
        ('anchors_in_range', all(0 <= a['pos'] <= L for a in an)),
        ('anchor_ids_unique', len({a['id'] for a in an}) == len(an)),
    ]
    return [(name, 'PASS' if ok else 'FAIL') for name, ok in checks]


def run_key(seq, gff=None, seqid=None, lock=LOCK):
    L = len(seq)
    centers, gc, cpg, at6 = windowed_observables(seq, lock['W'], lock['step'])
    z = smooth(robust_z(stiffness_signal(gc, cpg, at6, lock), lock['eps']),
               lock['smooth_radius'])
    shells = segment_shells(centers, z, L, lock['min_shell_bp'])
    motors = read_gff_tss(gff, seqid, L) if (gff and seqid) else []
    anchors = build_anchors(shells, L)
    loops = build_loops(motors, anchors, lock['loop_k']) if motors else []
    A4 = dict(length=L, shells=shells, anchors=anchors,
              motors=motors, loops=loops, lock=lock)
    A4['gates'] = gates(A4, L)
    return A4

How to interpret a region, step by step

This section is the practical heart of the paper. With the core pipeline of Section 6 in hand, a reader can take a region of their own and carry out an interpretation. The procedure has five steps, and we then work a real example all the way through, honestly, including a lesson about scale that the example itself forces.

The five steps

  1. Get a region. Obtain the sequence of the stretch of interest, and (if you have it) an annotation giving transcript coordinates. State the coordinates and the source, so the run is reproducible.

  2. Run the core pipeline. Call run_key on the region. Choose the window length to match the scale of the question — a whole chromosome is read well with a 22-kb window, a focused 100100-kb neighborhood with a 500500-bp window — and record the choice in the run’s Lock. The output is the A4 arrangement plus the gate verdicts.

  3. Read the four components. The shells are the regional stiffness/openability landscape; the anchors are the fixed boundaries (with a strength that says how sharp each one is); the motors are the transcription starts; the loops are the motor-to-anchor contacts.

  4. Split Layer 1 from Layer 2. Everything the A4 reports — where shells and boundaries sit, where motors are, which anchors a motor contacts — is Layer 1: structure and position fixed by the sequence. What the A4 does not report — how much is transcribed, how often an origin fires, when an event happens — is Layer 2, and requires runtime data the sequence cannot supply.

  5. State the interpretation, scoped honestly. Say what the sequence fixes, mark what would need runtime measurement, and match any fine-scale claim to a tool of the right resolution.

What the A4 tells you, and what it does not

In one sentence: the A4 arrangement is a complete, reproducible statement of the Layer-1 structure of a region — the stiffness landscape, the boundaries, the machine positions, and the contacts they imply — and it is deliberately silent about Layer-2 quantities, which it flags as the province of runtime data. A reader who internalizes this split will neither over-read a quantity into the sequence nor dismiss an unexplained quantity as noise; they will know exactly which question the sequence can answer and which it cannot.

A complete, self-contained interpretation that carries a real sequence through all of these steps at once — the A4 arrangement, the openability zoom, a translated gene, and the Layer-1/Layer-2 split, all from one short driver — is distributed with the reproducibility bundle (Appendix 5); the bundle’s scripts are the recommended starting point for a reader who wants to interpret a sequence of their own.

Analysis methods

Beyond the core pipeline, the paper reports a set of focused analyses, each of which isolates one facet of the framework. This section states the method for each; Part III reports the results. The conventions of Section 5 (composition over called bases, width-independent matching, in-fold motif learning, AT-matched negatives) are used throughout and not repeated.

Composition and openability (Tier A)

For any set of regions, GC and AT fractions are computed over called bases only. To test the framework’s prediction that functional sites which must open are AT-rich, two comparisons are made: coding versus intergenic-spacer AT within each genome, and the AT of a tight window at a landmark (the replication origin) against a background distribution of many random windows of the same size, reporting the deviation in standard deviations (σ\sigma). The same windowed signal underlies the shells of the core pipeline.

Dinucleotide signatures (Tier A)

Each genome is summarized by the frequencies of its sixteen ordered dinucleotides, a sixteen-dimensional vector. The distance between two genomes is the Euclidean distance between their vectors. Comparing within-species distances (between strains or chromosomes of one organism) to between-species distances tests whether composition is fixed at the species level.

Sequence variation (Tier A)

From population variant files (VCF), the number of polymorphic sites — positions that actually differ within the sample — is counted, both per population and per individual, and expressed as a fraction of the callable genome. The complementary quantity, the fraction identical across individuals, measures within-species fixedness. Sites that are invariant carry no individual-level information and are excluded from the polymorphism count by construction.

Translation and positional precision (Tier A)

A messenger RNA is read, its open reading frame located, and its codons translated with the standard genetic code to give the protein. Positional precision is then probed by three controlled perturbations: shifting the reading frame by one base, introducing a premature stop at a chosen codon, and changing a single base to test a missense substitution. The point is to show that the product is fixed by position, not by bulk composition: a one-base displacement collapses the product, while a different single change alters it precisely.

Deep-time ribosomal conservation (Tier A)

The small-subunit (18S) ribosomal RNA — the canonical universal phylogenetic marker — is recovered for several deeply diverged eukaryotes, aligned with FAMSA, and used two ways: a pairwise percent-identity across lineages, and a within-molecule, per-column conservation that separates the alignable functional core from the length-variable expansion segments (Section 5). We state the limit of this plainly: a single locus gives a noisy estimate of deep relationships, and sequence similarity is evidence about conservation of the core, not a per-gene hierarchy or a proof of descent on its own.

The helix coordinate (Tier A)

The three-dimensional coordinate of each base pair is computed from the B-form geometry of Section 3.5 (rise 3.43.4 Å, twist 34.29∘34.29^\circ, radius 1010 Å). This is a direct calculation, included to make concrete that sequence order is a spatial coordinate.

Yeast position, efficiency, and the global model (Tiers B and C)

The yeast analyses that motivated the framework use established methods, restated here for completeness. Position (nucleosome and replication-origin) is classified from sequence by logistic regression under cross-validation, reporting the area under the ROC curve; the recognition motif is learned within folds, and negatives are AT-matched so that composition alone cannot explain the result (Tier B). Efficiency of origin firing is regressed on sequence and then on sequence plus runtime measurements (the amount of helicase loaded, the replication timing, shared trans-factors) added one at a time, reporting the cross-validated R2R^2 at each step (Tier C for the runtime measurements). The residual above the efficiency ceiling is addressed by a whole-genome model whose single fitted parameter is the size of a limiting pool of firing factors (Tier B).

The reproducibility contract

Reproducibility here is a contract, not an aspiration. It has three parts — the discipline, the bundle, and the verification protocol — and it is what lets a reader trust, and check, every number in Part III.

The discipline: Lock–Derive–Gate. As stated in Section 1.6, every run Locks its assumptions, parameters, and code versions; Derives all outputs from the locked inputs by fixed rules, with no hidden manual steps; and Gates each output against admissibility checks, recording a verdict. The rule that gives this its force is simple: results are never tuned after the fact. If something must change — a weight, a window, a threshold — it becomes a new, separately recorded version, never a silent edit of an old number. The self-corrections reported in the limitations section are exactly this discipline in action.

The bundle. The separate reproducibility bundle contains: a scripts/ directory with one script per analysis (every Tier A result, plus the core pipeline of Section 6); a DATA_LOCK file recording the source and SHA-256 hash of every input; a MANIFEST recording the hash of every output; a gate-verdict table; a README mapping each figure and table in this paper to the script that produces it; and an environment specification (interpreter and library versions). The core pipeline is in the paper itself (Section 6); the bundle holds the bulkier analysis scripts.

The verification protocol. To verify a result, a reader: (1) checks the input hashes against DATA_LOCK; (2) runs the corresponding script under the recorded environment; (3) confirms the gates report pass; and (4) compares the output hash against MANIFEST. A result is reproduced when these agree. For a Tier-B result the reader first fetches the public source named in Table 3; for a Tier-C result, the original archive must be obtained, after which the result is reproducible in the same way.

Empirical tests

Sequence to chromatin phase I: the nucleosome scale

Section 4 reframed a shell as a region’s propensity to occupy a physical phase and a shell boundary as a condensate interface. That reframing is only worth the trouble if it is tested where it makes contact with measurement. This section and the next do that, at two scales. We begin at the scale of the nucleosome — the basic packaging unit, a segment of DNA wrapped on a histone core — because it offers the framework’s two most direct observables in a single, base-pair-resolution dataset: where packaging interfaces fall (the architecture around nucleosome-depleted regions) and how the units pack (the spacing law that the capacity identity predicts). Throughout, “stiffness signal” means the deterministic sequence summary of Section 3.2, read in the phase language of Section 4.

A nucleosome-depleted region (NDR) is a short stretch the packaging machinery leaves comparatively bare; NDRs sit at the functional seams of the genome — promoters, origins, boundaries — and are precisely the places where one packaged domain gives way to another. Sequence to chromatin phase II: the compartment scale, and the runtime knob

We now move up three orders of magnitude, from single nucleosomes to the megabase compartments of the folded genome. Hi-C, a genome-wide assay of which regions contact which, partitions chromatin into two compartments: an open, gene-rich, active “A” compartment and a closed, gene-poor, inactive “B” compartment (Lieberman-Aiden et al., Science, 2009). This is the natural arena for the central claim — that sequence composition sets phase propensity — and, just as importantly, for its sharpest falsifiable consequence, that sequence does not fix where the boundary between phases falls.

From the dial to quantity: the runtime’s other knob

The compartment “dial” of Section 2.2 already showed the runtime acting — tipping which phase a region occupies where the sequence left its propensity intermediate. That is the runtime moving phase. Where the sequence sets the interfacial-tension propensity γ\gamma (which phase, how firm), the runtime sets the fill fraction ϕ\phi (how much is packed in). Scope-relative fixedness: importance predicts conservation

Section 2.6 argued, qualitatively, that the more important a piece of the genome is to function, the more deeply it is fixed. This section makes that argument quantitative and, crucially, non-circular. The danger in any “important things are conserved” claim is circularity: if importance is only ever measured by conservation, the statement is empty. We avoid this by measuring importance four separate ways, each defined independently of conservation, and then asking whether each predicts conservation. Four independent, non-circular measures all pointing the same way is the evidence; their convergence is what no single measure could establish alone.

Theory and open program

New rules and falsifiable predictions

The analyses of Part II were run to test the existing framework, but in aggregate they suggest regularities the framework did not originally state. This section collects six such rules. Each is a hypothesis drawn by inference from the accumulated results, not an established result in its own right; we state each as a claim, give the basis in what we measured, and — the part that makes it science rather than story — state a prediction that could prove it wrong. Some can be partially tested with data already in hand; all are sharpened by the external data of Section 3. We resist the temptation to hedge them into vagueness: the convergence of Part II makes these worth stating boldly and then attacking.

Rule 1 — the sequence encodes a propensity field, not boundaries

Rule 1. Layer 1 (sequence) sets each region’s phase-propensity — which phase it tends to join and how firm the resulting interface is. The location of a domain boundary is not written in the sequence; it is emergent from the competing phases under the runtime conditions.

Basis. GC predicts which compartment a region occupies (AUROC up to 0.840.84; Section 2.1), yet compartment boundaries do not sit at sharp GC transitions (AUROC 0.520.52; Section 2.5), and 24%24\% of bins flip phase across cell types at fixed sequence (Section 2.2).
Corollary. “Position,” the grammar half of the thesis, splits into element-position (where a gene or site sits — encoded) and boundary-position (where an interface falls — emergent); see Definition 11.
Falsifiable prediction. At higher resolution than 100100 kb, boundary sharpness will remain anti-correlated with sequence determination: domain edges will still fail to coincide with sharp sequence features, and boundary position will track condition/runtime more than sequence.

Status — partially testable now; hypothesis. Supported at 100100 kb (the boundary null). The resolution-limited alternative is not yet excluded; finer compartment calls (Section 3) are required.

Rule 2 — the two layers are a condensate’s two knobs

Rule 2. Layer 1 sets a region’s interfacial-tension propensity γ\gamma (which phase, how firm the skin); Layer 2 sets its fill fraction ϕ\phi (how much is packed in). Position is the γ\gamma-knob; quantity is the ϕ\phi-knob (Definition 9).

Basis. The capacity identity is the law of ϕ\phi (Section 3.4); the stiffness signal tracks γ\gamma (Sections 4, 2.1); the cell-type-variable bins concentrate at intermediate GC — intermediate γ\gamma-propensity — exactly where a fill change can tip the phase (Section 2.2).
Unification. This binds the “dial, not a switch” observation to the capacity identity: the dial turns where γ\gamma is intermediate.
Falsifiable prediction. Sequence perturbations should move γ\gamma-behavior (phase identity, interface firmness) but not ϕ\phi; condition perturbations should move ϕ\phi but not γ\gamma. The set of bins that flip phase across conditions should coincide with the intermediate-γ\gamma band.

Status — partially supported; hypothesis. The intermediate-GC concentration of variable bins supports it. Clean separation of the two knobs needs perturbation experiments (Section 3).

Rule 3 — one latent constraint coordinate

Rule 3. The several measures of importance (codon position, functional category, pangenome presence, codon-usage bias) are noisy projections of a single latent “constraint” coordinate — roughly, the number of intersecting functional dependencies on a site — and conservation is a monotone function of that one coordinate.

Basis. Four independent importance axes all predict conservation monotonically and converge (Section 3); two of them correlate in the expected direction (Spearman −0.26-0.26; Section 4.3).
Falsifiable prediction. A single common factor should explain most of the joint variance; a combined constraint score should predict conservation better than any single axis and then saturate.
Current evidence (partial, this project). The axes share a factor but a combined fit (R2=0.154R^2=0.154) does not exceed the sum of singles (0.133+0.0520.133+0.052): constraint has a strong shared component but is not cleanly one-dimensional (Section 4.3).

Status — partial; hypothesis. Shared component confirmed; clean single coordinate not supported. Constraint is multidimensional with a dominant shared axis. A direct test wants structural and network constraint counts (Section 3).

Rule 4 — scope is an invariance timescale

Rule 4. A feature’s scope (Section 2.5) equals the slowest timescale on which it stays invariant. Identity-defining features have deep (phylogenetic) timescales; adaptive features have fast (ecological, expression) ones. “Identity vs adaptation” is “slow-clock vs fast-clock.”

Basis. The same importance→\tofixedness pattern appears at shallow time (four within-species axes, Section 3) and at deep time (18S core, Section 5).
Falsifiable prediction. Ranking features by invariance across nested timescales (individual →\to population →\to species →\to clade) yields a single consistent ordering; that ordering is the scope ladder.

Status — partial; hypothesis. Supported at two separated timescales (shallow + one deep locus). The full multi-timescale ordering needs multi-clade data (Section 3).

Rule 5 — the fixedness geometry is scale-invariant

Rule 5. The functional form relating constraint to fixedness is the same at every scale (codon, gene, genome, molecule, chromatin domain) and timescale; only the units of constraint change. In particular the exponential law of Section 4 holds across axes with a common decay when constraint is suitably rescaled.

Basis. “Steep == fixed, flat == free” and “deep in a phase == locked, near a boundary == soft” recur across every result in Part II; the exponential law appears on two independent axes (Section 4.2).
Falsifiable prediction. Rescaled constraint–conservation curves from different scales collapse onto one master curve; the exponent is scale-invariant.
Current evidence (partial). Two axes give the same exponential form and a comparable decay per SD (ratio 1.451.45; Section 4.2) — partial support, short of a single shared constant.

Status — partial (boldest); hypothesis. The most ambitious unification, and the most falsifiable: it predicts a data collapse. Constructing the collapse across scales (with their different constraint units) needs the external structural/network/fitness axes (Section 3).

Rule 6 — deep-valley positions move only in compensatory groups

Rule 6. A change at a high-constraint position is tolerated only when accompanied by compensatory changes at its co-evolving partners: the deep valley does not let one wall be climbed alone, it is translated as a whole (epistatic buffering).

Basis. The landscape geometry of Section 2: a deep valley’s stability comes from coupling between coordinates, so motion along it must be coordinated.
Falsifiable prediction. The rare nonsynonymous changes at high-constraint positions are enriched for compensatory co-variation with other positions, relative to changes at low-constraint positions.

Status — not testable here; hypothesis. Requires deep multiple-sequence alignments for co-evolution analysis, beyond the 1414-strain depth available here (Section 3).

Continuity of the kind: a landscape theory of fixedness

We come to the question that motivates the rest: why do important things not change — and therefore why, buffeted by its environment, does a kind remain itself? A human develops differently at the pole and the tropics, yet is unmistakably a human in both; a bacterial lineage adapts to a hundred niches yet stays that lineage. Part II establishes that importance predicts fixedness, across four axes and two timescales, and sharpens it into an exponential law. It does not, by itself, say why. This section offers the why. We are explicit at the outset: this section is a theoretical synthesis, a hypothesis. It unifies our confirmed pattern with established physics and developmental biology, and it is consistent with every fingerprint we measured, but it is not proven by sequence data — measuring the objects it invokes requires data this project could not reach (Section 3). With that stated once, we develop it without hedging, because a theory worth testing must first be stated clearly.

The proximate answer, and why it is not enough

The near answer is purifying selection: an individual that changes an important position loses function and leaves fewer descendants, so the change is erased from the population. This is correct and our pN/pS\mathrm{pN}/\mathrm{pS} data measure it directly. But it only moves the question one step: why is some particular position one where change breaks function, while another tolerates it freely? Selection is the eraser; it does not explain the shape of what is being erased. For that we need the geometry.

The landscape

Picture, for a given site, a landscape whose horizontal axis is the space of possible variants and whose height is the function lost by adopting that variant. Some sites sit in a deep, steep, narrow valley; others on a wide, flat shelf. Place a ball — the current state of the lineage — at the bottom of each.

In the deep narrow valley a small horizontal displacement — a mutation — runs the ball up a steep wall, a large loss of function, so it returns or its bearer is removed: the site is fixed. On the flat shelf a displacement costs almost nothing, so the ball drifts: the site varies. The crucial identification is that the steepness of the valley wall is exactly the “firmness” of Section 4, transposed from a chromatin interface to the level of function. A firm site and a steep valley are the same statement: a strong restoring force against displacement. Fixedness is steepness is firmness.

Why important sites have deep valleys: intersecting constraints

This is the geometric heart of the answer. A site is important when it must satisfy many constraints at once. Consider a residue that must (i) fit a particular pocket, (ii) hold the fold together, and (iii) contact three partners: the set of variants satisfying all of these is the intersection of the sets satisfying each, and an intersection of many conditions is small. The more constraints intersect, the smaller the allowed set, the narrower and deeper the valley, the stronger the restoring force. A site with one constraint has a broad permissive shelf; a site with many has a single allowed point. Importance is the number of intersecting constraints, and that number is the depth of the valley. This is purely geometric — it is why “important” and “deeply fixed” are not two facts but one.

This is also where the exponential law of Section 4 fits. If the rate at which a change is accepted falls off with the height of the barrier it must overcome — a Boltzmann-like relation, rate∼e−depth\text{rate}\sim e^{-\text{depth}} — then conservation is exponential in constraint depth, which is what we measured (pN/pS≈e−(⋅)constraint\mathrm{pN}/\mathrm{pS}\approx e^{-(\,\cdot\,)\,\mathrm{constraint}}, R2=0.975R^2=0.975). The measured exponential is the fingerprint of an underlying landscape with this geometry. It does not by itself prove the geometry — but it is exactly what the geometry predicts.

The named classics this recovers

The picture is not new physics; our contribution is to show one framework’s data wearing its fingerprint at many scales. Two established ideas are the same landscape. Canalization (Waddington, 1942; The Strategy of the Genes, 1957): development pictured as a ball rolling down a landscape of branching valleys (“creodes”), so that the same end state is reached despite genetic and environmental perturbation — the deep canal returns the ball. This is precisely “a human stays human”: the developmental trajectory sits in a deep canal that the environment cannot push it out of. And the protein folding funnel (Bryngelson & Wolynes; Onuchic and colleagues): a funnelled energy landscape down which many starting conformations flow to one folded state, with the critical residues that hold the funnel being the conserved ones. Both are our importance→depth→fixedness\mathrm{importance}\to\mathrm{depth}\to\mathrm{fixedness} at the developmental and the molecular scale respectively.

Continuity is the separation of timescales

Now the motivating question can be answered in one line. The environment pushes hard — but it pushes only on the fast, shallow layer: quantities, timing, expression, the ϕ\phi-knob of Rule 2. That is adaptation, and it is supposed to move. It cannot climb the deep, slow valley of the core grammar — the γ\gamma-knob, the body plan, the central machinery — because that valley is too steep and moves only on phylogenetic timescales far slower than any environmental fluctuation. Identity and adaptation are not in tension; they live on different layers with different timescales (Rule 4). A kind stays itself because its identity sits at the bottom of a deep, slow valley the environment’s hand cannot reach, while everything the environment does reach is the shallow, fast runtime that was always meant to move. This is the two-layer proposition of Section 1.2, read as the physics of continuity.

What the data show, and what they cannot

We hold the line on what is established. Across every scale and timescale we could reach, the data carry the fingerprint of this landscape: the synonymous sites tick at a flat neutral clock while functional sites are suppressed in proportion to constraint (Section 3); the suppression is exponential in constraint depth (Section 4); the deepest valleys are the most central machinery (Section 4.4); the chromatin phases are locked in their interiors and soft at their boundaries (Section 2); and the most central molecule holds its core across a billion years while its peripheral parts drift (Section 5). One geometry, many fingerprints. What sequence comparison cannot do is measure the valley directly — its depth, its walls, the count of intersecting constraints at a site. That requires reading the constraints themselves: the contacts and buried positions of solved structures, the connectivity of interaction networks, and the function-versus-variant maps of deep mutational scanning. Those measurements are the content of Section 3, and they are how this section graduates from a theory consistent with fingerprints to one tested against the landscape it posits.

Status — hypothesis (theoretical synthesis). The landscape/canalization account — intersecting constraints →\to deep steep valley →\to restoring force →\to fixedness, with continuity as the separation of a slow deep identity layer from a fast shallow adaptive one — is a synthesis grounded in established physics (Waddington canalization, folding funnels, purifying selection) and consistent with every fingerprint measured in Part II. It is not proven by sequence data. Its falsifiable core (Rules 3, 5, 6): conservation should scale with directly measured constraint — structural contacts, network centrality, mutational-fitness depth — with the exponential form of Section 4. Section 3 lists exactly the data that would confirm or refute it.

Limitations, and the discipline that catches errors

The framework’s credibility rests on being as clear about what it has not shown as about what it has. We collect the limits here, in one place, rather than scattering them.

The confirmed/hypothesis partition

The paper makes claims at three levels, and we never let them blur. Hypothesis (not shown here, external data specified): the geometric mechanism (Section 2, Pillar A); the continuity-of-kind thesis (Pillar B); the single-coordinate and scale-invariance rules (Rules 3,5); the epistasis rule (Rule 6). The status table of Appendix 7 tags every claim.

Depth, circularity, and proxies

Three recurring caveats deserve a single honest statement each. Depth: the importance→\to fixedness principle is confirmed at within-species (strain) depth; deep time rests on one locus. Circularity: we measured importance four ways that are each defined independently of conservation (the genetic code, annotation, gene retention, codon statistics), which is what makes “important things are conserved” a non-empty claim here; the same discipline must hold for every future axis (a structural-contact count or network degree is an independent importance measure, and is allowed to predict conservation). Proxies: ENC is a proxy for constraint, not constraint itself, and the length-matched ortholog method selects indel-free core genes, so all conservation contrasts are lower bounds. None of these undermines the results; each bounds their reach.

The expectation that variable-compartment bins would sit near chance for sequence prediction was wrong — they predict at AUROC 0.790.79, not chance (Section 2.2). The hope for a clean one-dimensional constraint coordinate was only partly met (Section 4.3). Each correction is reported where it occurred. The negative result on boundary localization is presented as a result, not buried, because the phase picture predicted it.

Methods

Conservation (pN/pS\mathrm{pN}/\mathrm{pS}).

For each gene, orthologs across strains are taken as coding sequences of identical length to the K-12 reference, which selects indel-free core genes (and makes all contrasts lower bounds). Differences are classified per codon as synonymous or nonsynonymous under the standard/bacterial code (transl_11, equivalent here to the standard table); pN\mathrm{pN} is nonsynonymous differences per codon, pS\mathrm{pS} synonymous per codon. Synonymous pS\mathrm{pS} is used throughout as a near-neutral mutation-rate baseline.

Codon-usage bias (ENC).

Bias is the effective number of codons, Wright’s NcN_c, computed per gene from synonymous codon usage with the standard degeneracy classes; no external reference set is required. Lower NcN_c (ENC) means stronger bias. Genes shorter than 300300 bp are excluded so that NcN_c is well estimated.

GC and compartments.

Per-bin GC is computed from sequence on the same 100100 kb grid as the compartment eigenvectors. Compartment assignment follows the eigenvector sign (negative == open A). Classification performance is reported as AUROC; monotone associations as Spearman correlations; the gene-density control as a partial Spearman and a logistic model with both predictors (Section 2.4).

Deep-time alignment.

18S rRNA sequences are aligned with FAMSA (via pyfamsa). Pairwise identity is computed over aligned, ungapped column pairs; within-molecule conservation is the per-column agreement over columns present in all five species. The Gonium sequence is excluded as a coverage artifact, following the bundle’s own caveat.

Statistics.

Exponential-law fits use ordinary least squares on decile-aggregated ln(pN/pS)\ln(\mathrm{pN}/\mathrm{pS}) versus the constraint proxy; per-gene fits use a 0.50.5 pseudocount to admit zero-substitution genes. Enrichment zz-scores use position-matched random expectation.

DNA grammar dictionary (quick reference)

A terse distillation of the framework, for quick reference; the body (Sections 2–3.4) expands each item.

Objects (A4). shell (stiffness segment) ⋅\cdot anchor (fixed boundary or recognized position) ⋅\cdot motor (machine site) ⋅\cdot loop (3D contact). Position in the sequence is a coordinate on the helix (3.43.4 Å and 34.29∘34.29^\circ per base pair).

Grammars. G1 physics: GC →\to stable/stiff; AT and poly-A tracts →\to soft/open. G2 recognition: a short pattern →\to a protein binds. Everything else commonly called a “rule” is one of these applied to a different cis value.

Dynamics. INIT →\to SCONSERV (satisfy hard constraints, conservative) →\to SDISSIP (relax, dissipative) →\to JEVENT (irreversible cut/bond/lock). Gates: an irreversible event releases energy (ΔE<0\Delta E<0); residual stress does not rise during relaxation.

Selection. Among the configurations a region admits, the one realized minimizes residual stress PresP_{\mathrm{res}} subject to hard constraints; ordering matters, not the absolute value; lowest-in-set is not global-optimum.

Parameters and scope. A value is cis (in the sequence) or trans/environmental (set by the cell). Scope runs local →\to regional →\to global →\to individual; a global value is invisible to per-site regression and appears only in whole-genome fitting.

Variability. Fixed (constant, carries no variation), cis (sequence-set), environmental (cell-set), genetic×\timesenvironment (both, hardest). What is fixed is relative to scope; the scope of first variation names the biological unit.

The asymmetry. Position is strongly sequence-encoded (Layer 1); quantity is set by the runtime, global resources, and copy numbers the sequence may not contain (Layer 2).

Conventions. Composition over called bases; width-independent nearest-site matching; recognition motifs learned within folds; AT-matched negatives.

Reproducibility. Lock–Derive–Gate; core pipeline in the paper; analysis scripts and hashes in the bundle (Appendix 5).
