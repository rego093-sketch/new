Foreign-material incorporation as a cross-chronometer accuracy limit

It does not dispute either clock — it addresses sample-level accuracy.

What this establishes

Radiocarbon and zircon U–Pb are accepted, independently validated clocks; this does not dispute either. It addresses accuracy at the level of the individual sample — set by contamination and event attribution rather than decay physics — and shows that the dominant failure shared by both has a single structure.

Scope & honesty

The decay clocks themselves are taken as established and are not disputed. Every quantitative claim is tagged directly confirmed [F], inferential [I], or assumption/open [A]. The work is explicit about the working range of each method (both the young and the extreme-old ends degrade) and about the one irreducible epistemic asymmetry: in deep time, accuracy is established by consilience, not by comparison with written records. A companion to the matrix-informed radiocarbon protocol.

Questions

Does this challenge radiocarbon or U–Pb dating?

No. Both decay clocks are accepted; the paper addresses accuracy at the level of an individual sample — contamination and event attribution — and offers a correction and exclusion protocol.

How can zircon contamination be removed but radiocarbon's cannot?

In zircon the older material sits in discrete grains and appears as separable age populations that can be excluded from the data; in radiocarbon the old carbon is mixed atom-for-atom, so it is homogeneous and must be corrected externally.
