Foreign-Material Incorporation as a Cross-Chronometer Accuracy Limit

Extending matrix-informed radiocarbon correction to zircon U–Pb geochronology: one mechanism, two detectabilities, and an honest accounting of where dating accuracy actually breaks.

Young Jae Lee  ·  Independent Researcher, Daegu, Republic of Korea
ORCID 0009-0002-7535-8245
Working draft · June 2026 · Companion to “Matrix-Informed Correction and Exclusion Protocol for Biogenic Radiocarbon Dating,” Zenodo 10.5281/zenodo.17718893

Abstract

Radiocarbon and zircon U–Pb are accepted, independently validated dating methods; this paper does not dispute either clock. It addresses a different and more general problem — accuracy at the level of the individual sample, set by contamination and event attribution rather than by the decay physics. We show that the dominant accuracy failure shared by both chronometers has a single structure: incorporation of foreign, older material biases a measured age toward greater age (the radiocarbon reservoir / dead-carbon effect; zircon inheritance, antecrysts and detrital recycling). The two differ only in detectability. In radiocarbon the old carbon is mixed at the atomic level and is therefore homogeneous, inseparable, and hidden — it must be corrected externally. In zircon the old material resides in discrete grains and therefore appears as separable age populations that can be excluded from the data itself. We formalise this as a two-axis decomposition (internal integrity vs. event attribution), unify the underlying physics through the Dodson closure number, and define a single protocol: screen → classify → correct-where-characterisable → cross-check → report honest uncertainty. We demonstrate the protocol on real data: a leave-one-out reservoir correction on published radiocarbon pairs; the canonical Lava Creek Tuff zircon dataset, where classification by crystal position (not by age) recovers the eruption age and isolates older antecrysts; and open-access SHRIMP-RG and detrital-zircon data. We are explicit about the working range of each method, about the assumptions correction depends on, and about the one irreducible epistemic asymmetry: in the deep-time interval, accuracy is established by consilience — concordance, cross-method agreement, astrochronology — not by direct comparison with written records.

IInferential. A sound but chain-dependent conclusion; strength stated where it matters.

1Scope and premise

The clocks are not the problem. The samples are.

Two claims must be separated at the outset, because conflating them is the most common error in critiques of geochronology. The first is that a decay clock is unreliable. The second is that a particular sample has been dated inaccurately. This paper concerns only the second.

The decay rates underlying both methods are measured directly in the laboratory, without any geological input. A pure uranium source emits a definite number of alpha particles per second; counting that activity over an hour fixes the decay constant through \(A=\lambda N\), and hence the half-life, with no clock “running” over geological time and no circular reference to a rock’s age [5]. F Decay constants are environment-independent over the relevant range: natural-reactor (Oklo) and astrophysical (SN 1987A) constraints bound any drift in the fine-structure constant to below \(10^{-7}\) over two billion years [8]. F We therefore take the clocks as given and do not re-litigate them.

What remains genuinely uncertain, and what controls accuracy in practice, is whether the material analysed actually records the event of interest, and whether it has exchanged matter with its surroundings. This is the domain of the present work, and it is the same domain the companion radiocarbon paper addresses for biogenic carbon [1]. The aim here is to show that radiocarbon and zircon U–Pb fail in the same way, to make that shared failure explicit and quantitative, and to handle it with a single, transparent protocol.

2Two axes of dating accuracy

Internal integrity is one thing; event attribution is another — and the second is where the error lives.

Every radiometric age can be decomposed along two independent axes.

Axis A — internal integrity (the clock). Given a closed system and a known initial state, does the measured parent–daughter ratio yield the right elapsed time? For U–Pb the answer is unusually strong, because two independent clocks (\(^{238}\mathrm{U}\!\to\!^{206}\mathrm{Pb}\) and \(^{235}\mathrm{U}\!\to\!^{207}\mathrm{Pb}\)) must agree — the concordance constraint, a built-in self-check that radiocarbon lacks. F

Axis B — event attribution (the sample). Does the dated material correspond to the event one wishes to date, and has it remained a closed system? This axis carries essentially all of the accuracy risk that this paper is about. It is not a precision limit and not a technology limit; it is an interpretive and geochemical limit.

The causal chain from decay law to reported age makes the separation precise: most links are directly confirmed or screened, but the closing link — attribution and closure — is where the chain is load-bearing and where validation is asymmetric.

3One contamination mechanism, two detectabilities

Old material in, age out too old — in both methods. They differ only in whether the contamination is separable.

The central observation of this paper is that the dominant Axis-B failure is identical across the two chronometers in mechanism and in sign:

Unified mechanism. Incorporation of foreign, older material into the dated sample biases the measured age toward greater age. In radiocarbon this is the reservoir / hard-water / dead-carbon effect and old-carbon contamination; in zircon it is inheritance, antecrysts, xenocrysts and detrital recycling.

The mechanism is bidirectional in its failure modes. Loss of the daughter product biases ages younger: episodic Pb loss in radiation-damaged zircon, and modern-carbon contamination in radiocarbon. Accuracy therefore requires guarding both directions.

Where the two methods diverge is not in the mechanism but in its detectability, and this single distinction explains why zircon is comparatively robust to the contamination both methods suffer.

Because the radiocarbon contaminant is chemically identical to the sample carbon and mixed atom-for-atom, it cannot be separated — only subtracted using an externally estimated reservoir offset, which varies with locality and context. Because zircon inheritance arrives as separate crystals carrying their own concordant ages, it presents as distinct age modes that can be identified and removed. I This is the precise sense in which a single zircon date is reliable for the grain analysed but requires context to be trusted as the age of a rock-forming event: the date is robust; the attribution is the weak link.

4Shared physics: the closure / exchange number

Reservoir exchange and diffusive closure are the same dimensionless problem.

The companion radiocarbon framework characterises matrix exchange through a Fourier-type diffusion number, and the same dimensionless group governs diffusive retention in minerals. The diffusion number,

$$ N_{D} \;=\; \frac{D\,\tau}{L^{2}} \;\equiv\; \mathrm{Fo}, $$

is the ratio of a characteristic diffusion length to the system size, and it is formally the Fourier number \(\mathrm{Fo}\) of heat/mass transfer. Dodson’s closure-temperature formulation,

$$ T_{c} \;=\; \frac{E}{R\,\ln\!\left(\dfrac{A\,\tau\,D_{0}}{a^{2}}\right)}, $$

contains the same dimensionless grouping \(A\tau D_0/a^2\) inside the logarithm [2]. Dodson explicitly noted that a frozen chemical system in contact with a reservoir is formally identical to the closure-temperature problem — which is exactly what unifies the radiocarbon reservoir effect with diffusive isotopic closure and, by extension, with the retention that preserves a zircon’s crystallisation age. I The practical consequence is that the same small set of dimensionless quantities controls (i) how faithfully a radiocarbon sample records atmospheric \(^{14}\mathrm{C}\) and (ii) how faithfully a zircon retains the daughter Pb that fixes its age. The two papers are thus methodological siblings, not analogies of convenience.

5The unified protocol

Screen → classify → correct where characterisable → cross-check → report honest uncertainty.

The protocol applies to both chronometers; only the implementation of each step differs, following Table 1.

The decisive design choice is in Step 2 for zircon: classification is made on age-independent grounds — crystal position, texture, common-Pb content — not by selecting the youngest age. This is what keeps the procedure non-circular, and §7 demonstrates it on real grains.

6Demonstration I — radiocarbon, the homogeneous case

Reservoir contamination is hidden, so it must be corrected externally — and the correction can be validated out-of-sample.

The companion paper’s reservoir correction was tested by leave-one-out (LOO) cross-validation on published, independently constrained radiocarbon pairs. F On the Elk Hills shell–charcoal series the correction reduced the root-mean-square error from 418 yr to 141 yr (a 66% reduction); on the Lake Chichancanab leaf-wax/macrofossil pairs it reduced RMSE from 739 yr to 453 yr (39%). The smaller Chichancanab gain is itself informative: it signals limited transferability of a locally estimated reservoir offset, exactly the caveat the protocol requires reporting. I

7Demonstration II — zircon U–Pb, the canonical case

On the real Lava Creek grains, classifying by crystal position recovers the eruption age and isolates the older antecrysts — without ever using age to decide.

We applied the protocol to the published grain-level zircon U–Pb dataset for the Lava Creek Tuff (LCT) supereruption (Matthews, Vazquez & Calvert, 2015), comprising 183 SHRIMP-RG analyses with \(^{230}\)Th-corrected \(^{206}\mathrm{Pb}/^{238}\mathrm{U}\) dates, common-Pb fractions, crystal-position labels (crystal faces vs. interiors/cores) and stratigraphy [3]. F

Classifying strictly by crystal position — faces are the last-grown material and therefore closest to eruption; cores grew earlier — yields:

Honest caveats. (i) The sanidine \(^{40}\mathrm{Ar}/^{39}\mathrm{Ar}\) value used here as a quick cross-check (≈638 ka, all-fusion weighted mean of dataset S2) was not reduced with proper step-heating/plateau and outlier criteria; the published curated sanidine eruption age is ~625–631 ka. The robust anchors are the faces (626.5 ka), the published eruption age (631 ka) and the MIS tie (~630 ka). A (ii) Classifying faces vs. cores is reliable only when CL texture and chemistry are unambiguous; where they are not, the assignment can fall back on age, which is the genuine residual vulnerability discussed in §9 — see the independent near-rim dataset that places LCT crystallisation at 658.8 ± 6.6 ka [4].

8Demonstration III — zircon U–Pb, the general case

Common lead is real and can wreck an apparent age — and the data themselves reveal it. Inherited grains dominate detrital populations — and the youngest constrains the true event.

To show the screening steps on fully open data, we used the standard geochronology toolbox IsoplotR’s real example datasets [6]. F

Common-Pb screening and correction. A real \(^{204}\)Pb-bearing suite is, before correction, severely discordant — \(^{206}\mathrm{Pb}/^{238}\mathrm{U}\) and \(^{207}\mathrm{Pb}/^{235}\mathrm{U}\) dates of ~429 and ~1300 Ma, a 67% discordance — because non-radiogenic common \(^{206,207}\)Pb inflates the signal. This is the direct, empirical answer to the question “is there \(^{206}\)Pb in nature?”: there is, and it is measurable through \(^{204}\)Pb. Correcting for it (Stacey–Kramers common-Pb composition [7]) restores concordance at ~312 Ma. A second, clean suite is concordant at ~249 Ma without correction. F

Honest caveat. The common-Pb correction depends on an assumed common-Pb isotopic composition. Its leverage grows as the common fraction grows — i.e. for young, low-radiogenic grains — which is precisely why young U–Pb ages are intrinsically harder, independent of instrument quality (§9). A

Inheritance in detrital populations. In a real detrital-zircon sample the single-grain ages span ~129–2994 Ma and are dominated by old source grains. The depositional event is constrained only as younger than the youngest coherent grains (here ~130–200 Ma) — a maximum depositional age, never the deposition age itself. F This is the inheritance mechanism in its purest form, and it confirms the user’s reasoning that the youngest population carries the event constraint — with the §7 safeguard that one screens by concordance first, because Pb loss can make a grain spuriously young.

9Working range and honest limits

Every chronometer has a working range, both ends degrade, and deep-time accuracy rests on consilience — not on written-record ground truth.

Both ends degrade. U–Pb is not uniformly precise across all ages. At the young end the accumulated daughter is minute and is swamped by even trace common Pb, so apparent ages are dominated by the common-Pb correction and its assumed composition (§8). At the extreme-old end the parent is depleted — \(^{235}\mathrm{U}\) falls to ~1.3% of its initial amount by ~4.4 Ga, so \(^{207}\mathrm{Pb}/^{235}\mathrm{U}\) becomes hard for the oldest samples (\(^{238}\mathrm{U}\) remains ~50%, so \(^{206}\mathrm{Pb}/^{238}\mathrm{U}\) and \(^{207}\mathrm{Pb}/^{206}\mathrm{Pb}\) are preferred there). The method has a range, like any measurement. F

The irreducible asymmetry. Radiocarbon is directly validated in its core interval against historical artefacts and dendrochronology counted to 12,593 calendar years, with the floating Lake Suigetsu varve record extending it further (its independence partly tied to Hulu Cave) [9]. F Zircon U–Pb, by contrast, operates in an interval that cannot be checked against any written record. Its accuracy is established by consilience: internal concordance of two decay clocks, agreement with independent methods (e.g. \(^{40}\mathrm{Ar}/^{39}\mathrm{Ar}\)), and astrochronological tuning. This is strong evidence, but it is not the same kind of evidence as direct ground truth, and this paper states the distinction plainly rather than eliding it. I

Residual soft spots, stated. (i) Autocryst/antecryst assignment can become circular where CL texture and trace-element chemistry are ambiguous — the LCT itself has a competing near-rim interpretation at 658.8 ± 6.6 ka [4], and other systems are reported as inconclusive. A (ii) Reservoir-offset transferability in radiocarbon is locality-dependent, as the unequal LOO gains in §6 show. A Neither is concealed; both define where the protocol’s uncertainty is genuinely large.

10Validation methodology

Confirmed anchors, out-of-sample tests, and a standing distinction between fact and inference.

The framework is held to three rules, applied throughout this paper. First, primary validation uses independently confirmed reference ages — historically attested for radiocarbon, and for zircon the published, cross-anchored eruption ages — rather than ages produced by the same model. Second, corrections are tested out-of-sample (leave-one-out), because a correction that only improves the points used to build it is a curve fit, not a clock. Third, every quantitative claim is tagged F/I/A, so that directly confirmed facts are never silently merged with chain-dependent inferences or with assumptions. Strong, independently convergent evidence is weighted above long or self-referential chains, and single-source reasoning is flagged where it occurs.

11Conclusion

A respectful, contamination-aware accuracy framework — unified across chronometers, and honest about its own limits.

Radiocarbon and zircon U–Pb are not in question as clocks. Their shared accuracy bottleneck is the incorporation of foreign older material, which raises measured ages and which differs between them only in detectability — homogeneous and hidden in radiocarbon, granular and separable in zircon. Recognising this lets one treat both with a single protocol whose central discipline is to classify on age-independent grounds and to validate out-of-sample. Demonstrated on real radiocarbon pairs, on the canonical Lava Creek zircon dataset and on open SHRIMP-RG and detrital data, the protocol recovers the right ages where the material permits and declares the cases where it does not. Its honesty is structural: it states the working range of each method, the assumptions each correction rests on, the residual ambiguity in attribution, and the one place where deep-time accuracy is a matter of consilience rather than of direct, written-record ground truth. That is the strongest claim the evidence supports — and it is the claim this paper makes.
