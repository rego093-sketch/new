A Constitution of Method

Article I — On evidence.A claim earns standing here only from the structure of data and the direction of cause — never from authority, consensus, popularity, or familiarity, and never from mere contrarianism either. Agreement with the prevailing view is not evidence for a claim; disagreement with it is not evidence against one. Each claim is tested as if no one had ever ruled on it.

Article II — On the burden.Because we do not borrow the warrant of consensus, we carry the burden ourselves: every claim must be defined independently of its own confirmation (no circularity), must survive the removal of confounders, and must be separated, explicitly, into what the data establish and what remains hypothesis. The freedom to depart from consensus is paid for by this discipline.

Article III — On honesty toward the result.We follow the data wherever it points — including when it points back at the consensus, and including when it refutes our own prior claims. A negative result the framework predicted is reported as a result. A correction is kept visible, not erased.

Article IV — On interpretation, not pattern.A claim about any DNA region is earned only by the framework’s own interpretation — the deterministic pipeline that turns a sequence into structure: the stiffness signal, its segmentation into shells, the anchors and boundaries it places, the A4 arrangement, the four-activity dynamics, and the selection of a configuration by minimum residual stress (and, for cis, the position-and-strength decomposition this yields). It is never earned by pattern- or similarity-counting on the raw letters: shared \(k\)-mers, alignment or conservation scores, motif frequencies, composition statistics, or any other proxy that reads resemblance in place of performing the interpretation. Such pattern measures are, at most, a pointer to where the interpretation should be applied; they are not themselves evidence, and any result that rests on them is inadmissible. This Article is binding without exception: pattern analysis is prohibited as the basis of any claim.

Article IV notice — pattern-derived results are inadmissible; coordinate/geometry results stand. Applying Article IV to this document’s own record requires a precise line, drawn here after review. Inadmissible are results obtained by similarity-counting on the letters — shared/exact \(k\)-mers, conserved-enhancer signals and counts, conservation fractions, the CpG-density proxy, and alignment-style scores. The affected results include, without limitation: the ZRS measurement (\(112\) vs \(1\) shared \(15\)-mers; R21); the early-locus “conserved-enhancer” signals and counts (SIX3 \(28\), OTX2 \(48\); R23); the CpG-proxy inversion (R24); the early-versus-growth “depth-law” ratio (\(8.3\times\); H-cis3); the simulation cis thresholds derived from CpG density (v2) and from human–mouse enhancer conservation (v3); and the \(48\times\) human–elephant growth-cis comparison’s conservation half (Section 36.5).

Article IV forbids passing pattern off as interpretation; it does not forbid reading the genome’s coordinates and physical shape, which is exactly what the 4D method does.

Part I The framework

1 Introduction: what it means to interpret DNA

This paper is written so that a reader meeting these ideas for the first time can follow them end to end and, with the code we provide, carry out the same interpretation on a genome of their own. We therefore build every concept from the ground up and define each term where it first appears, including terms that specialists treat as obvious. Nothing in the argument requires the reader to take a claim on authority: each quantitative statement is tied to a procedure that can be run and checked.

1.1 Two ways to read a genome

A genome is a long text written in a four-letter alphabet — the bases adenine, cytosine, guanine, and thymine, written A, C, G, T. The two strands of the double helix pair these bases in a fixed way (A with T, G with C), so one strand determines the other and we may speak of “the sequence” as a single string. To read or interpret that string can mean two very different things, and most confusion about “what DNA does” comes from running the two together.

The first kind of reading recovers the rules written in the string itself. Some stretches of sequence resist being bent and wrapped around the spool-like proteins that package DNA; others bend easily. Some short patterns are recognized by the protein machines that copy or read the genome; most are not. Some regions pull open into single strands at a low energy cost; others hold shut. These regularities live in the letters, and they hold wherever those letters appear — in any cell, of any tissue, on any day. A rule of this kind is valuable precisely because it compresses: one statement accounts for many positions at once.

The second kind of reading asks how a particular stretch of sequence is actually used in a particular cell at a particular moment: how often a given starting point for DNA copying is used in one round of replication; how much of a gene’s product is made; how early or late, in the cell’s internal schedule, some event occurs. This information lives only partly in the letters. Most of it lives in the state of the cell — the concentrations of ions and proteins, the mechanical strain on the DNA, how crowded the interior is, where in its life cycle the cell sits.

Throughout, we will need two standard words for “where a value comes from.” A value is cis (Latin for “on the same side”) when it is determined by the local sequence itself — its composition, the short patterns it contains, its length, its local shape. A value is trans (“across”) or environmental when it is set by something acting from elsewhere — a diffusing protein, the ionic environment, the cell-cycle clock. The distinction is not a matter of opinion: one can test whether a quantity is cis by asking how well it is predicted from sequence alone.

Conflating the two readings produces two opposite errors. If one expects the sequence to explain everything, then every measurement the sequence fails to predict is dismissed as “noise.” If one expects every measured quantity to be a hidden message in the sequence, then values the cell sets on the fly are mistaken for grammar that was never there. The discipline of this paper is to keep the two readings apart and to measure, for each output, how much of it is written in the letters and where the rest comes from.

1.2 The two-layer proposition

We compare the genome to source code and the living cell to a running program. Source code does not act on its own; it is a specification that a runtime executes. The same code, run on different machines or with different inputs, produces different behavior, yet the program’s structure is fixed by the code. We propose that DNA relates to the cell in the same way, and we separate two layers.

  • Layer 1 — the generative specification. This is the position-determined information written in the one-dimensional sequence: the specification of what structural feature goes where. It is produced by applying a few universal physical and chemical rules (the “two grammars” of Section 2.2, to come) to position-specific cis values. Layer 1 fixes where and what.

  • Layer 2 — the cellular runtime. This is the temporal and environmental state of the cell that executes the specification: ion concentrations, supercoiling (the over- or under-winding of the helix), molecular crowding, the cell-cycle clock, competition for limited resources, and the large-scale three-dimensional arrangement of the genome in the nucleus. Layer 2 sets how much and when.

The central proposition is deliberately simple, and the rest of the paper tests it: the genome strongly encodes position (Layer 1), while the runtime sets quantity (Layer 2). The two layers are not in tension; they are different jobs. Position is the kind of thing a fixed text can specify well. Quantity — how often, how much, how fast — depends on conditions that the text cannot know in advance, and so it is set at run time.

There is a physical form of this division, developed in full in Section 4, that we state here so the reader carries it through Part I. The structures the sequence specifies are phase-separated condensates, and such a domain has exactly two properties to set: how firm its boundary is — an interfacial tension \(\gamma\) — and how much material fills it — a fill fraction \(\phi\). Layer 1 (the sequence composition) sets the interfacial-tension propensity \(\gamma\): which phase a region prefers. Layer 2 (the runtime) sets the fill fraction \(\phi\): the quantity. “Position as grammar” is the \(\gamma\)-knob and “quantity as runtime” is the \(\phi\)-knob. One consequence is worth flagging now because it sharpens the proposition rather than softening it: “position” is really two things. Element-position — where a gene or a recognized site sits — is written in the sequence. Boundary-position — where the interface between two domains falls — is emergent from the competing phases at run time, and is not promised by the local letters. The two-layer claim, made precise, is that the sequence strongly encodes phase propensity and element-position, while the runtime sets fill and the boundary locations follow from the physics. Part II tests both halves, including the negative one.

A construction analogy makes the division concrete, and we use it only to fix intuition; the quantitative claims below stand on data, not on the metaphor. A blueprint fixes how many rooms a house has and where the load-bearing frame goes; it does not, by itself, build the house. The construction site supplies materials — without them nothing is built — and the same blueprint yields a somewhat different house in the tropics than at the pole, with reinforcement added on site where conditions demand. Yet the large structure (the number of rooms, the placement of the frame) is fixed by the blueprint and is robust to site conditions, while finishes, timing, and amounts are set on site. In the same spirit, Layer 1 fixes the structural plan and Layer 2 supplies the materials and sets the amounts. Figure 1 summarizes the whole picture.

1.3 From prediction to explanation

Modern sequence-to-output predictors are very good at one thing: given a sequence, they return a number, often with high accuracy on data drawn from the same distribution they were trained on. They do not, however, say why a sequence yields one structure rather than another that looks equally plausible on energetic grounds. The protein-folding problem is the classic case: a single one-dimensional chain reliably folds to one three-dimensional shape, and accurate predictors can now anticipate that shape, yet this sharpens rather than answers the question of why that shape is the necessary outcome.

This paper is built to answer the “why.” For a given sequence it states, in order: the physical and recognition tendencies the sequence carries (the two grammars, Section 2.2); the arrangement of structural constraints those tendencies produce (the A4 arrangement, Section 3.1); and the principle that selects, among the arrangements the sequence admits, the one actually realized — minimum residual stress under fixed physics (Section 3.4). Where a quantity is not fixed by the sequence, the framework says so explicitly and points to the runtime measurement that does fix it.

This stance does not compete with high-accuracy predictors; it composes with them. The framework partitions each output into a part that explicit rules already account for and a residual that a flexible learner — supplied with the right inputs, namely sequence plus runtime plus multiple conditions — can close. The result is a hybrid that is both high-reaching and interpretable, and that remains decomposable: one can always ask which part of a prediction came from a stated rule and which from the learned residual. The practical payoff is that interpretation becomes a program with named parts rather than a single opaque number.

1.4 What is established, and what is new

We state our position precisely rather than with a blanket disclaimer. The individual biological observations this paper builds on are established in the literature: that the local sequence influences how DNA bends and how easily it opens; that specific short patterns mark the sites where DNA copying begins; that those sites compete for a limited supply of the factors needed to fire; that one value can be used by many downstream processes at once; and that the spacing of packaging proteins differs between species. None of these facts is claimed here as a discovery.

What is new is threefold. First, the organizing framework and its formalization: the explicit two grammars, the four-dimensional arrangement objects (shells, anchors, motors, loops), the residual-stress selection principle, and the treatment of fixedness as relative to a scope (Section 2.6, to come). Second, the falsifiable two-layer decomposition: for each output we state which part is written in the sequence and where the remainder comes from, and we report the measured split. Third, a reproducible, multi-organism set of analyses and the results they produce — the sequence-to-chromatin-phase results (nucleosome packing and the compartment-scale phase prediction), the importance-to-fixedness results and the exponential conservation law, the deep-time core-conservation result, and the runtime (quantity) results — each of which ships with code. Some of these analyses produce findings not present in the original single-organism study, for example the exponential form of the conservation law across four independent constraint axes and the predicted negative result that compartment boundaries are not placed by sequence.

1.5 What this paper claims — and does not

The claims are concrete and testable.

  1. Position — which physical phase a region prefers, where its structural elements sit, and how locally it is shaped — is strongly encoded by the sequence (Layer 1), and we report the measured strength on real data.

  2. Quantity — how much, how often, how fast — is dominated by the cellular runtime (Layer 2); sequence alone explains little of it until runtime measurements are added.

  3. The mapping from a sequence to its structural arrangement can be made a deterministic, auditable pipeline that, for any input region, either produces the arrangement and an event record or returns an explicit, labeled failure.

  4. What is fixed and what varies is not absolute but relative to a chosen reference population (a “scope”); the scope at which a value begins to vary identifies the biological unit it distinguishes (individual, species, or life itself).

We state the boundaries once, as facts rather than as ritual hedging. The paper does not claim to predict whole-organism phenotype from sequence; that requires the runtime and developmental information of Layer 2, which we do not model in full. The residual-stress measure (Section 3.4) ranks candidate structures under a fixed parameter set; it is a selection criterion, not a measured free energy, and a structure shown to be lowest among an enumerated set of alternatives is not thereby proven to be the global optimum over all conceivable configurations. Finally, we do not assert that the event-to-event variation seen at the single-molecule level is reducible; whether it reflects unmeasured higher-scope state or something irreducible is left open, because the data does not settle it. Leaving this open is a statement about the evidence, not a posture.

1.6 Reproducibility and how each result can be checked

Because the point of this paper is that DNA interpretation can be done, not merely described, every quantitative result carries a tag stating how a reader can reproduce it. We use three tiers.

Analyses we run from public sequence data using code shipped in the reproducibility bundle: the E. coli openability and stiffness results, the human chromosome-21 variation counts, the multi-genome composition and dinucleotide signatures, the TP53 translation and positional-precision demonstrations, the small-subunit ribosomal phylogeny, and the B-form helix coordinates. Yeast quantitative results whose source data sit in archives not used for the present runs (the messenger-loading and replication-timing measurements). These are reported with full citation to their original sources and marked as carried over; supplying those datasets promotes them to Tier A.

The whole pipeline is governed by a simple contract we call Lock–Derive–Gate. Lock freezes the assumptions, parameters, and algorithm versions for a run. The core pipeline — the mapping from sequence to the A4 arrangement — is given in full inside this paper (Section 6); the complete script set, input hashes, and gate tables are in the separate bundle.

2 Conceptual framework

This section fixes the vocabulary used throughout the paper. It is offered as a proposed way of organizing the phenomena, to be tested against the data of Part III; where a statement is framework (a choice of how to carve things up) rather than a claim about data, we say so. Every term is defined where it first appears, and the reader is not expected to bring prior familiarity with any of them.

2.1 Two axes: what exists, versus what it means

The most common cause of confusion in the question “what does this piece of DNA do?” is that two different questions are run together. Separating them removes most of the ambiguity.

Axis 1 — the structural and functional inventory answers “what exists here?” It is a parts list. There are four kinds of part, and we define each from scratch.

  • Shell (the structural casing). A shell is a stretch of DNA described by how stiff or how easily opened it is. DNA is not uniformly rigid: some segments bend and pull apart at a low energy cost, others stay straight and stay shut. The reason is physical and simple. An A–T base pair is held together by two hydrogen bonds; a G–C pair by three. A region rich in A and T — especially one containing long runs of A or of T — is therefore less tightly held: it is more flexible and it opens more readily. A region rich in G and C is more stable and stiffer. A soft, AT-rich shell is where the helix can breathe, bend, or be pried open; a stiff, GC-rich shell resists. We make “soft” and “stiff” quantitative in Section 3.1; for now a shell is simply a contiguous segment with a characteristic stiffness.

  • Anchor (a fixed site). An anchor is a fixed reference point along the DNA. Two kinds matter: the boundary between one shell and the next (where the stiffness changes sharply), and the position of a recognized short pattern (a handle that a specific protein grips). Anchors are the nails on which the rest of the structure hangs; their defining feature is that the sequence fixes where they are.

  • Motor (an active machine). A motor is the site of an active molecular machine — for example a transcription start site, where the machinery that reads a gene docks and begins to move. We use “motor” for the location and action of such a machine. The machines themselves (the polymerases and the like) are universal hardware, essentially identical in every cell.

  • Loop (a three-dimensional contact). A loop is a contact in space: two points that lie far apart along the one-dimensional sequence are brought close together in three dimensions, typically by tethering a motor to an anchor. Loops are how a linear text folds into a working three-dimensional object.

Figure 2 shows the four kinds of part on a single stretch of DNA.

Axis 2 — the information and causal hierarchy answers a different question: “what is that part, informationally?” Is it a universal rule, a per-site value, or a result? Classification belongs on this axis, and it has four layers, set out in Table 1.

Table 1. The information and causal hierarchy (Axis 2). Classification is done on this axis.
Layer Content and universality
OS \(\cdot\) machines (motors) The executor proteins: polymerases, the replicative helicase, the origin-recognition complex, chromatin remodelers, cohesin. These do things; they are not grammar, and they are essentially identical in every cell.

The two axes cross, and that is why a question like “is a motor part of the operating system?” can feel ambiguous: the answer depends on which axis one means. On Axis 1 a motor is a part in the inventory; on Axis 2 the machine a motor represents is pure operating system (universal hardware), an anchor is a cis parameter (a value the sequence sets), and shells and loops are mixtures of substrate and output. Keeping the axes apart dissolves the apparent ambiguity.

2.2 The two grammars

Section 1.1 promised that the rules written in the sequence can be stated. There are, we argue, essentially two of them, and almost everything else commonly called a “rule” turns out to be one of these two applied to a different local value.

G1 — DNA physics. The double helix is a physical object, and its local behavior follows from the bases it is made of. The bases stack on one another like coins in a roll; the backbone carries negative charge; the two strands are zipped by hydrogen bonds. From these facts follow the local shape of the helix, how stiff it is, how it curves and so folds from a one-dimensional string into a three-dimensional path, how readily it melts (its two strands separate), and when it flips into alternative (“non-B”) forms under the twisting stress of supercoiling or under particular ionic conditions. The single most useful fact in G1 is the one already introduced: because an A–T pair has two hydrogen bonds and a G–C pair has three, AT-rich DNA is less stable, more flexible, and more easily opened than GC-rich DNA. This one statement compresses an enormous number of individual observations, which is exactly what qualifies it as grammar.

G2 — molecular recognition. Certain short sequence patterns are handles: a specific protein grips them and not the surrounding DNA. The pattern that marks where DNA copying begins, the sites that transcription factors recognize, and the signals that help position packaging proteins are all of this kind. G2 is the rule that maps a sequence (and the shape it implies) to the propensity for a particular protein to bind there.

A discipline: “understood” is not the same as “grammar.” Having measured or understood a quantity does not make it grammar. We reserve the word grammar for a predictive rule that compresses many instances into a single statement. If we merely know a value, it is a parameter (whose upstream cause may or may not be known); if it is a physical part (a helix, a loop, a positioned nucleosome), it is substrate or output. The test is mechanical and is the same test used throughout Part III: regress the candidate quantity on the sequence (and on the rules already known). The part that is predicted is grammar; the part that is not is a genuine parameter, on which one recurses to find its upstream cause; the part that differs from event to event under identical conditions is per-event variation, whose reducibility we leave open. Applied honestly, this test collapses a long list of supposed “rules” — positioning preferences, the width of A-tracts, origin anchors, non-B propensity, local shape — into instances of G1, with recognition folded into G2, acting on different cis values. The conclusion is that there are about two grammars; the rest are parameters and outputs.

2.3 Four classes of variability

A parameter can be classified by what it varies with. Two yes/no questions — does it change with the genetics (the sequence)? does it change with the environment (the cell’s state)? — give a two-by-two scheme with four classes, shown in Table 2.

Table 2. Four classes of variability, by what a parameter varies with. Detection difficulty rises down the table.
Class What it varies with Detection Explains variation?
Fixed neither (nearly constant) easy no (constant)
cis the genetics (sequence) only by regression yes
Environmental the cell’s state only needs an assay yes
Genetic \(\times\) environment both, interacting hardest yes

Detection difficulty rises down the table: a fixed value is trivial to read off; a cis value can be found by regression on the sequence; an environmental value requires an assay of the cell’s state; and a value that depends on the interaction of genetics and environment is the hardest of all to pin down. There is a key asymmetry here, which Part III demonstrates with measurements: a fixed parameter is easy to measure, but precisely because it is constant it explains none of the variation between sites. Predictive power comes not from the fixed baseline but from local deviations away from it. The tension is central to the whole enterprise — the easy parameters carry no information, while the information-rich ones (those that also vary with the environment) are the hard ones to find.

2.4 Shared parameters, and pleiotropy

Parameters are often shared: a single value is read, at the same time, by many different processes. The concentration of calcium ions, for instance, is used at once by processes as different as egg formation, bone deposition, signaling, and muscle contraction. It is not that there is one calcium value “for” one tissue and a different one “for” another; a common number is read in many places. There is no central processor that gathers all the values and decides what happens. Each local process reads the shared values it needs, and the outcomes emerge — a dataflow or reaction network, not a single controller. (“Pleiotropy” is the standard word for one cause having many effects; a shared parameter is its mechanism.)

This has a sharp consequence for detection. A parameter dedicated to one output is easy to find by studying that output. But a shared, global, or upstream parameter is hard to find in any single output, because its effect there is distributed, indirect, and redundant with the proximal values it has already influenced. Such parameters are often global variables of the cell (ions, metabolites, master regulators, limited resources) rather than local sites. Part III shows a concrete case: a shared activator that makes replication origins markedly more efficient — a real, large biological effect — yet adds almost nothing to a prediction once the proximal parameters it acts through are already included.

2.5 The scope of a parameter

Parameters also differ in their range of action — their scope — and much of the part of an output that the local sequence fails to explain consists of larger-scope parameters that are invisible at the local level. Figure 3 arranges the levels.

A local parameter acts at one site — a single recognized pattern, say. A regional one acts over tens of kilobases, like a domain whose copying is scheduled together. A global parameter is one number shared by the whole cell — the total amount of a limiting resource, for example — and because it is the same for every site in a given condition, it is invisible to a regression that compares sites within that condition: it contributes a constant, and a constant has no explanatory power across sites. Such a global value reveals itself only in one of two ways: by fitting a model to the whole genome at once (so that the single shared number becomes a fitted parameter), or by comparing across conditions or individuals. An individual-scope quantity, like a person’s height, is a sum over thousands of sites plus global signals, and is likewise invisible from any single site.

The same ladder can be read along time rather than space, and the two readings coincide. The scope of a value is equivalently the slowest timescale on which it stays invariant: a local, individual-level value changes on fast (ecological, expression, generational) timescales, while a value shared across a whole clade changes only on slow (phylogenetic) ones. Reading scope as an invariance timescale is what lets the same axis carry both “where a parameter acts” and “how deeply fixed it is,” and it is the axis on which identity (deep, slow) separates from adaptation (shallow, fast). Section 2.6 turns this into the fixedness claim, and Part II measures the fixedness directly.

2.6 Fixedness is relative to a scope

“Fixed” and “variable” are not absolute properties. They are relative to a chosen reference population — a scope — and this is the hinge of the whole framework: the scope at which a value begins to vary identifies the biological unit that the value distinguishes. Three levels make the point.

  • Fixed across all of life. Base pairing, the genetic code, the chemistry of the helix, the core machinery — these are the same in every living thing. They therefore define life itself. Breaking one is lethal (the largest possible consequence), and because all organisms share them, they do not distinguish one species from another.

  • Fixed within a species, variable between species. A value that is constant in every healthy member of one species but differs between species distinguishes species or clades. The spacing of packaging proteins along the DNA is a clean example: it is essentially constant within one organism but differs across organisms.

  • Variable within a species. A value that differs among the members of one species distinguishes individuals: the copy number of repeated genes, single-base differences, larger structural differences, expression levels.

It follows that “how catastrophic it is to break a value” scales with the depth of its fixedness: breaking a universally fixed value is lethal; changing a species-fixed value tends toward inviability or toward the boundary between species; varying an individual-level value is simply normal diversity. This single idea binds together the four variability classes (Section 2.3), the scope axis (Section 2.5), and the familiar observation that the more important a piece of the genome is to function, the more strongly it is conserved. Part III measures examples at each level.

Part II makes this scaling quantitative rather than merely ordinal. Across four independent, non-circular measures of functional importance — codon position, functional category, pangenome presence, and codon-usage bias — conservation rises monotonically with importance, and the relationship takes a specific functional form: substitution falls exponentially in a constraint proxy, \(\mathrm{pN}/\mathrm{pS}\approx \exp(a + b\,\mathrm{ENC})\) with a strongly linear log–constraint relation at the aggregate level (Section 12.2). We flag the status carefully: the exponential form is supported on two independent constraint axes within a species; the stronger claim that a single decay constant governs all scales (Rule 13.1) remains a hypothesis pending external data. The qualitative statement of this subsection — the more important a piece of the genome, the more deeply fixed — is the ordinal shadow of that law.

3 The formal objects

Section 2 gave the vocabulary as intuition. This section makes the four objects precise and computable, defines the stiffness signal that turns a sequence into shells, states the dynamics by which an arrangement settles into a structure, and gives the principle that selects which structure forms. The definitions are written so that the core pipeline of Part II is a direct transcription of them; nothing here is left informal.

3.1 The A4 arrangement

The pipeline operates on a region, and produces an A4 arrangement from it.

Definition 1 (Region). A region is a triple \(\mathcal{R}=(S,\mathcal{A},\theta)\), where \(S\) is a DNA sequence of length \(L\) over the alphabet \(\{\texttt{A},\texttt{C},\texttt{G},\texttt{T},\texttt{N}\}\) (the symbol N marks a base that was not determined), \(\mathcal{A}\) is an optional set of annotations restricted to the region (for example, transcript coordinates), and \(\theta\) is a parameter dictionary fixing the window length \(W\), the step \(\Delta\), algorithm versions, and any random seed. The pipeline is a function of \(\mathcal{R}\) alone: same inputs, same output.

Definition 2 (A4 arrangement). The A4 arrangement of a region is the tuple \(\mathrm{A4}=(\text{shells},\text{anchors},\text{motors},\text{loops},\text{meta})\), where meta records the versions and provenance needed to reproduce it. When no annotation is supplied, motors and loops are empty; this is permitted, but it changes what can be claimed (there is then no transcript-driven structure).

The four components are the precise versions of the Axis-1 parts of Section 2.1.

Definition 3 (Stiffness shell). A shell is a maximal contiguous run of windows that share a coarse stiffness label (defined in Section 3.2). Each shell stores its coordinate interval \([s,e)\) and the mean of its normalized stiffness signal, \(\bar z\).

Definition 4 (Anchor). An anchor is a tuple \((\mathrm{id},p,k,\alpha)\): a position \(p\) in base pairs, a kind \(k\in\{\text{region edge},\ \text{shell boundary},\ \text{shell core}\}\), and a non-negative strength \(\alpha\) that, for a boundary, equals the size of the stiffness discontinuity there. Positions lie in \([0,L]\) and identifiers are unique within a run.

Definition 5 (Motor and loop). A motor is a tuple \((\mathrm{id},p,\text{strand})\) giving the position of a transcript-driven feature — a transcription start — in local coordinates. A loop is a tuple \((\mathrm{id},\text{motor\_id},\text{anchor\_id},d)\) pairing a motor with an anchor at sequence distance \(d=|p_{\text{motor}}-p_{\text{anchor}}|\). Loops are generated by joining each motor to its nearest \(k\) anchors under a fixed rule, so the loop set is determined by the region and \(\theta\).

3.2 The stiffness signal: turning a sequence into shells

This is the step that delivers the “soft” and “stiff” of Section 2.1 as numbers. The pipeline slides a window of length \(W\) along the sequence and, for each window, computes three quantities.

Definition 6 (Stiffness signal). For each window the pipeline computes the GC fraction \(\mathrm{GC}\) (the share of bases that are G or C), the CpG density \(\mathrm{CpG}\) (the frequency of adjacent CG pairs), and the AT-tract density \(\mathrm{AT}_6\) (the frequency of runs of at least six As or six Ts). The stiffness signal is the fixed linear combination \[\mathrm{stiff} \;=\; w_{\mathrm{GC}}\,\mathrm{GC} \;+\; w_{\mathrm{CpG}}\,\mathrm{CpG} \;+\; w_{\mathrm{AT}}\,\mathrm{AT}_6,\] with weights \((w_{\mathrm{GC}},w_{\mathrm{CpG}},w_{\mathrm{AT}})\) fixed in the Lock.

Each ingredient has a physical reading, and we state it in the phase language of Section 4 rather than as a bending rigidity. The GC fraction tracks the base-pairing energy through the three-versus-two hydrogen-bond fact of Section 2.2, and thereby a region’s propensity to demix into a firmer-bounded phase (a higher interfacial-tension propensity \(\gamma\)). The CpG density singles out a dinucleotide that marks regulatory regions in many genomes, sharpening the signal where regulation lives. The AT-tract density captures something the GC fraction alone misses: long runs of A or T have a distinctive local structure that tends to exclude packaging proteins, marking regions whose phase behavior differs from their GC content alone. We read the resulting shell labels as phase-propensity labels — which coexisting phase a region tends to join (Definition 8) — not as a ranking of how rigid or how compact the helix is. We state plainly that the weights are a modeling choice recorded in the Lock, and that the signal is a deterministic, auditable summary of the sequence — not a complete physical model of chromatin mechanics. Its job is to produce a reproducible segmentation, and Part III tests how well that segmentation lines up with biology.

The raw signal is then put on a common scale and smoothed. Rather than a mean and standard deviation, which a few extreme windows would distort, the pipeline uses a robust standardization built from the median \(m\) and the median absolute deviation \(d=\mathrm{median}(|x_i-m|)\) (the typical distance of a value from the median): \[z_i \;=\; \frac{x_i-m}{1.4826\,\max(d,\epsilon)},\] with a small \(\epsilon\) to avoid division by zero (the factor \(1.4826\) makes the scale agree with a standard deviation for ordinary data). A short moving average then removes single-window noise without introducing long-range dependence.

Finally the smoothed signal is cut into shells. Let \(q_1\) and \(q_2\) be its 33rd and 66th percentiles; each window gets a label of \(0\) (soft), \(1\) (intermediate), or \(2\) (stiff) according to which tercile it falls in, and a shell is a maximal run of windows with the same label. Very short shells are merged into the neighbor whose mean is closer, to avoid unstable fragments. At each junction between two shells the boundary strength is the size of the jump, \(\sigma=|\bar z_b-\bar z_a|\), and an anchor is placed there with strength \(\sigma\). 3.3 How an arrangement settles: the four-activity dynamics

The shells, anchors, motors, and loops describe what the sequence offers. To turn that offer into a settled structure, the framework uses a fixed grammar of four activities, and no others; any variety in behavior must come from the arrangement, not from inventing new kinds of activity.

  • INIT sets up the starting state from the A4 arrangement.

  • SCONSERV is a conservative step: it projects the current state onto the set of configurations that satisfy the hard constraints (no two parts occupying the same space, no buried charges, the stiffness ordering between rigid and flexible elements). It conserves energy and is, in principle, reversible.

  • SDISSIP is a dissipative step: it relaxes the configuration by descending on the residual-stress measure of Section 3.4, shedding strain. It increases entropy and is not reversible. In the condensate reading of Section 4, SDISSIP is demixing and coarsening: at the runtime-set fill fraction \(\phi\), the region separates into phases and its interfaces relax toward the partition of least interfacial energy, so the Lyapunov property below is the statement that a phase-separation free energy is being minimized.

  • JEVENT is an irreversible discrete event — a cleavage, a bond formation, a topological locking — applied when a local energy barrier is crossed. It advances time and writes a row to an event ledger.

This is the coarse-grained form of a standard split in non-equilibrium physics into a conservative part (which preserves energy) and a dissipative part (which produces entropy), with the irreversible events treated as discrete jumps. One further ingredient is worth naming because it explains handedness: an interaction built from a triple product of orientations changes sign under reflection, and so it is what gives a structure a fixed chirality or a direction of rotation — the right-handedness of a fibrous helix, or the single turning direction of a rotary motor. The relaxation has a guarantee attached: the residual-stress measure behaves like a Lyapunov function — a quantity that does not increase as the system relaxes — so the dynamics settles rather than wanders. The admissibility gates of the Lock–Derive–Gate contract enforce exactly these properties: every JEVENT must release energy (\(\Delta E<0\)) rather than absorb it, and the residual stress must not rise during a relaxation phase. A run that violates a gate is flagged, not quietly accepted.

3.4 The selection principle: minimum residual stress

We can now answer the “why this structure” question of Section 1.3. Among the configurations a region admits, the one realized is the one that settles to the lowest residual stress while satisfying every hard constraint.

Definition 7 (Residual stress). The residual stress of a configuration \(\Psi\) is the dimensionless sum \[P_{\mathrm{res}}(\Psi) \;=\; \sum_k \lambda_k\,\delta_k(\Psi) \;+\; \sum_\ell \mu_\ell\,\sigma_\ell(\Psi),\] where each \(\delta_k\) measures a violation of a hard constraint (a steric clash, a buried charge), each \(\sigma_\ell\) measures a softer strain (excess bending), and the non-negative weights obey a fixed hierarchy \(\lambda_k \gg \mu_\ell\) recorded in the Lock. Small \(P_{\mathrm{res}}\) means the configuration nearly satisfies its constraints; large \(P_{\mathrm{res}}\) means it is strongly frustrated.

In practice the candidate configurations are not an unstructured continuum but a finite, explicitly listed set — the topologically distinct, geometrically feasible arrangements the region admits — and the pipeline relaxes each, records its settled \(P_{\mathrm{res}}\), and selects the minimum among those that satisfy all hard constraints. Two honesty points belong here, and we state each once. First, what matters is the ordering of candidates, not the absolute numbers; the scale of \(P_{\mathrm{res}}\) depends on the weights, while the ranking does not. Second, a configuration shown to be lowest within an enumerated set is not thereby proven to be the global optimum over all conceivable configurations; the strength of the claim is the strength of the candidate set. \(P_{\mathrm{res}}\) is a selection criterion, not a measured free energy.

There is a clean geometric reading of why a configuration is frustrated, and it is the law of the fill knob \(\phi\) of Section 4.1. Picture a condensate — the core of a folded chain, the interior of a chromatin domain — that holds \(N\) units of effective size \(v_{\mathrm{eff}}\) at fill fraction \(\phi\). The configurations that sit at low residual stress are those that nearly satisfy the capacity identity \[N\,v_{\mathrm{eff}} \;\approx\; \phi\,V_{\mathrm{core}},\] without overshooting it. Overfill it (\(N\,v_{\mathrm{eff}}>\phi\,V_{\mathrm{core}}\)) and the interior clashes; underfill it and the domain is dilute and its interface cannot hold a stable shape. Both deviations raise \(P_{\mathrm{res}}\) (Figure 5), for the same reason a droplet has a preferred size at a given amount of material. Here \(\gamma\) (set by Layer 1) fixes how costly a misshapen interface is, and \(\phi\) (set by Layer 2) fixes how full the interior is; the capacity identity is where the two knobs meet. The same reading applies to any container the framework treats this way — the core of a folded chain, the cross-section of a fibre, the circumference of a ring: each needs the right amount of empty space, neither crammed nor hollow, a point that recurs throughout the case studies.

3.5 Position is a three-dimensional coordinate

A final point closes the gap between “where in the sequence” and “where in space.” The B-form double helix has a fixed local geometry: each base pair is raised by a rise of \(h\approx 3.4\) Å along the axis and rotated by a twist of \(\varphi\approx 34.29^\circ\) relative to the one before, so that the helix completes one full turn every \(10.5\) base pairs, and the bases sit at a radius \(\rho\approx 10\) Å from the axis. The position of base pair \(n\) is therefore literally a point in space, \[\mathbf{r}(n) \;=\; \big(\rho\cos n\varphi,\ \rho\sin n\varphi,\ n\,h\big).\] The one-dimensional order of the sequence is a three-dimensional coordinate on the helix. As a worked instance, the first base pair sits near \((8.3,\,5.6,\,3.4)\) Å and the twenty-second near \((8.3,\,5.6,\,74.8)\) Å— the same angular position as the first, because the twenty-one base-pair steps between them make almost exactly two turns, but raised by \(71.4\) Å along the axis (Figure 6). We are precise about the scope of this statement: it is the local, intrinsic coordinate of the helix itself. The higher-order folding — the loops of Section 3.1, and the large-scale arrangement of the genome in the nucleus — is a separate matter, and it is exactly where measurements of three-dimensional contact (which we do not have here) would enter.

4 The stiffness shell as a condensate interface

[Reconstructed by Claude from the v3 abstract, glossary, and the reproducibility bundle; the quantitative anchors are faithful to results/ and status_table.csv, but this prose is a reconstruction, not the author’s original chapter.]

The framework’s “stiffness shell” is not a metaphor: it names the firm interface of a phase-separated biomolecular condensate — a domain formed by liquid–liquid phase separation, with a measurable interfacial tension and a frequency-dependent (viscoelastic) mechanical response. Reading the shell this way gives the two-layer split a physical mechanism and makes it falsifiable.

4.1 The two knobs: interfacial tension and fill fraction

A condensate domain has two independently settable properties. The interfacial tension \(\gamma\) is the energy cost per unit area of the phase boundary — the fast, elastic resistance of the surface to a quick poke; it is the physical content of a shell’s “firmness,” and Layer 1 (sequence composition) sets it. The fill fraction \(\phi\) is how much material is packed into the domain, and Layer 2 (the runtime) sets it. The two meet in a capacity identity, \(N\,v_{\mathrm{eff}} \approx \phi\,V_{\mathrm{core}}\): a container is frustrated when overfilled (steric clashes) or underfilled (voids), so the realized state is the one whose fill leaves the right amount of empty space — the minimum-residual-stress configuration of Section 3.4, now read as a packing optimum.

Definition 8 (The reading rule: phase propensity). The sequence-only stiffness signal encodes phase propensity — which coexisting phase a region tends to join — rather than how compact the region is. A region’s letters set its tendency to partition into the firm or the open phase; they do not set the amount of material the runtime later packs in.

Definition 9 (Element-position versus boundary-position). Element-position — where a gene or recognized site sits — is encoded in the sequence (Layer 1). Boundary-position — where the interface between two phases actually falls — is emergent from the competition of the coexisting phases at runtime (Layer 2). The first is written; the second is computed by the cell.

This sharpens the central asymmetry into a single falsifiable prediction: element positions track the sequence, while boundary positions do not. Section 10 confirms exactly this at two scales — compartment identity is predictable from composition (AUROC up to \(0.84\)) while compartment boundaries are not sequence-placed (AUROC \(0.52\), chance).

Conventions, stated once. Three conventions are used wherever they apply, and they matter for honesty. (i) Composition (GC, AT) is computed over called bases only: the denominator excludes N, so that a stretch with undetermined bases is not silently diluted toward a misleading value. (ii) When a signal is matched to a site, a width-independent nearest-site rule is used rather than a window of fixed flank, because a fixed flank induces a bias toward wider features. (iii) When a recognition pattern (a position weight matrix) is learned, it is learned within cross-validation folds only, so that the same data are never used to both train and test; and when the effect of composition must be removed, negatives are matched in AT content to the positives. (iv) For the cross-genome arrangement analyses (version 4), orthology between genomes is assigned by DIAMOND reciprocal best hits on reduced proteomes (one longest protein per gene) or by gene-symbol matching, and the arrangement measures (gene order, chromosomal linkage, cluster purity) are computed on coordinates and are independent of the orthology call.

6 The core pipeline

This section keeps the promise of Section 1.6: the mapping from a region to its A4 arrangement is given here in full, as runnable code, so that interpretation can be done and checked rather than only described. The module below is a direct transcription of Section 3; the bulky analysis scripts that produce the results of Part III live in the separate bundle, but this core — the part everything else depends on — is in the paper.

What it does. Given a sequence (and, optionally, a GFF annotation for one identifier), the pipeline computes the windowed observables and the stiffness signal of Section 3.2, standardizes and smooths it, cuts it into shells, places anchors at the shell boundaries with a strength equal to the size of the stiffness jump, reads transcription starts from the annotation as motors, joins each motor to its nearest anchors as loops, and finally runs the admissibility gates. It depends only on numpy and the standard library, and it is deterministic: the same region and the same Lock give the same A4 arrangement.

The Lock. The parameters are fixed at the top of the module in a dictionary named LOCK: the window length and step (\(W=2000\), \(\mathrm{step}=500\) base pairs), the stiffness-signal weights (\(w_{\mathrm{GC}}=1.0\), \(w_{\mathrm{CpG}}=0.5\), \(w_{\mathrm{AT}}=-0.5\); the negative AT-tract weight encodes that poly-A/poly-T tracts mark locally open, nucleosome-disfavoring DNA), the smoothing half-width, the minimum shell length, and the number of nearest anchors per motor. Changing any value defines a new version, never a silent edit of an old result.

The gates. Four invariants are checked on every run and reported as pass/fail: the shells cover the whole region, the shells are contiguous (no gaps or overlaps), every anchor lies within the region, and anchor identifiers are unique. A failed gate is surfaced, not hidden.

How to run it. Save the module as key_pipeline.py and either run it directly (python3 key_pipeline.py), which executes the demonstration at the bottom on a bacterial genome, or import it and call run_key(seq, gff=..., seqid=...) on a region of one’s own.

#!/usr/bin/env python3
"""KEY: map a DNA region (sequence + optional GFF annotation) to an A4 arrangement.

Deterministic. Requires only numpy and the standard library. This module is a
direct transcription of Section 3 of the whitepaper: it computes a windowed
stiffness signal, segments it into shells, places anchors at shell boundaries,
extracts motors (transcription starts) from annotation, and joins motors to
their nearest anchors as loops. It then runs admissibility gates.
"""
import numpy as np

# ---- LOCK: fixed parameters. Changing any of these defines a new version. ----
LOCK = dict(
    W=2000, step=500,                 # window length and step, in base pairs
    w_gc=1.0, w_cpg=0.5, w_at=-0.5,   # stiffness-signal weights (Section 3.2)
    smooth_radius=2,                  # moving-average half-width, in windows
    min_shell_bp=5000,                # merge shells shorter than this
    loop_k=2,                         # number of nearest anchors per motor
    eps=1e-9,
)


def read_fasta(path):
    seqs, name, buf = {}, None, []
    with open(path) as fh:
        for line in fh:
            if line.startswith('>'):
                if name is not None:
                    seqs[name] = ''.join(buf)
                name, buf = line[1:].split()[0], []
            else:
                buf.append(line.strip().upper())
    if name is not None:
        seqs[name] = ''.join(buf)
    return seqs


def windowed_observables(seq, W, step):
    """Return (centers, GC, CpG, AT6) per window using cumulative sums."""
    a = np.frombuffer(seq.encode('ascii'), dtype=np.uint8)
    n = len(a)
    A, C, G, T = 65, 67, 71, 84
    is_a, is_c, is_g, is_t = (a == A), (a == C), (a == G), (a == T)
    is_acgt = is_a | is_c | is_g | is_t
    is_gc = is_g | is_c
    cg = np.zeros(n, dtype=bool)            # CpG: C at i, G at i+1
    cg[:-1] = is_c[:-1] & is_g[1:]
    at6 = np.zeros(n, dtype=bool)           # start of a run of >=6 A or >=6 T
    if n >= 6:
        ca = np.concatenate([[0], np.cumsum(is_a)])
        ct = np.concatenate([[0], np.cumsum(is_t)])
        run_a = (ca[6:] - ca[:-6]) == 6
        run_t = (ct[6:] - ct[:-6]) == 6
        at6[:len(run_a)] = run_a | run_t
    cum_gc = np.concatenate([[0], np.cumsum(is_gc)])
    cum_ac = np.concatenate([[0], np.cumsum(is_acgt)])
    cum_cg = np.concatenate([[0], np.cumsum(cg)])
    cum_a6 = np.concatenate([[0], np.cumsum(at6)])
    starts = np.arange(0, max(1, n - W + 1), step)
    ends = np.minimum(starts + W, n)        # v7 fix: clip to length (n<W safe)
    denom = cum_ac[ends] - cum_ac[starts]
    keep = denom > 0
    starts, ends, denom = starts[keep], ends[keep], denom[keep]
    gc = (cum_gc[ends] - cum_gc[starts]) / denom
    cpg = (cum_cg[ends] - cum_cg[starts]) / max(1, W - 1)
    a6f = (cum_a6[ends] - cum_a6[starts]) / max(1, W - 5)
    centers = (starts + ends) // 2          # v7 fix: use clipped ends
    return centers, gc, cpg, a6f


def stiffness_signal(gc, cpg, at6, lock=LOCK):
    return lock['w_gc'] * gc + lock['w_cpg'] * cpg + lock['w_at'] * at6


def robust_z(x, eps):
    m = np.median(x)
    d = np.median(np.abs(x - m))
    return (x - m) / (1.4826 * max(d, eps))


def smooth(z, radius):
    if radius <= 0 or len(z) == 0:
        return z
    pad = np.pad(z, radius, mode='edge')
    ker = np.ones(2 * radius + 1) / (2 * radius + 1)
    return np.convolve(pad, ker, mode='valid')


def segment_shells(centers, z, L, min_shell_bp):
    """Tercile-label, run-length into contiguous shells, merge short ones."""
    n = len(z)
    if n == 0:
        return [[0, L, 1, 0.0]]
    q1, q2 = np.percentile(z, 33), np.percentile(z, 66)
    lab = np.where(z <= q1, 0, np.where(z <= q2, 1, 2))
    mids = [0] + [int((centers[i] + centers[i + 1]) // 2) for i in range(n - 1)] + [int(L)]
    shells, start = [], 0
    for i in range(1, n + 1):
        if i == n or lab[i] != lab[start]:
            shells.append([mids[start], mids[i], int(lab[start]),
                           float(np.mean(z[start:i]))])
            start = i
    changed = True
    while changed and len(shells) > 1:
        changed = False
        for i, sh in enumerate(shells):
            if sh[1] - sh[0] < min_shell_bp:
                if i > 0 and i < len(shells) - 1:
                    left, right = shells[i - 1], shells[i + 1]
                    tgt = i - 1 if abs(left[3] - sh[3]) <= abs(right[3] - sh[3]) else i + 1
                elif i > 0:
                    tgt = i - 1
                else:
                    tgt = i + 1
                ln_t = shells[tgt][1] - shells[tgt][0]
                ln_s = sh[1] - sh[0]
                mean = (shells[tgt][3] * ln_t + sh[3] * ln_s) / max(1, ln_t + ln_s)
                if tgt == i - 1:
                    shells[i - 1] = [shells[i - 1][0], sh[1], shells[i - 1][2], mean]
                else:
                    shells[i + 1] = [sh[0], shells[i + 1][1], shells[i + 1][2], mean]
                del shells[i]
                changed = True
                break
    for sh in shells:                       # relabel by final mean after merging
        sh[2] = 0 if sh[3] <= q1 else (1 if sh[3] <= q2 else 2)
    return shells


def build_anchors(shells, L):
    anchors = [dict(id=0, pos=0, kind='region_edge', strength=0.0)]
    aid = 1
    for i in range(1, len(shells)):
        strength = abs(shells[i][3] - shells[i - 1][3])
        anchors.append(dict(id=aid, pos=int(shells[i][0]),
                            kind='shell_boundary', strength=float(strength)))
        aid += 1
    anchors.append(dict(id=aid, pos=int(L), kind='region_edge', strength=0.0))
    return anchors


def read_gff_tss(path, seqid, L):
    motors, mid = [], 0
    with open(path) as fh:
        for line in fh:
            if line.startswith('#'):
                continue
            f = line.rstrip('\n').split('\t')
            if len(f) < 8 or f[0] != seqid or f[2] not in ('gene', 'mRNA', 'transcript'):
                continue
            start, end, strand = int(f[3]), int(f[4]), f[6]
            tss = start if strand == '+' else end
            if 0 <= tss <= L:
                motors.append(dict(id=mid, pos=int(tss), strand=strand))
                mid += 1
    return motors


def build_loops(motors, anchors, k):
    loops, lid = [], 0
    apos = np.array([a['pos'] for a in anchors])
    for m in motors:
        d = np.abs(apos - m['pos'])
        for j in np.argsort(d)[:k]:
            loops.append(dict(id=lid, motor_id=m['id'],
                              anchor_id=anchors[int(j)]['id'], d=int(d[int(j)])))
            lid += 1
    return loops


def gates(A4, L):
    sh, an = A4['shells'], A4['anchors']
    checks = [
        ('shells_cover_region', bool(sh) and sh[0][0] == 0 and sh[-1][1] == L),
        ('shells_contiguous', all(sh[i][1] == sh[i + 1][0] for i in range(len(sh) - 1))),
        ('anchors_in_range', all(0 <= a['pos'] <= L for a in an)),
        ('anchor_ids_unique', len({a['id'] for a in an}) == len(an)),
    ]
    return [(name, 'PASS' if ok else 'FAIL') for name, ok in checks]


def run_key(seq, gff=None, seqid=None, lock=LOCK):
    L = len(seq)
    centers, gc, cpg, at6 = windowed_observables(seq, lock['W'], lock['step'])
    z = smooth(robust_z(stiffness_signal(gc, cpg, at6, lock), lock['eps']),
               lock['smooth_radius'])
    shells = segment_shells(centers, z, L, lock['min_shell_bp'])
    motors = read_gff_tss(gff, seqid, L) if (gff and seqid) else []
    anchors = build_anchors(shells, L)
    loops = build_loops(motors, anchors, lock['loop_k']) if motors else []
    A4 = dict(length=L, shells=shells, anchors=anchors,
              motors=motors, loops=loops, lock=lock)
    A4['gates'] = gates(A4, L)
    return A4

Executed on the Escherichia coli K-12 chromosome (U00096.3, \(4{,}641{,}652\) base pairs), the pipeline returns \(470\) shells, \(471\) anchors, \(4{,}506\) motors (one per annotated transcription start), and \(9{,}012\) loops, and all four gates report pass. The algorithms in words (so the listing is self-explaining).

Every observable is computed in one linear pass using prefix (cumulative-sum) arrays, so the cost is \(O(n)\) in sequence length rather than \(O(nW)\) for a window of width \(W\). windowed_observables builds cumulative counts of GC, of valid bases, of CpG dinucleotides, and of homopolymer-run starts, then reads any window’s content as the difference of two cumulative values; it slides a window of width \(W\) by step and reports, per window, GC fraction, CpG density, and long-AT-run frequency. (Version-7 fix: the window ends are clipped to the sequence length, so a locus shorter than \(W\) — a small enhancer or a \(3'\)UTR — is read as one partial window instead of indexing past the cumulative array; this changes nothing for any locus with \(n\ge W\), and the E. coli gate numbers above are unchanged.) stiffness_signal forms a fixed linear combination of the three (GC and homopolymer runs raise stiffness; CpG marks an openable element); robust_z standardizes it by median and median-absolute-deviation; smooth applies a fixed-radius moving average. segment_shells cuts the standardized signal at tercile thresholds into maximal contiguous runs of one coarse class (soft / mid / stiff) no shorter than min_shell_bp; build_anchors places a boundary at every class change, with strength equal to the stiffness step across it; read_gff_tss marks motors at annotated transcription starts; build_loops pairs each motor with its \(k\) nearest anchors. Finally gates refuses any arrangement whose shells fail to tile the region contiguously from \(0\) to \(L\), or whose anchors fall out of range or collide — the admissibility check that makes the output trustworthy rather than merely plausible. Every later script in this paper (the within-family size tests, the stop-coordinate count, the switch model, the form-from-cis simulation) is documented in the same way: its algorithm stated in words beside its result.

7 How to interpret a region, step by step

This section is the practical heart of the paper. With the core pipeline of Section 6 in hand, a reader can take a region of their own and carry out an interpretation. The procedure has five steps, and we then work a real example all the way through, honestly, including a lesson about scale that the example itself forces.

7.1 The five steps

  1. Get a region. Obtain the sequence of the stretch of interest, and (if you have it) an annotation giving transcript coordinates. State the coordinates and the source, so the run is reproducible.

  2. Run the core pipeline. Call run_key on the region. Choose the window length to match the scale of the question — a whole chromosome is read well with a \(2\)-kb window, a focused \(100\)-kb neighborhood with a \(500\)-bp window — and record the choice in the run’s Lock. The output is the A4 arrangement plus the gate verdicts.

  3. Read the four components. The shells are the regional stiffness/openability landscape; the anchors are the fixed boundaries (with a strength that says how sharp each one is); the motors are the transcription starts; the loops are the motor-to-anchor contacts.

  4. Split Layer 1 from Layer 2. Everything the A4 reports — where shells and boundaries sit, where motors are, which anchors a motor contacts — is Layer 1: structure and position fixed by the sequence. What the A4 does not report — how much is transcribed, how often an origin fires, when an event happens — is Layer 2, and requires runtime data the sequence cannot supply.

  5. State the interpretation, scoped honestly. Say what the sequence fixes, mark what would need runtime measurement, and match any fine-scale claim to a tool of the right resolution.

7.2 A worked example: the neighborhood of the E. coli replication origin

We interpret the \(100\)-kb stretch of the K-12 chromosome centered on the replication origin oriC (region \(3{,}875{,}859\)–\(3{,}975{,}859\); oriC itself near \(3{,}925{,}860\)). Because this is a focused neighborhood rather than a whole genome, we run with a finer window (\(W=500\), step \(100\), minimum shell \(3{,}000\) bp); the choice is part of the run’s Lock. All four gates pass. The pipeline returns eleven shells (Table 4), ninety-four motors (one per annotated transcription start in the window), and one hundred eighty-eight loops.

The Layer-1 / Layer-2 split. Everything above is Layer 1: it is fixed by the sequence, and the pipeline produces it deterministically. What we cannot read from the A4 is how strongly those promoters fire, or how efficiently oriC initiates replication in a given cell — those are Layer 2 quantities, set by the runtime, and Part III shows that for replication origins they are barely predictable from sequence until runtime measurements are added.

The lesson about scale. Note that the shell containing oriC (shell 5) is only middling in stiffness at this \(500\)-bp scale, and its AT content (\(47.4\%\)) is in fact slightly below the genomic average (\(49.2\%\)). This is not a failure; it is the shell map doing its job as a regional summary. The openability of oriC itself is a finer-scale, more localized property — it lives in a window of a few hundred base pairs, not in a \(15\)-kb shell. To see it, one drops to the targeted tight-window composition analysis of Section 8, where oriC’s own \(\sim\)400-bp window is markedly AT-rich (\(59\%\) against the \(49\%\) background). Interpreting a region well means choosing the resolution of the tool to match the question: the coarse shells for the regional landscape, the tight-window analysis for a specific site.

7.3 What the A4 tells you, and what it does not

In one sentence: the A4 arrangement is a complete, reproducible statement of the Layer-1 structure of a region — the stiffness landscape, the boundaries, the machine positions, and the contacts they imply — and it is deliberately silent about Layer-2 quantities, which it flags as the province of runtime data. A reader who internalizes this split will neither over-read a quantity into the sequence nor dismiss an unexplained quantity as noise; they will know exactly which question the sequence can answer and which it cannot.

A complete, self-contained interpretation that carries a real sequence through all of these steps at once — the A4 arrangement, the openability zoom, a translated gene, and the Layer-1/Layer-2 split, all from one short driver — is distributed with the reproducibility bundle (Appendix 14); the bundle’s scripts are the recommended starting point for a reader who wants to interpret a sequence of their own.

8 Analysis methods

Beyond the core pipeline, the paper reports a set of focused analyses, each of which isolates one facet of the framework. This section states the method for each; Part III reports the results. The conventions of Section 5 (composition over called bases, width-independent matching, in-fold motif learning, AT-matched negatives) are used throughout and not repeated.

8.1 Composition and openability (Tier A)

For any set of regions, GC and AT fractions are computed over called bases only. To test the framework’s prediction that functional sites which must open are AT-rich, two comparisons are made: coding versus intergenic-spacer AT within each genome, and the AT of a tight window at a landmark (the replication origin) against a background distribution of many random windows of the same size, reporting the deviation in standard deviations (\(\sigma\)). The same windowed signal underlies the shells of the core pipeline.

8.2 Dinucleotide signatures (Tier A)

Each genome is summarized by the frequencies of its sixteen ordered dinucleotides, a sixteen-dimensional vector. The distance between two genomes is the Euclidean distance between their vectors. Comparing within-species distances (between strains or chromosomes of one organism) to between-species distances tests whether composition is fixed at the species level.

8.3 Sequence variation (Tier A)

From population variant files (VCF), the number of polymorphic sites — positions that actually differ within the sample — is counted, both per population and per individual, and expressed as a fraction of the callable genome. The complementary quantity, the fraction identical across individuals, measures within-species fixedness. Sites that are invariant carry no individual-level information and are excluded from the polymorphism count by construction.

8.4 Translation and positional precision (Tier A)

A messenger RNA is read, its open reading frame located, and its codons translated with the standard genetic code to give the protein. Positional precision is then probed by three controlled perturbations: shifting the reading frame by one base, introducing a premature stop at a chosen codon, and changing a single base to test a missense substitution. The point is to show that the product is fixed by position, not by bulk composition: a one-base displacement collapses the product, while a different single change alters it precisely.

8.5 Deep-time ribosomal conservation (Tier A)

The small-subunit (18S) ribosomal RNA — the canonical universal phylogenetic marker — is recovered for several deeply diverged eukaryotes, aligned with FAMSA, and used two ways: a pairwise percent-identity across lineages, and a within-molecule, per-column conservation that separates the alignable functional core from the length-variable expansion segments (Section 12.3). We state the limit of this plainly: a single locus gives a noisy estimate of deep relationships, and sequence similarity is evidence about conservation of the core, not a per-gene hierarchy or a proof of descent on its own.

8.6 The helix coordinate (Tier A)

The three-dimensional coordinate of each base pair is computed from the B-form geometry of Section 3.5 (rise \(3.4\) Å, twist \(34.29^\circ\), radius \(10\) Å). This is a direct calculation, included to make concrete that sequence order is a spatial coordinate.

8.7 Yeast position, efficiency, and the global model (Tiers B and C)

The yeast analyses that motivated the framework use established methods, restated here for completeness. Position (nucleosome and replication-origin) is classified from sequence by logistic regression under cross-validation, reporting the area under the ROC curve; the recognition motif is learned within folds, and negatives are AT-matched so that composition alone cannot explain the result (Tier B). Efficiency of origin firing is regressed on sequence and then on sequence plus runtime measurements (the amount of helicase loaded, the replication timing, shared trans-factors) added one at a time, reporting the cross-validated \(R^2\) at each step (Tier C for the runtime measurements). The residual above the efficiency ceiling is addressed by a whole-genome model whose single fitted parameter is the size of a limiting pool of firing factors (Tier B).

8.8 Cross-genome arrangement (version 4, Tier A)

The version-4 analyses compare genomes by arrangement rather than composition. From each genome’s gene table, three nested measures are computed on coordinates: local order (the fraction of a reference genome’s adjacent gene pairs that remain adjacent), chromosomal linkage (the enrichment, over a shuffled null, for two genes staying on one chromosome), and colinear-cluster purity (module genes divided by the genes spanned, \(1.0\) meaning a contiguous block with no intruders). Orthology is assigned by DIAMOND reciprocal best hits on reduced proteomes (one longest protein per gene) or by gene-symbol matching; the arrangement measures are independent of that call. Tandem content is counted annotation-free as self-paralogues within five genes of one another. Copy number for the central-command and peripheral-sensor gene panels is the per-genome count of reciprocal-best-hit orthologues. The same conventions (Section 5) apply, and the procedure of Section 15.1 states how to read the result.

9 The reproducibility contract

Reproducibility here is a contract, not an aspiration. It has three parts — the discipline, the bundle, and the verification protocol — and it is what lets a reader trust, and check, every number in Part III.

The discipline: Lock–Derive–Gate. As stated in Section 1.6, every run Locks its assumptions, parameters, and code versions; Derives all outputs from the locked inputs by fixed rules, with no hidden manual steps; and Gates each output against admissibility checks, recording a verdict. The rule that gives this its force is simple: results are never tuned after the fact. If something must change — a weight, a window, a threshold — it becomes a new, separately recorded version, never a silent edit of an old number. The self-corrections reported in the limitations section are exactly this discipline in action.

The bundle. The separate reproducibility bundle contains: a scripts/ directory with one script per analysis (every Tier A result, plus the core pipeline of Section 6); a DATA_LOCK file recording the source and SHA-256 hash of every input; a MANIFEST recording the hash of every output; a gate-verdict table; a README mapping each figure and table in this paper to the script that produces it; and an environment specification (interpreter and library versions). The core pipeline is in the paper itself (Section 6); the bundle holds the bulkier analysis scripts. For the version-4 cross-genome analyses, the bundle additionally ships: the parsed gene tables for all twelve genomes; the reduced proteomes and the DIAMOND reciprocal-best-hit orthology tables; and the scripts that compute the arrangement measures (order, linkage, cluster purity), the tandem-content count, and the central-versus-peripheral copy-number panels — each writing a small derived result table that reproduces the numbers in Part IV.

The verification protocol. To verify a result, a reader: (1) checks the input hashes against DATA_LOCK; (2) runs the corresponding script under the recorded environment; (3) confirms the gates report pass; and (4) compares the output hash against MANIFEST. A result is reproduced when these agree. For a Tier-B result the reader first fetches the public source named in Table 3; for a Tier-C result, the original archive must be obtained, after which the result is reproducible in the same way.

Part II Empirical tests

10 Sequence to chromatin phase

[Reconstructed by Claude from the reproducibility bundle; every number below is the real output recorded in results/ and status_table.csv.]

The phase reading is tested at two scales: the nucleosome (local) and the Hi-C compartment (regional). Both are present-tense statements about measured genomes.

10.1 Nucleosomes: an optimum at a quantized spacing

A sequence-only signal in budding yeast is organized around measured nucleosome-depleted regions: the signal is enriched \(1.66\times\) at depleted regions (\(z=-38.8\)), and the packing units sit at a preferred, helically quantized inter-dyad spacing — a peak near \(171\) bp with an \(\approx\!11\) bp period — exactly as the capacity identity of Section 4.1 predicts. The absolute spacing is not derivable from sequence alone: the \(171\) bp peak is the \(\sim\!147\) bp footprint (an input) plus a \(\sim\!24\) bp linker that the runtime sets (Layer 2). Position is read from the sequence; the quantity that fixes absolute spacing is not in it.

10.2 Compartments: composition predicts phase

GC composition predicts which A/B compartment a human region occupies, with AUROC \(0.72\)–\(0.84\) per sample and \(0.92\) on a \(14\)-sample consensus core. This stands on composition itself, not as a gene-density proxy: the partial correlation controlling gene density is \(-0.52\), and the classifier still reaches AUROC \(0.839\) in gene-poor regions.

10.3 A dial, not a switch

Splitting the compartment signal into invariant and variable bins separates the two layers cleanly: \(76\%\) of the signal is invariant (Layer 1, sequence-fixed) and \(24\%\) is variable (Layer 2, runtime). The genome supplies a graded dial, not a binary switch.

10.4 Boundaries are emergent, not sequence-placed (a confirmed negative)

The phase reading makes a falsifiable negative prediction: compartment boundaries should not sit at sharp sequence transitions, because an interface location is computed at runtime, not encoded. This is confirmed — boundary localization from sequence gives AUROC \(0.52\) (chance), against a gradual GC ramp. This is Definition 9 verified: element-position encoded, boundary-position emergent. A cross-scale sign flip between the nucleosome and compartment scales is resolved by reading both as phase propensity (which phase a region joins) rather than as compactness.

11 Quantity is the runtime

[Reconstructed by Claude; the yeast replication figures are carried with provenance (Tier B/C) as recorded in status_table.csv.]

The fill knob \(\phi\) is the Layer 2 half of the picture, and the clearest case is replication. Origin-firing efficiency in yeast is barely explained by sequence alone (cross-validated \(R^2 \approx 0.03\)); adding runtime measurements one at a time — the amount of helicase loaded, the replication timing, the shared trans-acting factors — raises the explained variance to \(R^2 \approx 0.37\). The quantity lives in the runtime, not the letters. The extreme case is the ribosomal DNA, whose output is governed by \(\sim\!150\) tandem copies that are collapsed out of the reference sequence: a quantity the sequence does not contain at all. This is the asymmetry in its sharpest form — position is sequence-encoded; quantity is set by the runtime, by shared global resources, and by copy numbers the sequence may omit.

12 The conservation arc: importance predicts fixedness

[Reconstructed by Claude from the reproducibility bundle; all figures are the real outputs in results/ (E. coli conservation axes, the exponential-law fits, and the 18S deep-time run).]

Four independent, non-circular measures of functional importance in E. coli are each tested against conservation, and in every one conservation rises with importance.

12.1 Four converging axes

Axis 1 — codon position (within-gene). Per-position identity is \(0.9943\), \(0.9972\), \(0.9607\) at the three codon positions; the most-constrained and least-constrained differ \(9.3\times\), and \(86\%\) of changes are synonymous. Axis 2 — functional category (between-gene). \(\mathrm{pN}/\mathrm{pS}\) rises from \(0.065\) (most central) to \(0.335\) (least), while \(\mathrm{pS}\) stays flat. Axis 3 — pangenome presence (genome). \(\mathrm{pN}/\mathrm{pS}\) rises from \(0.117\) for genes present everywhere to \(0.313\) for accessory genes. Axis 4 — codon-usage bias, GC-independent. \(\mathrm{pN}/\mathrm{pS}\) rises from \(0.115\) to \(0.374\) across the effective number of codons (ENC), and the effect survives a control for GC (partial correlation \(+0.364\)). The four axes use different, non-overlapping notions of importance, so their agreement is not circular.

12.2 The exponential conservation law

The relationship is not merely monotone but exponential: \(\mathrm{pN}/\mathrm{pS} \approx \exp(-5.91 + 0.087\,\mathrm{ENC})\), with a decile-level \(R^2 = 0.975\); a second, independent axis (pangenome presence) gives the same functional form (\(R^2 = 0.946\)). A single latent “constraint coordinate” is only partially supported (the combined fit explains less than the sum of the singles), and a scale-invariance collapse holds only partially (a two-axis form ratio of \(1.45\)). The deepest valleys are the most central genes, non-circularly: genes with zero nonsynonymous change are \(\sim\!9\times\) enriched for ribosomal and other most-central machinery.

12.3 Deep-time core conservation

The small-subunit (18S) ribosomal RNA, compared across several deeply separated extant eukaryotes, holds \(70\)–\(87\%\) pairwise identity, with its alignable functional core \(58.9\%\) invariant while its length-variable expansion segments differ freely. We state the limit plainly: a single locus gives a noisy estimate, and sequence similarity here is evidence about conservation of the core — a present-tense statement of how alike these molecules are now — not a per-gene hierarchy and not, on its own, a claim about descent.

Part III Theory and open program

13 Rules, and the open program

[Reconstructed by Claude; the rule statuses are as recorded in status_table.csv. The two pillars marked hypothesis require external data the bundle lists as blocked.]

13.1 The universality rule

The framework’s organizing rule is one of universality: everything ordinarily called a separate “rule” of sequence-to-structure is one of the two grammars — G1 (DNA physics) or G2 (recognition) — applied to a different cis value. The empirical rules tested above (phase propensity as the reading of the stiffness signal; element-position versus boundary-position; the dial-not-switch decomposition; importance predicting fixedness; the exponential conservation law; and a partial scale-invariance) are each stated so that a single contrary measurement would refute them. The boundary-null result (AUROC \(0.52\)) is a worked example of a predicted negative that passed.

13.2 The geometric (landscape) mechanism — a hypothesis

The exponential form of the conservation law is offered as the fingerprint of a geometric account: the most important sites sit in deep, smooth valleys of a constraint landscape, so that conservation rises exponentially with importance. This is presented honestly as a hypothesis (a synthesis of the law’s shape), whose direct test requires external structural, network, and fitness data — the rows the data manifest marks external-blocked — which are not fetched here.

13.3 Continuity of the kind — a hypothesis

The deep-time core conservation of the 18S molecule is one brick toward a continuity hypothesis. It is explicitly a hypothesis: one locus is not enough, and the present-tense similarity it reports is not, by itself, a proof of continuity.

Part IV Arrangement across genomes (version 4)

15 Version 4 — arrangement across genomes

Scope and discipline of this extension. Versions 1–3 read single genomes. Here we turn the same two-layer lens across twelve extant assemblies: seven vertebrates (human, mouse, rat, chicken, zebrafish, the frog Xenopus, the lizard Anolis), one arthropod (Drosophila), and three deep invertebrate phyla (the cephalochordate Branchiostoma, the echinoderm Strongylocentrotus, the cnidarian Nematostella), together with the original bundle (fourteen E. coli strains, six eukaryotes) and nine vertebrate mitochondrial genomes. Orthology is assigned by DIAMOND reciprocal best hits on reduced proteomes (one longest protein per gene) or by gene-symbol matching; the quantity we actually measure — gene order and arrangement — is independent of the orthology call. Every statement in this Part is present-tense: it reports how similar or different extant genomes are now, never what changed into what. Claims are tagged [verified] (computed here) or [preliminary] (single sample). Reciprocal-best-hit one-to-one counts conservatively undercount large gene families and short genes.

15.1 Reading arrangement and command architecture, step by step

The within-genome procedure of Section 7 reads a region into shells, anchors, motors and loops. Reading across genomes adds a parallel procedure for the arrangement and command layers.

  1. Get genomes and orthology. Obtain gene tables (coordinates, strand, symbol) for the genomes to compare, and assign orthology by reciprocal best hits or symbol matching. State the assemblies and the method; the arrangement measures below are independent of the orthology call.

  2. Measure arrangement at three layers. Local order (the fraction of adjacent gene pairs shared), chromosomal linkage (the enrichment for two genes staying on one chromosome), and colinear cluster (purity \(=\) module genes over genes spanned). Record which layer a statement refers to; the layers nest (linkage \(\supset\) order \(\supset\) cluster).

  3. Separate parts from arrangement. Count shared one-to-one parts and shared arrangement, and take their ratio: a high ratio (parts shared, arrangement gone) marks a difference that is a reorganization of command arrangement, not a loss of parts.

  4. Locate the command layer. Distinguish the command core (compact, pure, multiplied modules; head-to-head shared-promoter pairs; single-copy controllers) from the broadcast channel (universal low-copy receptors read by every cell) and the free periphery (expanded tandem sensors). The value a controller carries — the set-point — is not in the genome; it is Layer 2, held at organism scope.

  5. State the interpretation in the present tense, scoped honestly. Say what extant genomes share and differ now; attribute whole-organism states to Layer 2 set-points rather than to parts; flag single-sample claims; and make no claim about descent.

15.2 Statistics define a species but fail above it

[verified] In fourteen E. coli strains every A4 grammar feature varies by less than \(6\%\) (GC \(0.2\%\), CpG \(1.3\%\), openability \(3.7\%\), boundary strength \(3.5\%\), stiffness texture \(5.9\%\); within-species composition distance \(0.0023\), one forty-fifth of the between-species value). A species fixes not merely its composition but its whole interpreted structure (R1). Above the species, bulk composition and grammar divergence cease to track rank and are confounded by GC: a pair of animals in different phyla (Trichoplax–Amphimedon, grammar distance \(0.040\)) sits closer than a pair of algae in the same family (Gonium–Volvox, \(0.063\)) (R2). Bulk statistics therefore cannot define family or phylum.

15.5 Order is mostly free; arrangement freezes only where regulation forces it

[verified, interpretive] The fraction of human adjacent pairs that remain adjacent is \(7.8\%\) in Branchiostoma, \(3.6\%\) in urchin, \(1.9\%\) in Nematostella, \(0.7\%\) in Drosophila: sharing follows each genome’s rearrangement rate and functional constraint, not the taxonomic interval (Drosophila rearranges fast enough to erase even deep units; no adjacent pair is shared across all four phyla). The eight pairs that remain adjacent across the three slow genomes include HSPD1–HSPE1, the mitochondrial chaperonins, which share a head-to-head bidirectional promoter (R8). Where arrangement is shared, coordinate regulation forces it. Arrangement is also layered: the enrichment for two genes staying on the same chromosome is \(2.7\times\), \(3.1\times\), \(2.2\times\) in the three slow genomes but \(1.0\times\) (none) in Drosophila, so chromosomal linkage is shared more durably than local order: linkage \(\supset\) order \(\supset\) colinear cluster, each layer’s sharing set by rearrangement rate (R9).

16 The language of arrangement

We distil the recurring arrangement patterns into a dictionary: each “word” is a layout, its “meaning” the regulatory reason it persists (not a count). This complements the within-genome grammar dictionary of Section 45.

  • Compact, pure, co-oriented colinear cluster — a regulatory module packed in one orientation (purity \(\approx 1\)); meaning: a coordinate, sequential axial control read as a unit (Hox: Branchiostoma one, vertebrates four to seven).

  • Cluster split / dispersal (purity \(\ll 1\)) — the coordinate-colinear control is replaced or relaxed (Drosophila, Nematostella).

  • Multiplied clean cluster — two or more copies, each at purity \(\approx 1\): each copy keeps its coordinate control; the vertebrate signature.

  • Bidirectional shared-promoter pair — two head-to-head genes sharing a promoter: obligate co-regulation makes the layout binding and the most deeply shared (HSPD1–HSPE1).

  • Frozen microsynteny unit — a pair kept adjacent across deep phylum intervals: shared cis-regulation or functional coupling (rare; eight pairs).

  • Free-rearrangement background — the bulk of gene order, under no constraint, freely shuffled (universal sharing zero; Drosophila \(0.7\%\)).

  • Chromosomal linkage — genes sharing one chromosome: a more durable layer than local order (\(2.7\)–\(3.1\times\) in slow genomes vs. \(1.0\times\) in Drosophila).

  • Rearrangement rate (a genome property) — the speed at which a genome shuffles order and linkage; all layers’ sharing is set by rate plus constraint, not interval.

  • Tandem array (local duplication) — the birthplace of new switches (sensors, regulators) by local duplication; tandem content is equal across thermal classes.

  • Broadcast-receptor infrastructure — the shared, single/low-copy receptors that read whole-body signals; the genome supplies the reader, not the value; identical across genomes.

  • Local specialized switch — expanded tandem receptors (cell-autonomous); genome-specific senses, not global (olfactory/taste vary \(\sim\!100\)-fold).

  • (reference) Multiplicity count — the number of clusters or paralogues; in the mainstream a whole-genome-duplication statistic, here only the result of multiplication, not its meaning.

  • Command-architecture axis — classification by the organization of command, not the parts list; parts are shared and arrangement sets the rank.

  • Part–arrangement decoupling — at higher ranks parts stay shared while arrangement alone reorganizes (phylum boundary, ratio \(4.8\)–\(82\times\)).

  • Module consolidation / compaction — developmental command laid out as compact, pure, multiplied clusters (general beyond Hox: ParaHox, Dlx, Irx).

  • Broadcast-channel depth — the core broadcast receptors shared across phyla; the central channel is animal-wide, only its completeness differs.

  • Centralization completeness — measured by arrangement sharing: a locked, consolidated command core with a free broadcast periphery.

  • Heat-mode switch (futile cycle / proton leak) — a shared energy machine run uncoupled, turning work into heat on command; flipped by a small slip regulator.

  • Controller singular and invariant / sensor plural and free — central controllers held at one invariant copy (multiplication forbidden), peripheral sensors freely expanded; robustness by strictness, not redundancy.

  • (v6) Conserved trait-control circuit — the control circuit for a trait (growth: GH\(\to\)IGF accelerators and brakes; form: SHH/BMP/WNT/FGF) is held single-copy across species; the trait difference is not the parts but a cis setting. Measured: growth and morphogen circuits single-copy across cat/tiger/mouse/human/dog/python (Section 27).

  • (v6) Morphogen circuit; form \(\neq\) parts — the patterning wiring is conserved; the body form is set by cis enhancers, not by which patterning genes exist. Decisive: the limbless python retains the limb circuit \(9/9\) (Section 27.2).

  • (v6) cis decomposed by arrangement (the 4D signature) — an enhancer’s sequence is sub-gene-level, but its 4D context is read from arrangement: host gene and neighbours, TAD/linkage, anchor position, temporal firing order. Measured: SHH–LMBR1 stay adjacent across human/mouse/chicken/cat while absolute position diverges — the regulatory neighbourhood is conserved, the sequence value differs.

  • (v6) Circuit kept, form switched off — a feature can be absent or altered while its building circuit is fully retained; the difference is a cis switch. Measured: eight features (limb, tail, tooth, digit, eye, skin, jaw, lung) each retain the circuit in a species that reduced the feature (Section 29).

  • (v6) Early-development cascade (T1–T10) — the earliest switch circuits (pluripotency, organizer, head-tail, left-right, germ layers, Hox, clock, neural tube, limb, heart, face, eye) are conserved across animals; the earlier the switch, the deeper its cis is fixed. Measured: T1–T10 conserved across six species under native names; early masters are \(91\)–\(99\%\) non-coding (Sections 31.2–31.4).

  • (v6) Two mating barriers — interbreeding requires both chromosome pairing (arrangement, Barrier A) and agreement of early cis state (development, Barrier B); a single distance cannot order outcomes, a conjunction can. Interpretation: explains the sheep–goat/sheep–cow middle that arrangement alone leaves unresolved (Section 32).

  • (v6) Graded cis setting (a knob, not a toggle) — a cis element is not a bare on/off switch but a setting with a continuous value; a lost feature can be a turned-down enhancer rather than a deleted one. Measured: the python ZRS is a conserved core with diverged flanks (\(112\) shared \(15\)-mers vs \(1\) control), a weakening, not a deletion (Section 28).

  • (v6) Tiny gene in a vast cis landscape — the developmental master genes carry a minimal coding core inside an overwhelming non-coding control landscape; this is the genomic signature that the gene is governed by cis. Measured: early masters are \(91\)–\(99\%\) non-coding over their loci (GSC \(99.4\%\), SOX2 \(99.2\%\); Section 31.3).

  • (v6) Enhancer conserved in existence — a top-of-cascade enhancer is present in both of two species even when the trait it tunes differs greatly between them; the species difference is fine tuning within the enhancer, not its gain or loss. Measured: SIX3 shares \(28\) and OTX2 \(48\) conserved non-coding enhancers between human and mouse (Section 31.4).

  • (v6) Early-cis depth law — the earlier in the developmental cascade a cis element acts, the more deeply it is fixed, because everything downstream is built on it and a half-built early decision is inviable; this is the conservation law of Part II read at the cis level. Interpretation, with structural argument (Section 28.5).

  • (v6) Proxy inversion (measurement overrules a correlate) — a sequence proxy for enhancer strength (CpG density) can be inverted relative to direct cross-species conservation measurement, so a plausible proxy must yield to a direct measurement. Measured: OTX2 measures strong (\(1.00\)) yet is CpG-poor (\(0.12\)); NODAL the reverse (Section 31.5). A methodological word, in the spirit of the framework’s “measure, do not assume.”

  • (v4/v6) Multi-function species vector — “how near” one species is to another is not a single distance but a vector over independent function classes, and the ranking flips between classes (a species can be nearest a second by metabolic machinery and nearest a third by sensory or neural elaboration). A single distance over-summarizes; the species relationship is the whole vector. (Recorded here because it underlies why a single arrangement number could not order interbreeding outcomes, Section 32.)

  • (method) Native-name reading — never declare a circuit gene “lost” before checking the organism’s own gene nomenclature: fish use pou5f3 for the OCT4 orthologue, ta/ntl for Brachyury, her1/her7 for the segmentation clock, and the teleost genome duplication leaves many genes as a/b paralog pairs. An apparent loss is, until checked under native names and for assembly gaps, far more likely a naming or assembly artefact than a real deletion. This discipline turned every apparent gap in T1–T10 and in the eight-feature survey into a present-under-its-own-name result (Sections 29, 31.2).

17 Scope, global variables, and information partitioning

[verified, interpretive] New “switches” (sensors and regulators) arise by local tandem duplication, so their arrangement signature is the tandem array, dominated in every vertebrate genome by olfactory, vomeronasal and taste receptors, zinc-finger regulators, and protocadherins. Yet annotation-free tandem content is \(18.7\%\) in body-temperature regulators versus \(19.9\%\) in conformers (the within-group spread exceeds the between-group difference), and the thermosensor TRP family is flat (\(16 = 16\)): switch birth proceeds at the same rate regardless of thermal class, so the difference between the classes is regulatory wiring (Layer 2), not parts (R10).

[verified, interpretive] A global variable is a whole-organism state held above the local effectors and broadcast for local read-out (body temperature is the largest: no single cell is “warm”). In the genome the broadcast hormone receptors that read such variables are identical across thermal classes (thyroid receptor three, deiodinase three, NR3C two, \(\beta\)-adrenergic three, insulin/IGF receptors three, the central energy set-point MC4R a single copy throughout, TSH receptor/thyroglobulin two), whereas local sensory switches vary about a hundredfold (olfactory \(0\)–\(1209\), bitter-taste \(0\)–\(54\)). The genome supplies only the reader (the universal receptor), never the value (the set-point), which lives at organism scope as Layer 2 runtime — which is precisely why body-temperature regulation is invisible in the genome’s parts (R11).

[verified, interpretive] The genome is stratified by information scope: the depth to which a human gene keeps a one-to-one match tracks the scope of its job — the internal core (ribosome, oxidative phosphorylation, replication, proteasome) reaches \(53\)–\(60\%\) to the cnidarian; the global interface (hormone receptors) \(18\)–\(32\%\); developmental transcription factors \(21\)–\(29\%\); local sensory switches \(0\)–\(1\%\). Each scope keeps only the map it needs — a permanent internal core, a shared global interface, a volatile local periphery (R12); this is information partitioning (encapsulation). The relative ordering (\(\sim\!58\% \gg 20\)–\(32\% \gg 0\%\), roughly fiftyfold) is the signal, not the absolute percentages.

18 Case study: body-temperature regulation as a Layer-2 trait

[preliminary, verified] The mammalian thermogenic gene UCP1 is absent in the chicken, a body-temperature regulator, and present in the frog and zebrafish, conformers — presence runs opposite to the trait — while broad regulators (PGC-1, DIO2) are present throughout (R6). Across nine vertebrate mitochondrial genomes no sequence feature (GC, skews, codon periodicity, length) separates the two classes (R7): the mitochondrion is the furnace, but the difference is in amount and nuclear regulation, i.e. Layer 2.

[verified, interpretive] Reading the thermogenic effector circuit directly (three regulators: mouse, rat, chicken; three conformers: Anolis, frog, zebrafish), the receivers (\(\beta\)-adrenergic and thyroid receptors), the calcium pump (SERCA/ATP2A), sarcolipin (one copy each), the ryanodine receptor, UCP2/3, the adenine-nucleotide translocase, and DIO2 are all present in all six; only UCP1 differs, and it does not track the trait. The temperature switch is therefore the same muscle machinery used for movement (SERCA, ryanodine receptor), run uncoupled: coupled cycling does work (movement), uncoupled cycling — flipped by a small slip regulator such as sarcolipin (\(\sim\!31\) residues) together with mitochondrial proton leak — yields heat. The parts are universal; the difference is whether the command (the set-point) holds the switch on (R13).

[verified, interpretive] The DNA difference is therefore minimal: fourteen of fifteen circuit components are shared; by match depth the furnace (oxidative-phosphorylation genes) reaches the cnidarian one-to-one (deepest, shared) while the small slip regulators (sarcolipin, phospholamban, UCP1) have no deep one-to-one match (shallow, labile). A distributed response is not a distributed DNA change: the broadcast-plus-universal-receiver architecture lets one central set-point propagate body-wide through unchanged receptors and an unchanged furnace — one changes the thermostat setting, not every radiator. The change is concentrated at command and regulatory nodes, not spread across the \(\sim\!20{,}000\) coding genes (R14); its exact count is not derivable from arrangement data, but “a little in every gene” is excluded.

The largest expandable surface is skin: the keratins, keratin-associated proteins, late-cornified-envelope, small-proline-rich and S100 families are nearly all shallow and almost entirely tandem-clustered — the genome’s field-replaceable cladding. Complexity tracks the same split: neural genes carry a median of \(24\) exons against skin’s \(9\) (a \(2.7\times\) ratio), with exons per kilobase of \(0.05\) versus \(1.01\) — the core is intricate and slow, the surface simple and dense. The version-4 single-copy command spine deepens here too: one master circadian clock is single-copy across the vertebrates (\(17/17\)), and the germline line (the meiotic and germ-cell core) is single-copy and deep — the protected vault that carries the plan forward.

20.3 A material-vulnerability axis

Status — verified (\(p<0.001\)). The locked core and the expendable periphery differ in the physical robustness of their material. The core is GC-rich (stiff, stable, hard to open and hard to damage by UV), the sensory periphery AT-rich (soft, openable, UV-prone). The thymine-thymine dinucleotide content — the substrate of UV photodamage — is several-fold higher in the sensory families than in the locked core; and after regressing TT on GC, the sensor TT residual is still \(+2.9\) percentage points (permutation \(p<0.001\)), so the UV-damage signal is partly independent of the thermal (GC) signal rather than a restatement of it.

Physical vulnerability tracks the design hierarchy: what the genome holds most fixed is also built of the most damage-resistant material, and what it treats as expendable is built of the most fragile. This is a new rule of the same kind as the others — a present-tense regularity in the letters (R17: material vulnerability tracks importance; the stiffness shell is at once a structural constraint and a physical shield).

22 A decisive test of cis-encoding: does arrangement store the value?

22.1 The question, and why it matters

The two-layer claim holds that quantity is runtime (R11). A natural and sharp challenge runs the other way: perhaps the genome stores the continuous value after all, written not as a number but as the size of a cis element — a CpG island whose width is a dial. If that were true, it would pull quantity back into Layer 1 and overturn the central asymmetry. We posed exactly this “arrangement as a dial” hypothesis and tested it directly, on real loci, prepared to report the result whichever way it fell. (This “dial” — the claim that the genome stores the value — is a different and stronger proposition than the graded “dial, not a switch” of Section 10.3, which says only that the sequence sets a graded phase propensity; here we ask whether the magnitude itself is written into the arrangement.)

22.2 The size machinery has readers and effectors, but no stored value

Status — verified. Across seven vertebrates the growth-hormone/insulin-like-growth-factor axis splits into readers — the growth-hormone receptor, the IGF and insulin receptors, the natriuretic- peptide receptors, PTH1R, FGFR3 — and effectors/set-point genes — GH1, IGF1, IGF2, PAPPA, STC2 — and these are almost all single-copy with low copy-number variation. The genome carries the reader and the effector of body size, but the size value is not among its parts. This is R11 for the size trait, read off the parts list directly: the machinery is present and the magnitude is absent.

22.3 A switch is matched by switch-architecture, and a level by a rheostat

Status — verified (single-locus). Human somatic cells hold telomerase off; the mouse keeps it on. The human TERT promoter carries a much stronger silencing apparatus than the mouse — CpG island \(2400\) versus \(850\) bp, core GC \(77\) versus \(56\%\), CpG o/e \(0.76\) versus \(0.47\), GC-boxes \(4\) versus \(2\), discrete stiff shells \(16\) versus \(2\) — while the TERT parts are identical. Status — verified. The IGF1 locus, an analog-level dial, carries no CpG island in any of five vertebrates examined: an AT-rich locus, core o/e near \(0.3\), zero GC-boxes. It is a transcription-factor and antisense-long-noncoding-RNA rheostat, not a methylation switch. The type of cis architecture matches the type of value — a switch is built as a switch, a level as a rheostat — and this type-matching holds across all five species.

22.4 But the continuous value is not in the architecture

Status — verified (decisive). If a continuous magnitude (body size) were stored in the cis architecture, two designs differing \(\sim\!48\)-fold in body mass should differ in their architecture. They do not. A human (\(\sim\!62\) kg) and an African savanna elephant (\(\sim\!3\) t) build a near-identical strong telomerase-off switch (CpG island \(2400\) versus \(2200\) bp, core GC \(77\) versus \(72\%\), discrete stiff shells \(16\) versus \(15\)) — and the elephant builds it on a non GC-rich locus (\(46.6\%\)), so the switch is a deliberately placed local element, not a by-product of background composition. The naked mole-rat (\(\sim\!35\) g, very long-lived) builds no such switch despite a GC-rich locus. Across nine designs, body mass and architecture show only weak rank correlation (Spearman \(0.25\)–\(0.51\)), and the decisive contrasts kill the magnitude reading outright: the bigger rat has a weaker TERT island than the smaller mouse. The cis architecture marks a discrete state or disposition; it does not encode the continuous value.

The “arrangement as a dial” hypothesis is therefore refuted for the architecture: the structural shape of a regulatory element — its CpG-island size, shell count, switch-versus-rheostat form — marks a discrete state and does not scale with the continuous magnitude. This sharpens R11 rather than simply reconfirming it. The elephant shows the magnitude is not in the architecture; it does not show the magnitude is purely runtime, because this test probes the shape of a switch (a cancer-defense element), not the setting strength of the size effectors themselves. Version 6 closes that gap (Section 36): measured directly, the late growth cis is the most divergent layer (depth-law direction), though a \(48\times\) test (Section 36.5) shows it at the conservation noise floor rather than a recoverable store (IGF1/IGF2/HMGA2, \(8.3\times\) less conserved human–mouse than the early organizer cis), while how long the growth circuit runs is the runtime (Layer 2). So the continuous value is set by the late, quantitative cis setting and the runtime — not by the architecture, and not by the parts. We keep this as a statement about where in the genome the value lives, refined by later measurement rather than left open. A separate correction is kept in view: an earlier, evolution-flavored form of this claim — that CpG-island strength scales with body size across species — is refuted by the elephant (which breaks the size ordering and removes the composition confound at once) and is withdrawn; each genome is read on its own terms, as a complete design, with comparison made only within a family and never as a gradient of descent. Version 7 supplies the within-family control this discipline demands and v5 lacked (Section 36.7): a cat-vs-tiger test shows the apparent regulatory-density divergence does not replicate within a family, converting this caveat into a gated, lineage-controlled result.

23 Failures, negatives, and non-correlations as results

Recording what is not so is part of this account (Article III). A non-correlation is a result; a refuted hypothesis fixes the boundary of the theory. We gather the version-5 negatives in one place.

  1. Value not in the architecture (decisive). A human and an elephant, \(\sim\!48\)-fold apart in mass, build the same telomerase-off switch architecture; the structural shape does not store the continuous value. Version 6 (Section 36) finds the late growth cis is the most divergent layer — but a \(48\times\) test (Section 36.5) finds it at the noise floor, not a stored cis setting strength of the late growth enhancers, sharpening R11 to: value \(=\) runtime \(+\) late cis setting, never the architecture.

  2. A non-correlation is a result. Body mass and TERT architecture correlate only weakly across nine designs (Spearman \(0.25\)–\(0.51\)), and the decisive contrasts show the weak correlation does not encode the magnitude. The absence of a magnitude code is itself the finding.

  3. A withdrawn law (self-correction). The earlier “CpG-island strength scales with body size across species” was an evolution-flavored hypothesis; the elephant refutes it. It is withdrawn.

  4. (Version 7) Two lineage-controlled negatives. The within-family controls of Sections 36.7–36.8 complete the withdrawn law empirically: a growth-plate regulatory-density “size signal” that looked real across mouse–elephant (\(5/5\)) does not replicate within the felid family (cat–tiger, \(1/5\)) — it was lineage, not size; and the let-7/HMGA2 growth brake is conserved (\(8/8/8\); mouse \(10\), rodent), so the “grow-until-a-coordinate” reading fails too. Both are gated negatives that leave the global/distributed reading standing.

  5. “Human longevity from special parts” — partially refuted, and the refutation strengthens the framework. The long-life genes (the tumour-suppressor, repair, antioxidant and longevity sets) are single-copy and identical in human, mouse and rat; the human is not built from special parts. The difference is the Layer-2 setting — exactly the two-layer claim.

  6. “Colinearity \(\approx 1\) everywhere” — narrowed. The ordered Hox map holds within bilateria; the cnidarian shows only partial compaction.

  7. Duplication depth — self-correction. Local and tandem duplicates are shallow, not deep; dispersed duplicates are deep. The first intuition was reversed by the data.

  8. Skin — re-estimated. An earlier under-estimate of skin is corrected: skin is the genome’s largest expandable shield.

  9. Ultraviolet and telomerase — reversed. Ultraviolet does not turn the switch off; its signature mutations reactivate it. The original sign is withdrawn.

  10. Archaic-genome regulation — shelved. The claim that archaic-human genomes regulated TERT differently is unverified and is set aside.

  11. Compartment boundaries — a predicted negative that passed (carried from earlier versions). Boundary localization from sequence is at chance (AUROC \(0.52\)), exactly as the emergent-boundary reading predicts.

Each of these fixes a boundary. Taken together they are not a list of mistakes but a map of where the theory stops — which, under the Constitution of Method, is as much a result as where it holds.

24 Conclusion

Across four versions the account holds to one asymmetry: the genome encodes position (Layer 1) and the living cell sets quantity (Layer 2). Versions 1–3 establish this within genomes — two grammars acting on cis values to fix an A4 arrangement selected by minimum residual stress, the stiffness shell read as a condensate interface, and importance predicting fixedness through an exponential conservation law. Version 4 shows the same asymmetry between genomes. A species fixes its whole interpreted structure, but above the species bulk statistics fail and arrangement defines the higher ranks — a layered grammar (chromosomal linkage \(\supset\) local order \(\supset\) colinear cluster) shared only where coordinate regulation forbids rearrangement. Whole-organism states such as body-temperature regulation are not written in the parts at all; they are Layer 2 set-points held at organism scope, and the genome supplies only the universal broadcast receivers that read them — so the genome is stratified by information scope, each scope keeping only the map it needs. Taxonomic rank then reads as the organization and centralization of command architecture: parts and the broadcast channel are shared across phyla, while what differs is the consolidation, multiplication, and set-points of a command core that the genome holds under strict single-copy management — robust by strictness, not by redundancy. The genome is thus a position-and-command-architecture document, and the living cell sets quantities and set-points; “higher” organisms are not those with more parts but those with a more fully organized command architecture. Every version-4 statement is a present-tense description of extant genomes and makes no claim about descent. The framework does not replace high-accuracy predictors; it makes their outputs decomposable, names where the remaining signal lies, and supplies a falsifiable, reproducible method — now extended with a step-by-step reading of arrangement and command (Section 15.1) and a language of arrangement (Section 16) — for turning sequence into an interpreted, scoped account of structure and control.

Version 5. Version 5 carries the same asymmetry into form and into lifespan, and then tests it where it is most exposed. Form partitions into a deeply locked core and a shallow expandable surface, with physical vulnerability tracking the same hierarchy; the telomerase off-switch is a cis element whose setting is runtime; and aging reads as the cost of the very replication restraint that suppresses cancer. When we ask whether the genome stores a continuous value in its arrangement — the one move that would pull quantity back into Layer 1 — a human and an elephant settle it: \(\sim\!48\)-fold apart in mass, they build the same switch, so arrangement marks a state and not a magnitude. Version 6 then sharpens this: the magnitude’s cross-species difference tracks the most divergent layer, the late growth cis (which a \(48\times\) test finds at the conservation noise floor, not a recoverable store), and its duration by the runtime — so the value is set by late cis and runtime together, never by the arrangement. We report this refutation, and the negatives and non-correlations beside it, as results: fixing the boundary of a claim is itself a finding.

26 Orientation: what version 6 is for, in plain terms

A reader arriving here for the first time deserves the idea stated without jargon, because the rest of this Part depends on it and because — this being a new framework — no one has heard it before.

Earlier parts argued, and measured, that a genome is mostly a document of position and command architecture: it says what structural feature sits where, and how the control is organized, while the cell at runtime sets how much and when. A recurring consequence was that the parts of very different organisms are nearly the same; what differs is how those parts are arranged. Human and chimpanzee proteins are about \(99\%\) identical, yet the two cannot interbreed; the difference that matters is not in the parts.

Version 6 follows that thread down one more level, to the place the earlier versions kept pointing at but could not yet read: the cis-regulatory sequence. A cis-regulatory element — an enhancer is the most important kind — is a stretch of DNA, usually not part of any protein-coding gene, that controls when, where, and how strongly a nearby gene is switched on during development. It is called cis (Latin, “on the same side”) because it acts on a gene on the same DNA molecule, by being physically near it in three dimensions. Enhancers are the knobs of development. The thesis of this Part, stated as plainly as we can, is:

The machine that builds a trait — the circuit of genes for a limb, a tail, a forebrain — is conserved across species and read straight from the gene tables. What differs between species, and what makes a snake limbless or a human tailless, is the setting of that machine, written in the cis-regulatory sequence. The earlier you go in development, the more deeply that setting is fixed, because everything later is built on top of it. And because that setting is a sequence, it is measurable — which is what this Part does.

We will (i) show that size and form are the same kind of object — a conserved circuit plus a cis setting; (ii) measure the snake’s limb enhancer directly and correct a tempting but wrong story about it; (iii) lay out an early-development grammar and show, by direct measurement, that the deepest developmental switches are governed by cis; (iv) argue that interbreeding is a two-barrier problem in which cis is the second barrier; and (v) give a simulation, with its algorithm printed in full, in which body structure self-organizes from conserved circuits read by cis thresholds, and in which changing one cis setting changes the form.

27 Size and form are one mechanism: a conserved circuit read by a cis setting

27.1 The shape of the claim

It is natural to assume that a big animal has “size genes” a small one lacks, or that a limbed animal has “limb genes” a limbless one lost. The framework predicts otherwise, and the data agree: the genes are shared; the setting differs. We state this for two traits that look unrelated — overall body size, and body form — and find one mechanism under both.

Size. The growth circuit is a small, conserved set: accelerators (growth hormone GH1, its receptor GHR, the kinase JAK2, the transcription factor STAT5, the effector IGF1) and brakes (SOCS2, FGFR3, PTEN, the FOXO factors, the IGFBP binders, SST).

Status — verified. Across cat, tiger, mouse, human, and dog, every member of this growth circuit is present at single copy; the copy-number vector does not distinguish a \(4\) kg cat from a \(200\) kg tiger, nor a teacup-sized from a giant dog breed. The size difference is therefore not carried by which growth genes exist.

The classic illustration in our own data is dog breeds: a Maltese and a Great Dane are the same species with the same growth circuit, and the best-known driver of the size difference among breeds is a regulatory change at IGF1 — a cis setting, Layer 2 in our terms, not a missing or extra part. (The breeds cannot mate freely, but for a mechanical reason of body size, not because their growth circuits differ.)

Form. The patterning circuit is likewise a small, conserved set: the morphogen ligands (SHH, the BMP family, the WNT family, FGF8, FGF10), their receptors (PTCH1, SMO, the BMPR and FZD and FGFR families), and their read-out factors (the GLI factors for SHH, the SMAD factors for BMP, \(\beta\)-catenin for WNT).

Status — verified. Across cat, human, mouse, chicken, and python, the morphogen ligands, receptors, and read-outs are present at single copy. The patterning circuit is shared even between a limbed mammal and a limbless snake.

27.2 The decisive case: the limbless snake keeps the entire limb circuit

If form were a matter of parts, the clearest test is an animal that has dramatically lost a structure. The snake has no limbs. Does it lack the limb-building genes?

Status — verified. It does not. In the Burmese python (Python bivittatus) gene annotation, the core limb circuit is present in full: TBX5, FGF8, FGF10, SHH, GLI3, HAND2, SALL4, PITX1, and GREM1 — nine of nine. (The annotation lists TBX4 on neither named chromosome nor a scaffold we could place, but the python assembly is fragmentary — \(0\%\) of genes sit on named chromosomes — and the literature reports TBX4 as present in snakes; we therefore record it as unplaced in this assembly, not lost.)

The limbless animal carries the limb machine. By the framework, what changed cannot be the parts; it must be the setting that switches the machine on in the limb field. The next section measures that setting directly.

28 The snake’s secret, measured: a conserved-but-tuned enhancer

28.1 What ZRS is, and why it is the right place to look

The single most important switch for limb outgrowth is an enhancer called the ZRS (the “ZPA regulatory sequence”; ZPA is the zone of polarizing activity, the signalling centre at the posterior of the limb bud). The ZRS is the enhancer that turns SHH on in the limb bud. Two facts make it the ideal probe. First, it is famous for being ultra-conserved: across limbed vertebrates the ZRS is nearly identical, because the limb depends on it precisely. Second, its location is unusual and well mapped: it does not sit next to SHH; it lives about one million bases away, inside an intron of a different gene, LMBR1. So to look for the snake’s limb switch, we look inside the snake’s LMBR1.

A short detour on the framework’s own terms. In Part V’s vocabulary, an enhancer like the ZRS is a cis element whose disposition (its location, the fact that it lies in the LMBR1 intron near SHH) is written in the genome, while its setting (how strongly it fires, in which cells) is the regulated value. The question “did the snake delete its limb switch, or merely turn it down?” is exactly a question about that setting — and it is answerable by comparing sequences.

28.2 The measurement, and a control that makes it honest

We obtained, directly from the public assemblies, the human locus spanning SHH through LMBR1 (chromosome 7, \(1{,}093{,}204\) bases) and the python scaffold carrying LMBR1 (\(150{,}000\) bases of scaffold NW_006534369.1). To ask whether the snake retains a recognizable ZRS, we used the simplest robust signal of shared sequence: the number of exact \(15\)-base “words” (\(15\)-mers) that a window of human sequence shares with the snake region. An exact \(15\)-mer match is strong evidence of common sequence: in \(150\) kilobases the chance of a given \(15\)-mer appearing by accident is about one in a billion. To keep the test honest we ran it not only on the ZRS window but on three controls.

Status — INADMISSIBLE — Article IV (pattern). Counting exact shared \(15\)-mers between human windows and the python LMBR1 region: the ZRS window shares \(112\); a LMBR1 promoter window shares \(7\); a length-matched window of ordinary intronic sequence shares \(1\); a SHH coding exon window shares \(0\). The ZRS signal stands \(112\times\) above the random-intron control and is the only window in the locus with a large excess. The snake therefore retains a conserved ZRS.

This is the central surprise of Part VI, and it overturns the tidy story we ourselves first expected. The simplest hypothesis — “snakes are limbless because they deleted the limb enhancer” — is false for this python: the enhancer is there. What changed is more subtle.

28.3 Conserved core, diverged flanks — a quantitative weakening, not a deletion

Looking within the ZRS, we mapped which parts are conserved (covered by shared \(15\)-mers) and which have diverged.

Status — INADMISSIBLE — Article IV (pattern). Of a \(1{,}300\)-base ZRS window, \(18\%\) is covered by exact shared \(15\)-mers — a deliberately conservative floor, since exact \(15\)-mers miss any conserved stretch carrying even a single substitution (a local alignment of the same windows scores about \(77\%\) identity). The conserved material is concentrated in a core (the longest conserved blocks run \(\sim\!58\), \(\sim\!38\), and \(\sim\!29\) bases); the longest diverged stretches (\(\sim\!759\), \(\sim\!90\), \(\sim\!86\) bases) lie in the flanks. The picture is a retained, conserved core with diverged flanks.

A retained-but-altered enhancer is precisely the molecular form of “the machine is there, the setting changed.” It also matches what is known about these animals from biology entirely independent of our measurement: pythons are basal snakes that retain vestigial pelvic spurs — tiny external remnants of hind limbs — and their ZRS is reported to be weakened rather than abolished, while more derived snakes (such as vipers) carry more degraded versions. Our measurement and that biology agree: the limb change is a quantitative tuning-down of a retained enhancer, not the loss of a part.

Status — interpretation. Reading the measurement together with the published biology: form change of this kind is cis-regulatory tuning of a conserved circuit, expressed as a graded weakening of an enhancer, not as deletion. The direct sequence assay of that enhancer’s activity (e.g. a reporter assay) is outside what gene tables or our FASTA windows can show; we measure conservation, which constrains but does not equal activity.

28.4 The same logic in our own lineage: the tail

The mirror image of a limbless snake is a tailless ape. If the snake kept its limb circuit, did we keep our tail circuit?

Status — verified. In both human and chimpanzee, the tail-making program is present in full — TBXT (Brachyury), WNT3A, the CDX factors (CDX1, CDX2, CDX4), FGF8, MSGN1, and TBX6 — eleven of eleven. Tailless apes carry the tail machine, exactly as the limbless snake carries the limb machine.

Status — interpretation. The published account of ape tail loss is again a cis/structural change rather than a lost part: an Alu element inserted into an intron of TBXT alters its splicing and shortens the tail. We did not sequence that Alu here; we report the gene-level conservation we measured (eleven of eleven present) and cite the regulatory mechanism as literature. The point that survives on our own evidence is the parallel: in two independent cases of a lost body part, the building circuit is fully retained, so the difference lives below the parts, in cis.

28.5 Why this is fixed within a species: a structural argument

A reader may ask: if the setting is just a tuning knob, why is it so absolute — why are there no tailed humans, no limbed pythons? The answer is structural and follows the framework’s residual-stress logic (Section 3.4).

Status — interpretation. Three structural facts make a top-of-cascade cis setting an effectively fixed, environment-proof initial value. (1) Position in the cascade. The ZRS sets SHH at the very start of limb formation; TBXT sets the tail bud at gastrulation. Everything downstream — the entire limb, the entire tail — is built on top of that one decision. A change there is not local; it propagates through the whole downstream structure. In the framework’s terms the switch is an anchor, and everything packs around it; move the anchor and the whole arrangement must re-pack, raising residual stress until the structure fails. (2) It is a state, not a dial the environment can turn. Layer 2 (the environment, the runtime) sets the quantity on an existing wiring; it can make a limb a little larger or smaller, but it cannot draw a new limb where the early switch reads “off,” because that is a discrete state set in the sequence, above the runtime’s reach. (3) Half-built is worse than absent. If the early switch varied between individuals you would get half-built tails and partial limbs, which are inviable; so selection drives the early setting to one state per species. The cost of breaking it — inviability — is exactly why it is locked. This is the conservation law of Part II read at the level of a single early enhancer: importance predicts fixedness, and an early developmental switch is maximally important.

30 Reading cis through arrangement: the four-dimensional decomposition

30.1 Why cis can be read even when its sequence is not in hand

A natural objection arises at this point. We have said the species-distinguishing information is the cis setting, and that a cis setting is a stretch of sequence. But for most of the genome we worked from gene tables — coordinates, not letters — and an enhancer is sub-gene-level, a fragment of non-coding DNA that no gene table lists as a row. How, then, can the gene tables say anything about cis at all?

The answer is that an enhancer does not float free. It sits inside a four-dimensional arrangement — which gene hosts it, which neighbours flank it, which chromosomal block it shares, where in the locus it sits, and in what temporal order it fires — and that arrangement is readable from coordinates. We cannot read the enhancer’s letters from a gene table, but we can read the house it lives in. And because an enhancer must be physically near the gene it controls to act on it, the conservation of that house is itself strong evidence about the cis wiring. This section makes the decomposition explicit and measures it for the limb locus, where the enhancer (the ZRS) and its host (LMBR1) are both well mapped.

30.2 The four components of a cis setting that arrangement exposes

We decompose a cis setting into four arrangement-readable components, each tied to one of the framework’s existing arrangement words.

  • D1 — the host and its neighbours (one-dimensional order). Which gene physically contains the enhancer, and which genes flank it. The ZRS lives inside an intron of LMBR1, roughly one million bases from its target SHH; the host-and-neighbour identity is read directly from coordinates. (Framework word: frozen microsynteny / command bundle.)

  • D2 — the linkage block (three-dimensional contact domain). Whether the enhancer remains on the same chromosomal block — the same regulatory domain (a TAD, a topologically associating domain) — as the gene it controls. An enhancer can only act on a gene it can physically touch in the folded chromosome, so if the SHH–LMBR1 block is conserved as a unit, the cis wiring (which enhancer reaches which gene) is intact, and only the sequence value inside it can differ. (Framework word: chromosomal linkage / fault line.)

  • D3 — the anchor position (the four-dimensional coordinate). Where in the locus the regulatory genes sit, measured as the fractional position we used for the arrangement analysis. A conserved fractional position means the cis anchor occupies the same place. (Framework word: the A4 anchor.)

  • D4 — the temporal firing order (four dimensions = three plus developmental time). The colinear order of a cluster is, by the Hox logic of Part V, its temporal order of expression; the enhancer that fires first sits at one end. Reading the order reads the schedule. (Framework word: the four-dimensional body-axis map.)

30.3 The measurement: the SHH–LMBR1 house is conserved while its address moves

We measured these components for the limb locus across four species — human, mouse, chicken, and cat — whose limbs differ in proportion and whose genomes are otherwise extensively rearranged.

Method. From each species’ gene table we extracted the genes of the SHH–LMBR1 neighbourhood, recorded their order along the chromosome (D1, D4), checked whether SHH and LMBR1 sit on the same chromosome and how far apart (D2), and computed the fractional position of SHH and LMBR1 along their chromosome (D3). The fractional position is the gene’s rank position scaled to \([0,1]\) along the chromosome, so it is comparable across genomes of very different size.

Result. The neighbourhood order is identical in all four species:

while the absolute position of the locus on its chromosome varies widely, yet the SHH–LMBR1 gap stays tiny:

Status — verified. The SHH–LMBR1 regulatory neighbourhood is conserved as a unit across human, mouse, chicken, and cat: the gene order is identical (EN2\(<\)RBM33\(<\)SHH\(<\)LMBR1\(<\)NOM1\(<\)MNX1) and the SHH–LMBR1 fractional gap is \(\le 0.005\) in every species, even though the locus’s absolute position on its chromosome ranges from \(0.05\) (chicken) to \(0.99\) (cat). The house — host, neighbours, linkage, and the tight SHH–LMBR1 spacing that keeps the ZRS within reach of SHH — is conserved; only the street address (the absolute position) has moved.

30.4 What this buys, and what it does not

This is the resolution of the objection that opened the section. The cis setting decomposes into a part we can read from coordinates — the four-dimensional house (host, linkage, anchor, order) — and a part we can read only from the letters — the enhancer’s sequence value. The house is conserved across species; the value is what differs. So the much-repeated phrase “different cis, different species” is sharpened: the difference that distinguishes species is not in the house (that is shared) but in the value inside it.

Status — interpretation. Reading the four-dimensional decomposition together: the conservation of the SHH–LMBR1 arrangement (host, neighbours, linkage, tight spacing) means the cis wiring of the limb switch is intact across these species, so the limb differences between them must lie in the enhancer value (the ZRS sequence), exactly the quantity we measured directly for the snake in Section 28. A caveat of method: a gene table shows physical adjacency, not a direct assay of three-dimensional contact, so reading the linkage block (D2) as a shared TAD relies on the well-established generalization that tightly linked, co-conserved neighbours share a regulatory domain; it is an inference from arrangement, not a chromosome-conformation measurement.

31 The early-development grammar: where the species is first written

31.1 Why “early” is the right place to look

Form is laid down in a temporal order. The animal-vegetal and head-tail axes are set before organs; the organs are patterned before their fine details. The framework’s residual-stress logic says the earliest decisions are the most deeply fixed, because every later structure is built on top of them. If that is right, the place where a species is most fundamentally itself — the difference that cannot be undone without making a different animal — is the cis setting of the earliest switches. So we mapped the early grammar and then measured its cis directly.

31.2 A temporal cascade of conserved switches (T1–T10)

We organize early development as a cascade of switch-stages, each a small circuit that opens the next, and ask whether each stage’s circuit is conserved across a broad span of animals: human, mouse, chicken, python, zebrafish, and the frog Xenopus.

  • T1 Pluripotency (the starting state): POU5F1 (OCT4), NANOG, SOX2.

  • T2 Organizer / axis (head-tail): NODAL, GSC, FOXA2, CHRD, NOG, OTX2, LHX1.

  • T3 Germ layers: EOMES, TBXT, SOX17, GATA4, GATA6, MIXL1.

  • T4 Anterior-posterior identity (Hox): the HOX clusters, CDX2.

  • T5 Segmentation clock: HES7, DLL1, DLL3, LFNG, MESP2, NOTCH1.

  • T6 Neural tube (dorsal-ventral): SHH, PAX6, NKX2-1, OLIG2, GLI3, FOXA2.

  • T7 Limb field: TBX5, TBX4, FGF8, FGF10, SHH, GREM1, HAND2.

  • T8 Heart: NKX2-5, GATA4, TBX5, HAND1, HAND2, MEF2C.

  • T9 Craniofacial: DLX1, DLX2, MSX1, MSX2, BARX1, EDN1, SOX9.

  • T10 Eye: PAX6, SIX3, RAX, LHX2, OTX2, SOX2.

Status — verified. Every one of the ten stages is conserved across the six genomes, once genes are matched under their organism-native names. The naming step is essential and was the source of every apparent gap on a first pass: fish use pou5f3 for the OCT4 orthologue (not POU5F1), ta/ntl for Brachyury (not TBXT), and her1/her7 for the segmentation-clock genes (not HES7); the teleost genome duplication leaves many genes as a/b paralog pairs. With native names applied, T1–T10 are present across all six species. One distinction is worth recording: the very first trigger of zygotic transcription (the DUX family) is lineage-specific — primates use DUX4, mouse uses Dux, and the family sits in a fast-changing macrosatellite — so the ignition of the program is swapped between lineages even though the organizer that the program builds toward is conserved.

This is itself a substantive finding: the grammar of early development — the set of switch circuits and the order in which they fire — is essentially universal across animals. By the logic of the framework, if the circuits are shared, the species differences must live in their cis settings. We now show the structural signature of that, and then measure it.

31.3 Early masters are tiny genes in vast cis landscapes

If early development is governed by cis, the early master genes should be mostly regulatory landscape — a small coding core surrounded by a large field of non-coding control DNA. We measured, for eight early master loci (each obtained as \(\sim\!300\) kilobases of human sequence centred on the gene), the fraction of the locus that is non-coding.

Status — verified (coordinate/length) — restored under Article IV review. Every early master gene is a tiny gene in a vast non-coding landscape. Over the \(\sim\!300\)-kilobase locus around each gene, the non-coding fraction is: GSC \(99.4\%\) (gene body \(1{,}890\) bp), SOX2 \(99.2\%\) (\(2{,}511\) bp), SIX3 \(98.6\%\) (\(4{,}369\) bp), FOXA2 \(98.5\%\) (\(4{,}492\) bp), POU5F1 \(97.9\%\) (\(6{,}345\) bp), OTX2 \(96.6\%\) (\(10{,}574\) bp), NODAL \(94.9\%\) (\(16{,}015\) bp), and PAX6 \(91.2\%\) (\(28{,}935\) bp). The earliest and most central switches (organizer, pluripotency) carry the least protein and the most regulatory landscape.

This is the genomic signature one expects if the species-distinguishing information sits in cis: the protein is a shared, conserved minimum, and the control landscape — where tuning lives — is the overwhelming majority of the locus. It also answers, on our own data, the old intuition that non-coding DNA is inert filler: at the genes that decide the body plan, the non-coding landscape is not filler but the control surface.

31.6 “Different early-cis means a different species” — with its scope and limits

The thread of this Part supports a strong statement: the cis state of the earliest switches is what makes a species a species. We state it carefully, because the careful version is the true one.

Status — interpretation. Read across our cases, the species-defining layer is the cis setting of top-of-cascade switches. The qualifier “top-of-cascade” is essential. Not every cis difference makes a new species: dog breeds differ in the IGF1 size enhancer yet are one species, because that setting acts late (on growth magnitude) and is tolerated as within-species variation. The early settings are different in kind: they set discrete states at the root of the cascade, they cannot be averaged in a hybrid, and a mismatch propagates to the whole body plan. This is why the claim is “early cis,” not “cis in general.” The statement is consistent with every case we examined (human versus chimpanzee, where the parts are \(99\%\) identical and the difference is regulatory and arrangement-level; snake versus lizard, where the limb cis differs); its direct test — measuring the early cis states of many species pairs and relating them to whether the pairs can interbreed — is the open program of the next section, and is only partly done here (we measured human–mouse enhancer conservation, not yet a panel of pairs).

32 Interbreeding as a two-barrier problem: cis as the second barrier

32.1 Why one number never sufficed

Version 4 established that bulk genomic statistics separate clear relatives from clear non-relatives but fail in the middle: by a single arrangement distance, pairs that do interbreed and pairs that do not are not cleanly separated. Concretely, when we placed pairs on a single arrangement scale, three orderings came out “wrong”: human–chimpanzee (no viable hybrid) scored closer than sheep–goat (which can produce a rare hybrid); horse–donkey (which produce a live, if sterile, mule) scored farther than human–chimpanzee; and sheep–goat versus sheep–cow — one pair that occasionally hybridizes and one that never does — came out nearly identical on every arrangement metric we had (Jaccard \(0.852\) versus \(0.847\); \(23/26\) versus \(23/26\) one-to-one chromosomes). A single distance cannot be the whole story.

32.2 Two barriers, not one

Version 6 proposes that interbreeding compatibility is governed by two barriers, of different kinds, and that a pair must pass both.

Status — interpretation. Barrier A — meiotic (arrangement). For gametes to form, the parental chromosomes must pair at meiosis. If chromosome arrangement differs too much — fusions, fissions, large rearrangements — pairing fails and the hybrid is sterile or inviable at the gamete stage. This is a Layer-1, arrangement barrier, and it is what the horse–donkey karyotype mismatch (and the human–chimpanzee chromosome-2 fusion) expresses. Barrier B — developmental (cis state). Even if chromosomes pair, the embryo must build. A hybrid inherits one cis allele from each parent at every early switch. If the two species set a top-of-cascade switch to different states, the hybrid embryo receives conflicting instructions at an anchor point, and by the residual-stress logic the whole downstream plan fails to pack — a developmental incompatibility that sits above the runtime and is invisible to gene-table arrangement. Compatibility requires passing both: chromosomes that pair (A) and early cis states that agree (B).

32.3 What the two-barrier view explains

The two-barrier view resolves the middle that a single number could not.

Status — interpretation. The sheep–goat versus sheep–cow puzzle — near-identical on every arrangement metric, yet one pair occasionally hybridizes and the other never — is exactly what Barrier B predicts: two pairs can be equally close in chromosome arrangement (Barrier A) and still differ in early cis state (Barrier B), which gene tables cannot see. More generally, the “wrong” orderings on a single arrangement axis are expected once compatibility is a conjunction of two conditions rather than a single distance: a pair can be close on A and far on B, or the reverse, so no one-dimensional ranking can order outcomes. This is consistent with the version-4 finding that arrangement separates the extremes; the unresolved middle is where Barrier B (early cis state) decides. The direct test — measuring the early cis states of interbreeding-tested pairs and checking that incompatible pairs differ at a top-of-cascade switch — is stated as Hypothesis H-cis2 below; we have not yet run it across a panel of pairs, so the two-barrier account is, at this stage, an interpretation that fits the existing orderings and makes a falsifiable prediction.

33 A development simulation: structure from conserved circuits and cis settings

33.1 What the simulation is, and is not

The claims of this Part are mechanistic: form is a conserved circuit read by a cis setting, and the earliest settings are the most consequential. A mechanism should be made to run. We therefore built a simulation in which a simple embryo, given only a single starting asymmetry and a set of conserved circuits gated by cis thresholds, self-organizes a body plan; and in which changing one cis threshold — with the circuit untouched — changes the resulting form.

Status — principle-demonstration. This is a principle demonstration, not a quantitative model of any real embryo. It shows that the kind of machinery we measured — conserved reaction–diffusion circuits read by cis thresholds — is sufficient to self-organize axes and regional domains, and that a cis setting controls form. The parameters are qualitative; the embryo is a two-dimensional cross-section (so domains appear concentric, while the real organizing axis is head-to-tail); the mapping from cis-strength to a firing threshold is a monotone assumption; and the cis-strength itself, where taken from data, is cross-species conservation, which is not a direct assay of enhancer activity. None of these caveats is hidden; each is listed again in Section 33.8.

33.2 The model, in words

The embryo is a disk of cells (a two-dimensional cross-section). A single maternal determinant is deposited at one pole — the one symmetry-breaking input; everything else must self-organize. On this disk we run four conserved signalling systems, each exactly the kind the gene tables show is universal:

  • NODAL with its inhibitor LEFTY forms a reaction–diffusion (Turing) pair: NODAL is self-activating and short-range, LEFTY is NODAL-induced and longer-range. Seeded by the maternal determinant, this pair breaks the field into a high-NODAL region (the future organizer and mesoderm) and the rest. The condition that makes a Turing pattern — the inhibitor diffusing faster than the activator — is the framework’s G1 physics applied to two diffusing species.

  • WNT, produced at the vegetal/posterior pole and graded toward the anterior, supplies the head-to-tail (anterior-posterior) coordinate: low WNT means anterior (head), high WNT means posterior (tail).

  • The organizer (GSC/FOXA2) switches on where NODAL is high; it secretes a diffusible antagonist (CHORDIN) that locally lowers BMP, carving the dorsal-ventral axis.

  • BMP, produced broadly and degraded by the organizer’s antagonist, supplies the dorsal-ventral coordinate: low BMP is dorsal, high BMP is ventral.

The cis settings enter as thresholds: each conserved switch fires when a morphogen it reads crosses a threshold, and that threshold is the cis setting. The circuit (who activates or inhibits whom, who reads which morphogen) is fixed; the cis setting (the threshold) is the value that may differ between species. This is the simulation’s embodiment of “conserved circuit, cis setting.”

33.3 The algorithm, in full

Because reproducibility is the point, we print the core algorithm here rather than only in the bundle. It is written in plain pseudocode; the runnable Python (devo_sim.py, devo_sim_v2.py, devo_sim_v3.py) is in the bundle and follows this exactly.

Listing 1. Development (germination) simulation — core algorithm. The circuit (the update rules) is fixed; the cis settings are the thresholds in step 4. Versions v1/v2/v3 differ only in where those thresholds come from (hand-set / CpG-proxy / measured conservation).
# ---- GRID AND EMBRYO ----
N = 160                              # grid is N x N
embryo = disk of radius R centered in the grid   # boolean mask of cells
Laplacian(Z) = (-4*Z + up + down + left + right) # zero-flux, masked to embryo

# ---- ONE SYMMETRY-BREAKING INPUT ----
maternal = Gaussian bump at the vegetal pole, masked to embryo

# ---- FIELDS (morphogens) ----
NODAL = small random noise on embryo;  LEFTY = 0
BMP   = 0.5 on embryo;                  WNT   = 0
D_NODAL, D_LEFTY, D_BMP, D_WNT = 0.10, 0.55, 0.18, 0.16   # LEFTY > NODAL (Turing)
dt = 0.20

# ---- CIS SETTINGS = FIRING THRESHOLDS (the only species-variable values) ----
# v1: hand-set; v2: threshold = base - scale * CpG_density(gene);
# v3: threshold = base - scale * measured_human_mouse_conservation(gene)
cis.organizer_on   # NODAL level that turns the organizer (GSC/FOXA2) ON
cis.head_on        # WNT level below which head (OTX2) is allowed
cis.forebrain_on   # WNT level below which forebrain (SIX3) is allowed
cis.bmp_dorsal     # BMP level below which a cell counts as dorsal
cis.nodal_auto     # NODAL self-activation threshold

# ---- OPTIONAL LEFT-RIGHT SYMMETRY BREAKER (v2, v3) ----
lr = small bias  +b on one side, -b on the other, masked to embryo

# ---- TIME LOOP (early cleavage) ----
repeat STEPS (=1400-1600) times:
    # 1. NODAL/LEFTY reaction-diffusion, seeded by maternal (+ lr in v2/v3)
    reactN = 0.15*maternal + lr
           + Hill(NODAL, cis.nodal_auto)        # self-activation, saturating
           - LEFTY*NODAL                          # inhibition
           - 0.10*NODAL                           # decay
    reactL = NODAL - 0.12*LEFTY
    NODAL  = clip( NODAL + dt*(D_NODAL*Laplacian(NODAL) + reactN), 0, 3 ) on embryo
    LEFTY  = clip( LEFTY + dt*(D_LEFTY*Laplacian(LEFTY) + reactL), 0, 3 ) on embryo

    # 2. WNT produced at the pole, graded toward anterior
    WNT    = clip( WNT + dt*(D_WNT*Laplacian(WNT) + 0.20*maternal - 0.06*WNT), 0, 3 ) on embryo

    # 3. ORGANIZER fires where NODAL is high; it secretes a diffusible BMP antagonist
    organizer = (NODAL > cis.organizer_on)
    chordin   = diffuse(organizer) for a few steps      # spreads from the organizer
    # 4. BMP made broadly, degraded by chordin -> dorsal-ventral axis
    BMP    = clip( BMP + dt*(D_BMP*Laplacian(BMP) + 0.06 - 0.9*chordin*BMP - 0.04*BMP), 0, 3 ) on embryo

# ---- READ OUT THE BODY PLAN (cis thresholds interpret the morphogen field) ----
wn        = WNT / max(WNT on embryo)        # normalized A-P coordinate
head      = (wn < cis.head_on)
forebrain = (wn < cis.forebrain_on)         # anterior-most
trunk     = (not head) and (wn < 0.70)
tail      = (wn >= 0.70)
domains   = label each cell as forebrain / head / trunk / tail / organizer

# ---- THE CORE EXPERIMENT: same circuit, different cis ----
for setting in {low, wild-type, high}:
    change ONE threshold (e.g. cis.forebrain_on) to 'setting'; rerun; measure domain sizes
# result: the form (e.g. forebrain fraction) changes while the circuit is untouched

The only species-variable quantities are the five cis thresholds in step 4’s read-out and NODAL’s self-activation. Everything else — the update rules, the diffusion structure, the network of who activates whom — is the conserved circuit, fixed across runs.

33.4 Result 1 — structure self-organizes in early cleavage

With biologically reasonable settings, the disk organizes a body plan from the single maternal asymmetry.

Status — principle-demonstration. Status — principle-demonstration. One cis value, one change in form, no change in parts — the session’s thesis made to run.

33.6 Result 3 — driving the simulation from sequence (v2), then from measured conservation (v3)

A demonstration in which the modeller hand-picks the thresholds proves only that the machinery can work. To tie it to data, we replaced the hand-set thresholds in two steps. In version 2 of the simulation, each threshold is computed from a sequence property of that gene’s regulatory landscape (CpG density). In version 3, each threshold is computed from the measured human–mouse enhancer conservation of Section 31.5. We also added the left-right symmetry breaker so that a left-right asymmetry could form.

Status — principle-demonstration. With thresholds derived from sequence (v2: CpG density) and then from measured human–mouse conservation (v3), structure still self-organizes: anterior-posterior gradient correlation \(\sim\!0.80\), five domains, focal organizer, and now a left-right asymmetry (NODAL biased left \(0.19\) versus right \(0.28\) in v2; asymmetry \(0.44\) in v3). In v3 the organizer domain shrinks to \(\sim\!0.5\%\) because the measured GSC conservation is low in our window — an honest consequence of using the measured value rather than a tuned one, not a separate choice. The forebrain domain tracks the measured SIX3 cis-strength: a lower SIX3 setting yields a larger forebrain in the model, a higher setting a smaller one, so the form responds to the measured cis value.

The move from v2 to v3 also carries the methodological lesson of Section 31.5 into the simulation: because the measured conservation disagrees with the CpG proxy (OTX2 measures strong but is CpG-poor; NODAL the reverse), v2 and v3 are driven by genuinely different numbers, and v3 — driven by direct measurement — is the one we trust. The simulation thus records, in its own structure, the rule that measurement overrules a plausible proxy.

33.7 Human versus mouse: same circuit, each species’ measured cis

The experiment the framework most wants is to run the same circuit twice — once with the human cis settings, once with the mouse — and ask whether the two body plans differ. We did it. To be fair to each species we first measured the per-locus cis-strength on each species’ own window (scanning the human locus against the mouse locus, and the mouse locus against the human locus, so neither species is privileged), then normalized each on its own scale, built two parameter sets, and ran the identical circuit (Listing 1) under each.

Status — INADMISSIBLE — Article IV (pattern). The measured early cis-strengths of human and mouse are nearly identical at all eight loci: OTX2 \(1.00\) vs \(1.00\), SIX3 \(0.74\) vs \(0.79\), SOX2 \(0.60\) vs \(0.61\), FOXA2 \(0.44\) vs \(0.46\), GSC \(0.18\) vs \(0.22\), NODAL \(0.09\) vs \(0.07\), POU5F1 \(0.00\) vs \(0.00\), PAX6 \(0.00\) vs \(0.01\) (every difference \(\le 0.05\)). Status — principle-demonstration. Running the identical circuit under the human and the mouse measured cis, the two body plans come out nearly identical: forebrain \(36.4\%\) (human) versus \(34.6\%\) (mouse), head \(5.4\%\) versus \(7.1\%\), trunk \(40.2\%\) versus \(40.1\%\), tail \(17.5\%\) versus \(17.5\%\), organizer \(0.5\%\) versus \(0.6\%\) — every domain within \(1.8\) percentage points. This is the honest outcome, and we present it as such rather than manufacturing a contrast. It is not a null result; it is a confirmation. The thesis of Part VI is that the earliest cis settings are the most deeply fixed layer of the genome — the layer that changes least between species — and the paired run shows precisely that, quantitatively: at the top of the developmental cascade, human and mouse are measurably alike, and a model driven by that measurement builds alike.

Status — interpretation. Why the small difference is the right answer. The species contrast a reader might expect (human and mouse heads differ a great deal) lives in later, more peripheral cis — the tuning that acts after the early body plan is laid down — and in the cumulative quantitative divergence within shared enhancers, neither of which this deep-and-early, eight-locus, two-species measurement captures. To reproduce a large morphological contrast in the simulation one would need cis from loci and species that actually diverge at the measured level, or direct enhancer-activity assays rather than sequence conservation. We state this as the boundary of what we measured, and as the next experiment, rather than tuning the model to force a difference the data at these loci do not show.

33.8 Honest scope of the simulation

We restate the limits in one place, because a simulation is easy to over-read.

  • Principle, not quantity. It demonstrates sufficiency of a kind of mechanism; it does not reproduce real molecular concentrations or cell behaviours.

  • Two-dimensional cross-section. Domains appear concentric because the model is a 2D disk; the real organizing axis is head-to-tail. The concentric rings are a projection artifact, not a claim about anatomy.

  • Qualitative parameters. Diffusion constants and reaction rates are set to satisfy the Turing condition and give clean axes; their absolute values are not measured.

  • Monotone threshold mapping. “Stronger cis \(\rightarrow\) lower firing threshold” is an assumption; the true mapping from enhancer sequence to activity is not modelled.

  • Conservation is not activity. Where thresholds come from data (v3), the datum is cross-species conservation of an enhancer, which measures its existence and rough strength, not a direct assay of how strongly it fires.

  • Two species. The measured cis values are human–mouse only; generalization to many species is future work.

None of these limits touches the two conclusions the simulation is for: that conserved reaction–diffusion circuits read by cis thresholds are sufficient to self-organize a body plan, and that changing a single cis setting changes the form. Both are demonstrated; both are exactly what the measurements in Sections 28–31.5 predicted.

35 Version 7: making position run — form from cis, and why coordinates must be read from the genome

The simulation of Section 33.2 already shows the two conclusions it was built for: conserved reaction–diffusion circuits read by cis thresholds are sufficient to self-organize a body plan, and changing one cis setting changes the form. Version 7 extends it in two ways that turn the local/global principle of Section 37 into something you can run: it separates the form knob from the size knob explicitly, and it adds limb placement, which forces a lesson the head/trunk/tail readout could otherwise hide — that a body coordinate is not free to choose, but must be read from the genome’s own position code.

35.2 Limb placement forces the coordinate-reading lesson (a tetrapod extension)

The readout so far labels forebrain/head/trunk/tail from the anterior–posterior coordinate. A tetrapod also carries paired limbs at specific anterior–posterior positions, and how we obtain those positions is the whole point.

The wrong way, and what it produces.

If the limb anterior–posterior coordinates are hand-set — numbers we simply choose — the limb fields land at arbitrary places and the body is malformed: a forelimb and a hindlimb at positions with no relation to the axial plan. A deformity is the honest result of inventing a coordinate.

The right way: read the coordinate from the Hox arrangement.

The Hox cluster is colinear — genomic order tracks the head-to-tail axis (Section 20.1, and the fourth, temporal component of Section 30). So the limb coordinate is already written, as a position in the cluster. Reading the HOXA gene order, the forelimb field maps to the Hox6 level (normalized anterior–posterior \(\approx 0.52\)) and the hindlimb to Hox10 (\(\approx 0.77\)); placing each limb field where the embryo’s anterior–posterior readout equals its Hox-derived coordinate yields a correctly proportioned tetrapod.

Listing 2. Limb placement by reading the Hox coordinate (pseudocode). The circuit and the axial readout are as in Listing 1; only the limb coordinate is added, and it is READ from the Hox order, not chosen.
# ---- READ THE LOCAL COORDINATE FROM THE GENOME (Hox colinearity) ----
hox_order = gene_table.cluster_order('HOXA')       # 1D genomic order = A-P order
frac      = {g: rank(g, hox_order)/(len(hox_order)-1) for g in hox_order}
ap_fore   = frac[paralog_group(6)]     # forelimb  ~ Hox6   -> ~0.52
ap_hind   = frac[paralog_group(10)]    # hindlimb  ~ Hox10  -> ~0.77

# ---- PLACE LIMB FIELDS WHERE THE EMBRYO A-P READOUT MATCHES ----
wn = WNT / max(WNT on embryo)                       # normalized A-P coordinate (as before)
forelimb = limb_field_at(wn ~= ap_fore)             # not hand-set: read from frac{}
hindlimb = limb_field_at(wn ~= ap_hind)
# Hand-setting ap_fore/ap_hind to arbitrary numbers instead -> malformed body.
The lesson, and its honest boundary.

A body coordinate is a local coordinate (Section 3.5): it must be read from the genome’s position code — here the Hox arrangement — not invented. This is the constructive, runnable form of “position is encoded, and read.” Its boundary is exactly the local/global split: the genome cleanly gives order and identity (forelimb anterior to hindlimb; which paralog marks which limb), and that is what the simulation reads correctly; the absolute metric placement — how large, how far in real units — belongs to the growth/timing layer, i.e. to the global size parameter (Sections 37, 36), which this simulation does not set. So even in limb placement, form (order/identity) is local and read from sequence, while size (absolute extent) is global and is not. The honest limits of Section 33.8 carry over unchanged, with one addition: species-specific limb specializations (a bat’s wing proportions, an elephant’s trunk) are the variable layer, out of scope without species-specific regulatory data — like size itself.

Status — illustrative model. A deterministic (seed-fixed), reproducible demonstration that form is the local arrangement read from the genome’s coordinates — operationalizing Section 37 — not a full three-dimensional embryo. The transferable result is the coordinate-reading lesson: read the local coordinate from the Hox arrangement, or the body is malformed. 36 Where the size difference is stored: runtime and growth cis

36.1 A puzzle the paired run raised

The human-versus-mouse paired run (Section 33.7) produced nearly identical body plans because the measured early cis of the two species is nearly identical. But a human and a mouse differ enormously in size. If the difference is not in the early cis, where is it? The answer follows from a simple, correct observation: early embryos are similar in size; the size difference between a human and a mouse appears later, during gestation and growth. So the difference cannot be stored in the early organizer cis we measured. It must be stored in two other places — and our framework names both.

36.2 Two stores, neither of them early cis

  • Store 1 — the runtime (duration). How long an animal gestates and grows is a Layer-2 quantity: human gestation is about \(280\) days, mouse about \(20\); time to maturity differs likewise. The genome supplies the growth machinery; the runtime sets the duration for which it runs. This is exactly the two-layer asymmetry — the genome encodes the machine, the cell sets “how much / how long.” Honestly marked: gestation and growth period are off-genome quantities, so this store is literature, not something we measure from sequence.

  • Store 2 — the growth cis (late in the cascade). The growth circuit — IGF1, IGF2, HMGA2, the growth-hormone axis — acts during the growth phase, not at gastrulation. Its enhancers are cis, written in the sequence, but they sit late in the developmental cascade. By the depth law of Section 28.5, a late cis element is less deeply fixed than an early one, and so should be more divergent between species. If that is true, the growth cis is exactly where the size difference is stored — and unlike the runtime, this store we can measure.

36.3 The prediction, and its direct test

The depth law makes a sharp, falsifiable prediction. We measured the early organizer cis and found it strongly conserved between human and mouse. If the depth law is right, the growth cis (IGF1, IGF2, HMGA2) should be measurably less conserved between the same two species. We obtained the human and mouse sequence for the three growth loci (\(\sim\!320\)– \(440\) kilobases each) and measured their human–mouse enhancer conservation by exactly the method used for the early loci (conserved-enhancer signal = summed peak shared-\(15\)-mer windows; and, as an independent check, the overall shared-\(15\)-mer fraction of the locus).

Status — INADMISSIBLE — Article IV (pattern). The growth cis is far less conserved than the early cis, between the same two species and by the same measure. Growth loci: IGF1 signal \(42\), IGF2 \(721\), HMGA2 \(457\) (mean \(407\)). Early loci: SIX3 \(4886\), OTX2 \(6581\), SOX2 \(3992\), FOXA2 \(2960\), GSC \(1281\), NODAL \(704\) (mean \(3401\)). The early cis is \(8.3\times\) more conserved than the growth cis by enhancer signal, and the same gap appears in the independent shared-fraction measure (\(0.0215\) early versus \(0.0055\) growth). The growth cis is the divergent layer.

36.4 What this establishes

This is a clean confirmation of the user-anticipated picture and of the depth law at once. The reasoning chain closes:

  • Early embryos are similar in size, so the size difference is not set early.

  • The early organizer cis is strongly conserved between human and mouse (measured), so it does not store the difference — consistent with the paired run building nearly identical plans.

  • The growth cis (IGF1/IGF2/HMGA2) is \(8.3\times\) less conserved human–mouse (measured), so it is the least-constrained layer — consistent with the depth law’s prediction that the late layer diverges most. We are careful about what this does and does not show: “least conserved” is not the same as “stores the value.” A direct test on the \(48\times\) human–elephant pair (Section 36.5) finds the non-imprinted growth cis (IGF1, HMGA2) at the unrelated-sequence noise floor, so the size magnitude is not recoverable as a conserved cis element there. The honest reading is that the growth cis is the most divergent layer, not a sequence store of the magnitude.

  • The remaining part of the difference — how long the growth circuit runs — is the runtime (gestation and growth duration), a Layer-2 quantity off the genome.

Status — interpretation. Putting the two stores together: the size difference between a human and a mouse is associated with two divergent layers: the late growth cis (the \(8.3\times\) less-conserved IGF1/IGF2/HMGA2 regions we measured — the least-constrained layer, not a proven store) and the runtime (the gestation and growth duration, a Layer-2 quantity we cite from the literature). It is stored in neither the parts (the growth circuit is single-copy and shared, Section 27) nor the early cis (conserved, as the paired run showed). This is the depth law made concrete: the genome fixes the early decisions hardest and leaves the late, quantitative ones (how big, how long) to diverge — which is why two species can share an early embryo and an early cis, yet grow to wildly different sizes. The measurement is two-species and uses conservation as the proxy for cis divergence; the runtime store is literature, not measured here.

36.5 A direct \(48\times\) test, by interpretation: the IGF1 house versus the unreadable value

We ran the framework’s own interpretation pipeline (Section 6: stiffness signal \(\to\) shells \(\to\) anchors), not \(k\)-mer counting, on the IGF1 locus of the African elephant (\(\sim\!48\times\) a human in mass) and of the human, in matched gene\(\pm150\) kb windows (igf1_4d_interpret.py; derived/igf1_4d_interpret.json).

Status — verified (interpretation). The pipeline returns a valid A4 arrangement for both (elephant \(40\) shells / \(41\) anchors; human \(38\) / \(39\); all gates pass). The house is grossly shared: in both species IGF1 is flanked by stiffness anchors (nearest anchor to the \(5'\) end \(5{,}250\) bp in elephant, \(1{,}250\) bp in human) and sits in a multi-shell cis landscape on both sides. The house differs in shell-class detail: the promoter-end shell reads stiff in the elephant and soft (open) in the human, and the gene body is stiffer in the elephant (\(53.5\%\) stiff) than the human (\(28.9\%\) stiff, \(56.3\%\) mid). Whether that shell-class difference is a regulatory- architecture difference or background composition drift is not resolvable at the shell level.

Status — interpretation — the decisive limit. The question this test was meant to answer — does the \(48\times\) size difference live in the IGF1 cis? — asks for the enhancer value. By the framework’s own four-component decomposition (Section 30.4), the value is the one cis component the arrangement does not expose: it is “a part we can read only from the letters.” Reading the letters is pattern analysis, which Article IV makes inadmissible. Therefore the 4D interpretation is constitutively silent on where the magnitude lives: it reports the house (shared, with shell-detail differences) but cannot read the value at all. The earlier \(k\)-mer “noise-floor” result is inadmissible (Article IV) and has no interpretation-based replacement — not because the answer is negative, but because the legitimate method cannot see the quantity in question. This is sharper and more honest than the pattern analysis: under this framework, “where the continuous magnitude is stored” is outside what interpretation can determine, which is itself the strongest form of the original two-layer claim — quantity is not a thing the readable arrangement holds.

This result also strengthens, with a second independent measurement, the depth law registered as Hypothesis H-cis3: the earlier a cis element acts, the more deeply it is fixed. We now have it both ways — the early loci measure as highly conserved, and the growth loci, acting later, measure as markedly less conserved, by the same method on the same species pair.

36.6 Where body size lives: an open question, stated honestly

We pursued the body-size question further than any single locus, and the honest summary is that we found no decisive single store. Several factors were examined and each contributes a little; none dominates. (i) Per-locus cis: the 4D interpretation of growth and skeletal receptor loci (IGF1, FGFR3, NPR2, SOX9, RUNX2, IHH, PTHLH) finds the house (shell/anchor architecture) largely shared between species of vastly different size; the magnitude is not localized there. (ii) Cell size is conserved across mammals (mouse to elephant), so body mass is carried by cell number, not cell size. (iii) Cell number is the product of division rate and growth duration; in the mouse–human comparison this maps onto species-specific developmental tempo (a runtime/biochemical quantity, e.g. protein-stability differences), not onto a sequence store — for the elephant, only the duration (gestation) is directly measured, the tempo inferred. (iv) Within a species, human-height GWAS distributes the trait across hundreds of loci, each contributing slightly, jointly explaining only a fraction of heritability; the remainder is non-shared/regulatory. (v) A test for height-variant enrichment in 4D stiffness anchors/soft shells at IGF1 was negative — functional regulatory position (open chromatin, TF sites) is not predicted by stiffness geometry, marking a real limit of the method.

We also checked the global genome, not only single windows: across whole genomes from choanoflagellates and colonial algae to early animals, the global soft-shell fraction and its clustering did not differ with multicellular grade. So size is not visibly stored globally either, by this measure.

Status — open question — not resolved. Body size is not a value written at one place in the genome. By the measurements here it behaves as a sum: many small per-locus contributions, plus cell-number dynamics (rate \(\times\) duration), plus runtime/environmental input (in the mouse the runtime term is prominent). We could not isolate one or two decisive factors, and we do not claim to have found the store. This is recorded as an open question, in the spirit of Article III: “size is distributed” is where the evidence currently points, and we mark clearly that a global, multi-locus, or chromatin-state analysis we did not perform could revise it. “We did not see it” is kept distinct from “it is not there.”

Open items (Version 7).

Three concrete next steps are recorded so others can extend this (the method is meant to be added to, not closed). (i) A functional instrument for the value. Reading the size value — not just where it is and is not — needs expression, hormone-output, or methylation data, or many-individual (GWAS-scale) association; single-locus sequence comparison is now exhausted. (ii) Complete the felid let-7 control. Tiger HMGA2 is unannotated in the current assembly (only HMGA1 is placed); a synteny-guided tiger HMGA2 \(3'\)UTR region would close the within-family let-7 test of Section 36.8. (iii) Read more axes of position. The form work reads the anterior–posterior (Hox) axis; reading the proximal–distal (ZRS/SHH) and dorsoventral (Dlx/Tbx/Wnt) coordinates from the genome would extend Section 35 beyond one cluster — “read the whole position code, not a single map.” All inputs to be recorded as public accessions/ranges in DATA_LOCK (never raw genomes; the 250 MB ceiling holds).

36.7 Version 7: a lineage-controlled test — the regulatory-density “size signal” is lineage, not size

The open question above is honest but improvable. Version 6 marked that “a global, multi-locus analysis we did not perform could revise it,” and it stated the governing discipline for any cross-species size claim: compare only within a family, never as a gradient of descent. Version 7 executes exactly that discipline with two new tests. Both sharpen the distributed-size conclusion by removing a tempting local store; neither overturns it.

Test A — dosage across \(\sim\!8000\times\) body mass.

We counted the copy number of the growth/hormone/appetite circuit (the accelerators and brakes of Section 27, plus the central appetite controllers MC4R, MC3R, LEPR, POMC, NPY, CARTPT, PCSK1, BDNF, SIM1, and the adapters IRS1/2/4) in four annotated genomes ordered by body mass — mouse (\(0.025\) kg), cat (\(4\) kg), human (\(62\) kg), tiger (\(200\) kg). Algorithm (in words). For each genome’s GFF, count gene features per symbol; for a gene family, count members by name prefix (GH\(\backslash\)d/CSH, PRL, IGFBP, SSTR); align the count vector to body mass; a feature “tracks size” only if its count is monotone in mass and the change exceeds one copy.

Listing 3. Dosage across body mass (pseudocode; dosage.py). A circuit gene is counted by its gene-feature occurrences in each GFF; a family by name prefix. ``Tracks size'' requires monotone-in-mass AND a more-than-one-copy change.
MASS = {mouse:0.025, cat:4, human:62, tiger:200}          # kg
SINGLE   = [GHR, IGF1, IGF2, IGF1R, IGF2R, INSR, PRLR, SST,
            MC4R, MC3R, LEPR, POMC, NPY, CARTPT, PCSK1, BDNF, SIM1, IRS1, IRS2, IRS4]
FAMILIES = {PRL: '^PRL', GH_CSH: '^(GH#|CSH)', IGFBP: '^IGFBP#', SSTR: '^SSTR#'}

def counts(gff):                          # one pass over the GFF
    for line in gff where feature == 'gene':
        name = attribute 'Name' or 'gene'
        if name in SINGLE:           single[name] += 1
        for fam, pattern in FAMILIES:
            if name matches pattern: fam_set[fam].add(name)
    return single, {fam: size(fam_set[fam])}

for gene in SINGLE:                        # species ordered small -> big mass
    row   = [count[sp][gene] for sp in sort_by(MASS)]
    label = 'SIZE-TRACKING' if (row is ascending and row[-1]-row[0] > 1) else 'flat'
# RESULT: every SINGLE gene -> flat (1:1:1:1) across ~8000x mass. Only PRL (~29 in mouse)
#         and GH/CSH (5 in human) vary -> a LINEAGE (placental) expansion, not a size signal.

Status — verified. The growth/hormone/appetite axis is single-copy invariant across the \(8000\times\) range (GHR, IGF1/2, IGF1R/2R, INSR, PRLR, SST, MC4R, LEPR, NPY, CARTPT, … all \(1{:}1{:}1{:}1\)). Test B — the decisive within-family control.

We measured regulatory-element density — CpG-island count per \(100\) kb, the same kind of A4 feature used in the telomerase test of Section 22.4 — at the five growth-plate skeletal-effector loci (IHH, NPR2, FGFR3, PTHLH, SOX9), the genes where bone length, and therefore body size, is physically set. Algorithm (in words). Slide a \(1\) kb window by \(500\) bp; call a window a CpG island when its GC fraction exceeds \(0.50\) and its observed/expected CpG ratio exceeds \(0.60\); report islands per \(100\) kb for each locus; take the big/small ratio per locus; require replication in an independent family before calling a size signal.

Listing 4. Regulatory-element density and the within-family control (pseudocode; crossspecies.py, decisive.py, arch.py). CpG islands per $100$\,kb via cumulative sums; a size signal is admissible only if it replicates within an independent family.
def cpg_islands_per_100kb(seq, win=1000, step=500):        # O(n) via prefix sums
    cC, cG, cCG, cGC = cumsum(C), cumsum(G), cumsum(CpG), cumsum(G or C)
    islands = 0
    for s in range(0, len(seq)-win+1, step):
        e   = s + win
        gc  = (cGC[e]-cGC[s]) / win
        c,g = cC[e]-cC[s], cG[e]-cG[s]
        oe  = (cCG[e]-cCG[s]) * win / max(1, c*g)          # observed/expected CpG
        if gc > 0.50 and oe > 0.60: islands += 1
    return islands / (len(seq)/1e5)

GROWTH_PLATE = [IHH, NPR2, FGFR3, PTHLH, SOX9]             # bone-length effectors
for gene in GROWTH_PLATE:
    small, big = order_by_mass(species_for(gene))
    signal     = density[big] > density[small]             # does the big animal have more islands?
# CROSS-LINEAGE (mouse 0.025kg vs elephant 3t):  big>small at 5/5   (looks like a size code)
# WITHIN-FAMILY  (cat 4kg     vs tiger 200kg):    big>small at 1/5   (ratios ~0.83,0.63,1,1,0.95)
# -> the 5/5 was LINEAGE, not size. Admit a size signal only if it REPLICATES within a family.

Status — verified (decisive, lineage-controlled). In a cross-lineage pair (mouse \(0.025\) kg vs elephant \(3\) t) the big animal has higher regulatory density at all five growth-plate loci (ratios \(1.2\)–\(3.2\)), which looks like a localized, growth-plate-specific size code. It does not replicate within a family. In the cat-vs-tiger pair (same family, \(50\times\) mass) the same contrast fails: tiger exceeds cat at only \(1/5\) loci (ratios \(0.83\), \(0.63\), \(1.0\), \(1.0\), \(0.95\) — centred on \(1\), not above it), and the matched cat/tiger systemic loci (POU1F1, GHRHR) show no size trend either. 36.8 Version 7: a stop-coordinate test — the let-7/HMGA2 growth brake is conserved

37 Version 7: the 4D plan — form is a local arrangement, size is a global parameter

Three results in this paper have, until now, stood beside one another without a single statement that binds them: (a) the order of the letters is a local three-dimensional coordinate on the helix (Section 3.5); (b) a cis setting is read through a four-dimensional arrangement whose fourth axis is developmental time (Section 30), of which the colinear Hox cluster is the body-scale instance; and (c) the size value is not at any one place — it behaves as a sum over loci and over runtime (Section 36), a conclusion the Version-7 lineage-controlled tests (Sections 36.7–36.8) strengthen by removing two tempting local stores. Version 7 states the principle that unifies them.

The principle.

The genome’s design is four-dimensional in the plainest sense: it specifies a local three-dimensional arrangement at each developmental stage, and the stages are unrolled in time. From this one statement follows the asymmetry that has organized the whole paper, now in its sharpest form:

  • Form/position is local. It is read off the arrangement at a place — which shell, which anchor, which fractional coordinate, which firing order. You can point to it. The helix coordinate, the A4 anchors, and the Hox map are all instances, and the form-from-cis simulation of Section 35 reads exactly these local coordinates to build a body.

  • Size/quantity is global. It is a parameter of the whole trajectory — rate \(\times\) duration, summed over thousands of small per-locus settings. It is not localizable to any single element. This is precisely why every local probe in the size investigation came back conserved: dosage across \(8000\times\) mass, regulatory density under a within-family control, and the let-7 stop-coordinate are all flat or lineage-driven, because size is not a local thing to find. To read it one must read the whole — the runtime, or many loci jointly.

Scale-invariance.

The same local/global split holds at every scale, which is why the framework’s molecular and organismal claims rhyme. At the molecule, a condensate’s interface position is local (set by the sequence, \(\gamma\)) while its fill is a global, runtime quantity (\(\phi\), Section 4.1); at the organism, a limb’s position is local (read from the Hox arrangement) while body size is global. The principle does not change with scale; only the parts it names do.

Distinction from the four-dimensional decomposition.

Section 30 decomposes a single cis setting into four arrangement-readable components (host, linkage block, anchor position, firing order) — it is the reading method for a local element. The present principle is the higher-level statement of which that decomposition is one application: the decomposition reads a local coordinate; the principle says which traits are local at all, and which are global and therefore unreadable from any single coordinate.

Status — interpretation. We record this as R18 (the local/global rule), of the same kind as the others: the genome writes form as a local arrangement read per developmental stage (point-to-able, sequence-encoded), and leaves size as a global parameter of the whole run (a sum over loci and over runtime, not localizable). It subsumes R11 (quantity is runtime) and the depth law, names what every local size-probe negative was telling us, and states the instrument each trait needs: form is read from sequence and arrangement; size is read from the functional and runtime layers, or from many individuals jointly. “In the genome” and “localizable to one place” are different claims — form is both; size is only the first.

38 Version 7: making the switch run — the condensate and response switches

Section 4.1 gave the two knobs (\(\gamma\), \(\phi\)) and the capacity identity \(N\,v_{\mathrm{eff}}\approx\phi\,V_{\mathrm{core}}\), but left the switch — what happens as a domain crosses capacity — as a statement rather than a runnable object. Version 7 makes it a deterministic, reproducible model, because the switch is the mechanism that explains how a small, distributed (global) change in setting can produce a large, discrete outcome — the sharpness behind both body-size differences and obesity.

Model 1 — the condensate switch.

Read the firm interface as a two-state order parameter \(s\) in a double-well free energy \[F(s;h)\;=\;\tfrac14 s^4 \;-\; \tfrac12\,\gamma\,s^2 \;-\; h\,s, \qquad h=h(\phi)=k\,(\phi-\phi_c),\] where \(\gamma\) (sequence-set, Layer 1) fixes the barrier — the threshold — and \(\phi\) (runtime, Layer 2) tilts the wells. Algorithm (in words). Relax \(s\) by gradient descent \(\dot s=-\partial F/\partial s=-(s^3-\gamma s-h)\) to the nearest stable state; sweep \(\phi\) upward and then downward, each time starting from the previous state, so the tracked state reveals both bistability and hysteresis. Result. The state flips almost vertically as \(\phi\) crosses the \(\gamma\)-set threshold (maximum sensitivity \(\mathrm{d}s/\mathrm{d}\phi\approx 438\) in the locked run) and the up- and down-sweeps differ (a hysteresis loop): two cells — or two animals — with the same \(\gamma\) and only a small difference in \(\phi\) can occupy opposite states (lean/obese, small/large). Code: stiffness_switch.py.

# F(s; h) = s^4/4 - gamma*s^2/2 - h*s        (h = tilt set by fill phi)

Model 2 — the response switch (the cell that receives the hormone).

The concrete biology behind the abstract switch is the cell-cycle restriction point: the Rb–E2F system is a known bistable, hysteretic switch. The input is the hormone/mitogen level the cell receives (the response-side \(\phi\)); the conserved switch hardware (the \(\gamma\) side) reads it and commits to divide sharply. Algorithm (in words). Integrate a positive-feedback rate law \(\dot E = b + k_1\,S/(K_m+S) + g\,E^{n}/(K_d^{n}+E^{n}) - d\,E\) to its stable fixed point; sweep the input \(S\) up and down for hysteresis; vary the feedback gain \(g\) (the response sensitivity, a setting) and watch the commit threshold move. Result. A more sensitive cell commits to divide at a lower hormone level, so it divides more and the tissue grows larger — the response setting (not the hardware) sets the size outcome. Their content is the framework’s own prediction made to run: a conserved switch (\(\gamma\)) plus a small change in a global setting (\(\phi\)) near the boundary yields opposite, discrete states. This is the amplifier that turns a distributed, global size setting into a large, discrete body-size or body-weight difference — the runnable companion to R18 (Section 37) and to R11. We record the mechanism as R19 (the conserved response switch): a conserved bistable switch (\(\gamma\) hardware, sequence-set) whose runtime setting (\(\phi\)) flips the discrete state near the boundary — model tier, with single-locus (Rb–E2F) support.

38.1 Version 7: how the two knobs reconnect — a target \(\to\) rate \(\to\) stiffness hypothesis

The local/global rule (R18) and the switch (R19) leave one honest loose end: if the size value is a global parameter set over the whole run, how is it physically imposed on a tissue? We record a candidate mechanism as an explicit hypothesis, kept separate from the verified results.

Status — hypothesis. R25 (target \(\to\) rate \(\to\) stiffness; hypothesis). A size target (a global, \(\phi\)-type quantity) is implemented by tuning growth rate and duration, and is coupled back to the sequence-set stiffness \(\gamma\) through mechanotransduction: the tissue’s mechanical state — the firmness the shell of Section 4.1 names — is sensed by the Hippo/YAP pathway, which throttles proliferation toward an organ-size set-point. This closes the loop between the two knobs, \(\gamma\) (Layer 1, stiffness/threshold) and \(\phi\) (Layer 2, runtime fill/quantity), at the tissue scale, and ties the size question to R17 (the stiffness shell as both a structural constraint and a mechanical signal). It is a hypothesis, not a measurement: confirming it needs functional data (growth-rate, organ-size, and YAP-activity assays) that the present sequence-only method does not supply. It predicts that a distributed change in the rate/duration setting — not any single “size locus” — moves body size, consistent with every lineage-controlled null of Sections 36.7–36.8 and with the elephant trunk being a growth-layer, not a coordinate.

39 Two worked examples, step by step

Part II gave a worked example — the neighbourhood of the E. coli replication origin — so that a reader could follow the framework’s pipeline by hand. In the same spirit, and because this Part rests on measurements a reader will rightly want to reproduce, we walk through the two central cis measurements end to end. Each is written so that someone new to the framework can repeat it from the public assemblies with the scripts in the bundle.

39.1 Worked example 1: reading the snake’s limb enhancer (ZRS)

The question.

Did the limbless python delete its limb enhancer, or merely turn it down? This is a question about a cis setting, and it is answerable by comparing sequences.

Step 1 — locate the locus from coordinates we already have.

From the human gene table, SHH sits on chromosome 7 at \(155{,}799{,}980\)–\(155{,}812{,}463\), and LMBR1 — the gene whose intron hosts the ZRS — at \(156{,}669{,}012\)–\(156{,}893{,}183\). The ZRS is therefore expected near the SHH-distal end of LMBR1, about one million bases from SHH. We request the whole span as one region: NC_000007.14:155799980–156893183.

Step 2 — obtain the sequence as a region, not a genome.

Through the NCBI Genome Data Viewer we download the FASTA for exactly that range. It is \(1{,}093{,}204\) bases — about one megabyte of text, not the multi-gigabyte whole chromosome — which is why the reproducibility bundle stays small. We do the same for the python, whose assembly is scaffold-level: LMBR1 sits on scaffold NW_006534369.1, so we take NW_006534369.1:1–150000.

Step 3 — find the ZRS window in the human locus.

The region begins at genomic coordinate \(155{,}799{,}980\), so a feature at genomic position \(p\) sits at offset \(p-155{,}799{,}980\) in our file. The ZRS lies at roughly \(156{,}790{,}500\)–\(156{,}791{,}800\), i.e. offset \(\approx\!990{,}520\)–\(991{,}820\). We cut that \(1{,}300\)-base window as the human ZRS.

Step 4 — compare by exact shared words, with controls.

We ask how many exact \(15\)-base words (\(15\)-mers) the human ZRS window shares with the entire python LMBR1 region. A \(15\)-mer is long enough that an exact match almost never happens by chance — in \(150\) kilobases the expected number of matches to a given \(15\)-mer is about \(10^{-9}\) — so a large count of shared \(15\)-mers is direct evidence of conserved sequence, with no alignment heuristics or scoring choices to argue about. To keep ourselves honest we run the same count on three controls: the LMBR1 promoter, an arbitrary intronic window, and a SHH coding exon.

Step 5 — read the result.

The ZRS window shares \(112\) exact \(15\)-mers with the python; the LMBR1 promoter shares \(7\); the random intron shares \(1\); the SHH exon shares \(0\). The ZRS signal stands \(112\)-fold above the random-intron control. The snake keeps a recognizable ZRS. The tidy “the enhancer was deleted” story is false for this animal.

Step 6 — look inside the enhancer.

Mapping which bases of the human ZRS are covered by shared \(15\)-mers, \(18\%\) of the \(1{,}300\)-base window is conserved — a conservative floor, since an exact \(15\)-mer is broken by even one substitution (a local alignment of the same windows scores about \(77\%\) identity). The conserved material clusters in a core (longest conserved blocks \(\sim\!58\), \(\sim\!38\), \(\sim\!29\) bases); the long diverged stretches lie in the flanks. The reading is conserved core, diverged flanks — a retained enhancer that has been tuned, not a deleted one.

What we may conclude, and what we may not.

We may conclude that the python retains a conserved ZRS core, which refutes deletion and is consistent with the vestigial pelvic spurs pythons still grow. We may not conclude, from sequence alone, exactly how much the enhancer’s activity is reduced; conservation constrains activity but is not a direct assay of it. That is the honest edge of the measurement, and we mark it.

39.2 Worked example 2: measuring an early-development cis setting (SIX3)

The question.

The earliest forebrain master is SIX3. Human and mouse have very different head shapes. Is that difference carried by the presence or absence of SIX3’s enhancers, or by fine tuning within enhancers that both species share?

Step 1 — obtain matched regions in two species.

From the gene tables we find SIX3 and take \(\sim\!300\) kilobases around it in each species, enough to include the distal enhancers that sit far from the gene: human NC_000002.12:44791702–45096071 and mouse NC_000083.7:85771036–86083619. Each is about a third of a megabyte of text.

Step 2 — scan the human locus in windows.

We slide a \(500\)-base window along the human locus in \(100\)-base steps. For each window we count the exact \(15\)-mers it shares with the entire mouse locus. A window that shares many \(15\)-mers is a stretch of sequence conserved between the two species — a conserved non-coding element, i.e. an enhancer (or the gene itself).

Step 3 — separate the gene from its enhancers.

The SIX3 coding body occupies only about \(4\) kilobases (offset \(\approx\!150{,}000\)–\(154{,}369\) in our window). We exclude it, so that what remains is the regulatory landscape — the conserved non-coding blocks, which are the enhancers.

Step 4 — read the conserved enhancers.

Twenty-eight conserved non-coding enhancers are shared between human and mouse, with a total conservation signal of \(4{,}341\) (the summed peak shared \(15\)-mer counts). The two strongest sit far upstream — one about \(102\) kilobases away (peak \(402\)), another about \(139\) kilobases away (peak \(346\)) — exactly where SIX3’s known distal forebrain enhancers are reported to lie, a reassuring external check that the method finds real enhancers and not artefacts.

Step 5 — the conclusion, and the scope.

Between two species of markedly different head and brain proportion, the forebrain enhancers are conserved in existence. So the species-distinguishing difference is not the gain or loss of these enhancers; it is fine tuning within them — the same reading the ZRS forced for the limb, now at the top of the cascade. The scope is honest: we measured conservation (the existence and rough strength of each enhancer) between two species, not the precise activity difference between the human and mouse versions, and not a panel of many species.

Why this matters for the whole paper.

These two examples are the method behind every cis number in Part VI. The per-locus cis-strengths of Section 31.5, the proxy-disagreement result, and the thresholds that drive the version-3 simulation are all produced by exactly this procedure: take matched regions, count exact shared words in sliding windows, separate gene from landscape, and read the conserved enhancers. It uses no alignment heuristics and no tunable scores, so it is deterministic and a reader can reproduce every figure to the digit.

40 Why this reading differs from the standard one

This framework reaches conclusions that differ from several widely held readings of how genomes make organisms. Because the framework is new, we owe the reader an explicit, fair account of where we differ and why — and we settle each difference by measurement, not by assertion. We are not disputing anyone’s data; we look at the same genomes. What differs is which layer we read as the species-distinguishing information. In each pair below we state the standard reading, our reading, the reason, and the evidence, and we note where the standard reading is right within its range. The honest summary is that the standard readings are not wrong so much as aimed at the wrong layer: they describe the parts, while the species-distinguishing information is in the arrangement and the early cis setting.

40.1 What makes a species

Status — verified. Standard reading: species differ chiefly in their parts — genes gained or lost, and changes in protein sequence. Our reading: the parts are largely shared; what distinguishes species is the arrangement of those parts and the cis setting of their control. Why: part-distance does not order species cleanly (a multi-function vector, not one distance), and the clearest separations come from arrangement and regulation, not parts. Evidence: human and chimpanzee proteins are \(\sim\!99\%\) identical yet the chromosome-2 fusion blocks meiotic pairing; the limbless python retains nine of nine core limb genes. Where the standard view holds: protein-coding change is real and matters for specific molecular traits (e.g. enzyme kinetics, pathogen resistance); our claim is about what carries the species boundary, which is arrangement and early cis, not parts.

40.2 How form is set

Status — INADMISSIBLE — Article IV (pattern). Standard reading (one common version): morphological novelty comes from evolving the developmental “toolkit” — changing the patterning genes. Our reading: the toolkit (the patterning circuit) is conserved; form differs in the cis settings that deploy it. Why: animals that have lost a structure still carry its full circuit, so the change cannot be in the parts. Evidence: the python retains the limb circuit and a conserved ZRS (measured: \(112\) shared \(15\)-mers versus \(1\) for control); eight body features each retain their circuit in a species that reduced the feature. Where the standard view holds: toolkit genes do occasionally change, and protein-level changes in patterning genes exist; our evidence says the dominant route for the cases we measured is cis setting on a conserved circuit.

40.3 What an enhancer is, over evolutionary time

Status — INADMISSIBLE — Article IV (pattern). Standard reading: enhancers turn over rapidly — individual enhancers are gained and lost frequently between species. Our reading: the early, top-of-cascade enhancers are conserved in existence, and species differ by fine tuning within them. Why: the earliest switches are anchors on which everything later is built, so they are the most deeply fixed. Evidence: SIX3 shares \(28\) conserved non-coding enhancers and OTX2 shares \(48\) between human and mouse, two species of very different head shape. Where the standard view holds: enhancer turnover is real for many late, peripheral enhancers; our claim is specifically about the deep, early layer, where turnover is low precisely because the cost of change is high.

40.4 How to measure cis

Status — INADMISSIBLE — Article IV (pattern). Standard reading: regulatory regions can be located by proxies — CpG islands, generic conservation scores. Our reading: proxies can be inverted relative to the truth; a direct cross-species measurement must overrule them. Why: a proxy captures a correlate, not the quantity. Evidence: our measured human–mouse enhancer conservation disagrees sharply with CpG density — OTX2 measures strong yet is CpG-poor (\(1.00\) versus \(0.12\)), NODAL the reverse (\(0.11\) versus \(1.00\)). Where the standard view holds: CpG islands really do mark many active promoters; the lesson is not that the proxy is useless but that, when a direct measurement is available, it wins.

40.5 Why two species cannot interbreed

The standard account here has a name, and it is worth stating fully because we build on it rather than against it. The Dobzhansky–Muller model explains reproductive isolation as the accumulation of incompatible mutations: two populations diverge, each fixing changes that work fine on its own genetic background but clash when combined in a hybrid, so the hybrid is sterile or inviable. It is a powerful and well-supported idea, and nothing in our data contradicts it. What we add is structure: we name two concrete, measurable kinds of incompatibility and argue that a pair must clear both, which is why a single divergence number never ordered the outcomes.

Status — interpretation. Standard reading: reproductive isolation arises mainly from accumulated genetic incompatibilities (the Dobzhansky–Muller model) — diverging genomes fix mutations that clash on the hybrid background. Our reading: compatibility is a conjunction of two specific, measurable barriers — chromosomes that pair at meiosis (arrangement, Barrier A) and early cis states that agree so the embryo can build (development, Barrier B). Why: a single divergence distance cannot order the observed outcomes, but a conjunction of two conditions can — a pair can be close on one barrier and far on the other. Evidence: sheep–goat and sheep–cow are near-identical on every arrangement metric (Jaccard \(0.852\) vs \(0.847\); \(23/26\) vs \(23/26\) one-to-one chromosomes) yet differ in outcome, which a second (cis-state) barrier explains; horse–donkey (a live, sterile mule) and human–chimpanzee (no viable hybrid) invert on a single arrangement axis. Where the standard view holds: the Dobzhansky–Muller picture is correct and general; our Barrier A (arrangement mismatch blocking meiosis) and Barrier B (early-cis conflict blocking development) are two concrete, measurable instances of exactly the incompatibilities it describes. We are giving the model coordinates, not replacing it. This pairing is an interpretation with a stated test (H-cis2).

40.6 Where the body plan comes from

Status — principle-demonstration. Standard reading (informal): the genome is a blueprint that specifies the body. Our reading: the body plan self-organizes from conserved circuits read by cis settings; the genome supplies the circuit and the setting, not a picture of the result. Why: a single maternal asymmetry plus conserved reaction–diffusion circuits is sufficient to lay down axes and domains, with no body plan drawn in. Evidence: the simulation of Section 33 self-organizes head-tail, dorsal-ventral, and left-right axes and five domains, and one cis threshold changes the form. Where the standard view holds: “blueprint” is a useful shorthand for the fact that the outcome is reliable and heritable; our point is mechanistic — the reliability comes from self-organization under fixed rules, not from a stored image.

40.7 Where size lives, and what non-coding DNA is for

Status — INADMISSIBLE — Article IV (pattern). Standard readings: body size is carried by “size genes” or growth-hormone dosage; and non-coding DNA is largely inert. Our readings: size is a conserved circuit (GH–IGF) plus a cis setting (the breed data point to a regulatory change at IGF1); and at the genes that decide the body plan, the non-coding landscape is the control surface, not filler. Evidence: the growth circuit is single-copy across a \(4\) kg cat and a \(200\) kg tiger; the early master genes are \(91\)–\(99\%\) non-coding over their loci (GSC \(99.4\%\), SOX2 \(99.2\%\)). Where the standard views hold: growth-hormone level (a Layer-2 quantity) genuinely sets growth, consistent with our reading that the setting is runtime; and much non-coding DNA elsewhere may indeed lack sequence-specific function — our claim is local to developmental control loci, where it plainly does not.

Where the magnitude lives is not resolved by conservation.

  • The conservation law gains a depth axis. Part II’s “importance predicts fixedness” now extends into the cis layer with a depth statement: the earlier in the developmental cascade a cis element acts, the more deeply it is fixed, because everything downstream is built on it.

  • Two layers become three. Part IV’s part–arrangement decoupling (parts shared, arrangement reorganized) gains a third level: parts (shared) \(\subset\) arrangement (reorganized between clades) \(\subset\) early cis setting (the species-defining layer).

42 New propositions and hypotheses

We register version 6’s claims in the paper’s two registers — rules (present-tense regularities we measured) and hypotheses (testable proposals with stated falsification conditions). Each hypothesis names what would refute it, because a proposal that nothing could refute is not science.

Rules (measured).

  • R20 (conserved circuit, cis setting). For a trait that differs between species, the building circuit is conserved single-copy and the difference is a cis setting. Evidence: growth and morphogen circuits single-copy across cat/tiger/mouse/human/dog/python; the limbless python retains \(9/9\) core limb genes. Status: verified.

  • R21 (retained-but-tuned enhancer). A lost or reduced body feature can leave its enhancer retained but weakened, not deleted. Evidence: python ZRS shares \(112\) exact \(15\)-mers with human versus \(1\) for control; conserved core, diverged flanks. Status: [INADMISSIBLE — Article IV: pattern-derived, not interpretation](pattern: shared-\(k\)-mer counting).

  • R22 (early masters are cis landscapes). The earliest developmental master genes are tiny genes in vast non-coding landscapes. Evidence: \(91\)–\(99\%\) non-coding over their loci (GSC \(99.4\%\)). Status: verified (coordinate/length; restored — read from annotation, not similarity).

  • R23 (early enhancers conserved in existence). Top-of-cascade enhancers are conserved between species of different form. Evidence: SIX3 \(28\), OTX2 \(48\) conserved human–mouse enhancers. Status: [INADMISSIBLE — Article IV: pattern-derived, not interpretation](pattern: conserved-enhancer counting).

  • R24 (a proxy can invert). A sequence proxy (CpG density) for enhancer strength can be inverted relative to direct conservation measurement; direct measurement overrules it. Evidence: OTX2 \(1.00\) measured versus \(0.12\) proxy; NODAL the reverse. Status: [INADMISSIBLE — Article IV: pattern-derived, not interpretation](pattern: conservation vs CpG-density proxy).

Hypotheses (testable).

  • H-cis1 (early cis defines the species). The cis state of top-of-cascade switches is the species-defining layer. Predicts: closely related species that differ in body plan will differ measurably in early cis state. Refuted if: body- plan differences are found to track protein-coding change with early cis held constant, or if early cis varies freely within a species without phenotypic consequence.

  • H-cis2 (two-barrier interbreeding). Interbreeding requires both chromosome pairing (arrangement) and agreement of early cis state. Predicts: among pairs that pass the arrangement barrier, those that cannot interbreed will differ at a top-of-cascade cis switch. Refuted if: interbreeding outcome is fully predicted by arrangement alone across a panel of pairs, leaving no role for early cis state.

  • H-cis3 (early-cis depth law). The earlier in the cascade a cis element acts, the lower its within-species variation and the higher its cross-species conservation. Predicts: a monotone relationship between cascade depth and fixedness. Refuted if: late-acting cis elements are found as conserved and invariant as the earliest ones. Supported? [INADMISSIBLE — Article IV: pattern-derived, not interpretation](pattern: shared-\(k\)-mer conservation ratio) — the early organizer cis measured \(8.3\times\) more conserved (human–mouse) than the late growth cis (IGF1/IGF2/HMGA2), by the same method on the same species pair (Section 36) — the late layer is the divergent one, as the law predicts.

  • H-form (circuit\(+\)cis universality). Every between-species form difference decomposes into a conserved circuit plus a cis setting. Predicts: for any reduced or altered feature, the circuit will be found retained and the difference cis-regulatory. Refuted if: a form difference is found to require the gain or loss of a core circuit gene rather than a cis change.

  • H-emergence (self-organized body plan). Body axes and regional domains self-organize from conserved circuits read by cis thresholds, rather than being specified position-by- position. Predicts: minimal circuit\(+\)threshold models reproduce axis and domain formation and respond to single-threshold changes (as our simulation shows). Refuted if: axis formation is shown to require position-specific instructions not reducible to such circuits.

43 Discussion: one mechanism across scales

Pulled together, versions 1–6 tell a single story that descends through levels and arrives at one mechanism. At the level of bulk sequence, a genome specifies position and local shape, while the cell sets quantity (Parts I–II). At the level of arrangement across genomes, the parts are shared and what distinguishes higher taxa is how the command architecture is organized and centralized (Part IV). At the level of form and lifespan, the body is a locked core plus an expandable surface, and a cis switch like telomerase’s is set at runtime (Part V). And at the level this version reaches — the cis-regulatory sequence itself (Part VI) — size and form turn out to be the same kind of object: a conserved circuit read by a cis setting, with the earliest settings the most deeply fixed and therefore the most truly species-defining.

The throughline is the two-layer asymmetry stated on the first page: the genome encodes position and wiring strongly, and quantity weakly, leaving quantity to the runtime. A cis setting is exactly where those two layers touch. Its disposition — that an enhancer exists, where it sits, which gene it reads — is Layer 1, written in the sequence and conserved. Its value — how strongly it fires, and so how big a limb, how long a tail, how large a forebrain — is the regulated quantity, the knob. Evolution between related species, on this reading, is mostly the turning of these knobs on a shared machine, with the deepest knobs turned most rarely because everything is built on them.

This is why we could measure it. A knob that is a sequence is a knob you can read. The snake did not throw away its limb machine; it turned the limb knob down, and the turned-down knob is still legible in its DNA, conserved core and diverged flanks. The forebrain enhancers that a human and a mouse share are the same machine with the knobs set differently. And when we put conserved circuits and cis thresholds into a simulation, a body plan organized itself, and turning one knob changed the form — which is the whole claim, made to run.

We have been careful to mark the edges. Circuit presence we measured directly and broadly; cis conservation we measured directly but for two species and at the loci where we obtained sequence; the developmental-barrier account of interbreeding and the species-defining role of early cis are interpretations with stated tests; the simulation is a sufficiency demonstration, not a quantitative model. The immediate next experiments are concrete and within reach: run the simulation under human versus mouse measured cis to reproduce a species form difference; measure early cis states across a panel of interbreeding-tested pairs to test the two-barrier hypothesis; and extend the enhancer-conservation measurement to more species to test the depth law. Each is falsifiable, and each follows the same discipline as everything before it: read the sequence, measure what it says, and let the measurement — not the proxy, not the prior, not the consensus — decide.

Objects (A4). shell (stiffness segment) \(\cdot\) anchor (fixed boundary or recognized position) \(\cdot\) motor (machine site) \(\cdot\) loop (3D contact). Position in the sequence is a coordinate on the helix (\(3.4\) Å and \(34.29^\circ\) per base pair).

46.2 The GC weight, decoded and then confirmed

The “GC weight” of the stiffness signal is not a chosen \(1.0\): it is the slope of GC against duplex stacking stability computed from the SantaLucia (1998) unified nearest-neighbour free energies. On mouse Ihh the stability is \(+0.84 + 1.10\,\mathrm{GC}\) (coefficient of determination \(0.987\)), so the weight is the physics slope \(+1.10\) kcal/mol per unit GC. This decode generalises: across \(51\) regions spanning six species and four functional classes (growth-plate, early-development master genes, enhancers/limb, systemic-growth loci) the GC\(\to\)stacking fit holds with \(R^{2}>0.96\) at every locus, and the A4 pipeline’s self-gates pass on all \(51\). Cross-species \(\gamma\) at the same locus is conserved (cat versus tiger, across a \(50\times\) body-mass difference, \(|\Delta\gamma|=0.002\)), consistent with \(\gamma\) being set by sequence composition rather than size. Finally, the same weight’s sign is confirmed on measured data: regressing an in-vivo MNase nucleosome-occupancy map (Tsankov et al. 2010) on sequence over \(23{,}094\) windows gives a strong positive GC coefficient (standardised \(\beta_{\mathrm{GC}}=+4.06\), \(t=+27\)). The GC weight thus rests on three independent legs: thermodynamics, cross-species generalisation, and measured occupancy.

46.3 The stiffness shell, made explicit

A bare condensate droplet is held round by interfacial tension \(\gamma\) (sequence-set, Layer 1) but is fragile: it resists deformation only through a small Laplace stress \(\sim\gamma/R\) and ruptures on contact. Surface tension gives shape, not rigidity. Rigidity appears when a structured shell forms at the interface — a \(\mathrm{pH}\)/acid–base–controlled mineral deposit (calcium carbonate or phosphate) or a cross-linked layer — with a thin-shell buckling stress \(\sim k\,E\,(t/R)^{2}\,g(c)\), where \(g(c)\) has a percolation threshold near \(c^{*}\!=\!0.5\) because only a connected membrane bears load. In the consolidated model the bare droplet sits near \(10^{2}\) Pa while a closed calcium shell reaches \(\sim\!3\times10^{4}\) Pa — about \(300\times\) more robust — and increasing \(\gamma\) alone (even tenfold) cannot approach the shelled value. The genome (Layer 1) sets the soft phase and its composition (\(\gamma\)); the runtime (Layer 2) supplies the structuring material (calcium, phosphate) and triggers the deposition that closes the shell, so the shell/boundary is partly emergent — matching the predicted-negative result that compartment boundaries are not sequence-placed (area-under-curve \(\approx 0.52\)). The same thin-shell law scales from a micron condensate to the eggshell and the amniotic membrane, and to growth-plate endochondral mineralisation (cartilage hardened into bone by calcium phosphate); these are one principle — soft interior plus structured surface equals rigidity — across scales (the scale-invariance rule). This also explains why, in the response switch, higher \(\gamma\) yields a more robust (not steeper) bistability: barrier height \(\propto\gamma^{2}\) and hysteresis width \(\propto\gamma^{3/2}\), because a structured shell resists deformation.

46.4 The response switch and the size integral

The R19 condensate switch \(F(s;h)=\tfrac14 s^{4}-\tfrac12\gamma s^{2}-hs\) no longer carries free numbers: its barrier is the sequence-read \(\gamma\) (so the same nearest-neighbour physics that decodes the GC weight sets the switch threshold), and \(\phi_{c}\) is identified as the Layer-2 fill (the runtime operating point). The size model is written as \(\mathrm{SIZE}=\int \big(\,\text{reader\_gain}[\text{sequence}] \times \text{material\_supply}[\text{Layer-2}]\,\big)\,dt\): the genome encodes the readers (a conserved, single-copy growth-and-appetite circuit), the environment supplies the limited materials, and size is their runtime integral — which is why every local size probe returned conserved. Both remain illustrative-model tier.

46.5 The finite materials

Two finite “materials” are catalogued, one per scale. Material 1 (molecular): the ten unique nearest-neighbour stacking free energies (SantaLucia 1998), which give \(\gamma\) and decode the GC weight, ground the CpG and A-tract terms, and set the switch threshold. Material 2 (organism): the limited body-building inputs (amino-acid nitrogen, energy, calcium, phosphate), read by the conserved circuit; here the stiffness-shell mechanism upgrades calcium and phosphate from mere “growth fuel” to the shell-structuring material that converts the soft, \(\gamma\)-shaped phase into a rigid shell. Both obey one law: a finite material, read by a conserved reader, sets a global quantity not stored at any single locus.

46.6 Larger animals: \(\gamma\) across \(\sim\!2\times10^{5}\)-fold body mass

The cat-versus-tiger pair (\(\sim\!50\times\) mass) is extended to four species spanning about \(200{,}000\times\) — mouse (\(\sim\!0.025\) kg), cat (\(\sim\!4\) kg), tiger (\(\sim\!220\) kg), elephant (\(\sim\!5000\) kg) — on the five orthologous growth-plate loci IHH, FGFR3, NPR2, PTHLH, SOX9 (biggest_animals_v8.py). The sequence-set stiffness \(\gamma\) is conserved (mean within-orthologue coefficient of variation \(2.0\%\), below the between-gene \(3.4\%\)) and, decisively, does not track body size: after removing each gene’s mean, the correlation of \(\gamma\) with \(\log_{10}\) body mass is \(+0.002\). Individual loci show only weak, opposite-signed trends (FGFR3 up, PTHLH down, neither significant on four points) that cancel, and the largest single spread (NPR2) is a felid-lineage offset, not a size effect. By Article IV this is not a similarity argument; the admissible result is the absence of a size signal in the knob. A \(200{,}000\times\) size difference leaves \(\gamma\) where it is — exactly what “size is Layer 2 (runtime), not written at these loci” predicts.

46.7 The protein intermediate

The genome does not act on structure directly: it is transcribed and translated, and proteins are the agents in between. Making this explicit sharpens the two-layer reading at three nodes. (i) The readers of the size machinery — the GH/IGF and appetite circuit (e.g. GHRHR, POU1F1, IGF1/2) — are proteins, so the “reader gain” of the size integral is a protein’s activity. (ii) The shell-builders are proteins: RUNX2 (the master mineralisation factor), SOX9 (the master chondrocyte factor), the IHH/PTHLH signalling proteins that set the mineralisation front, and the enzymes and scaffolds (alkaline phosphatase, collagen) that deposit and structure the calcium shell — so the stiffness-shell mechanism is protein-executed: the runtime calcium (Layer 2) is laid down by protein machinery encoded in Layer 1. (iii) Tissue is protein, so size is accumulated protein: the Layer-2 material (amino-acid nitrogen, energy, Ca, P) becomes protein, and \(\mathrm{SIZE}=\int(\text{reader gain})\times(\text{material}\!\to\!\text{protein synthesis})\,dt\). This explains in one move why form is conserved while size varies: the same proteins (hence the conserved \(\gamma\) measured above), built up in larger or smaller amount as the runtime material allows. Reading the proteomes directly (protein_v8.py) confirms this across six species from mouse to the humpback whale — about \(1{,}200{,}000\times\) body mass — with the best-annotated reference, Homo sapiens, included as a final check. On the six growth-plate proteins, length is conserved (coefficient of variation \(3\%\)) and the condensate-relevant sticker composition (aromatic plus charged residues — a protein-level \(\gamma\) proxy) is tightly conserved (within-orthologue \(1.5\%\) against a between-gene \(10.4\%\); SOX9 varies by \(0.2\%\) across all six); after removing each protein’s mean, its correlation with \(\log_{10}\) body mass is \(+0.05\). Adding the largest animal had earlier removed a weak four-species lean (\(r\) had been \(+0.27\)), and the human reference leaves the null intact: there is no size code. The protein — the conserved form — is Layer 1; how much of it accumulates is Layer 2. The conserved DNA \(\gamma\) above is the shadow of conserved proteins.

46.8 The open frontier, and a method that corrects itself

A finished interpreter earns trust by being exact about its frontier. Two fine-scale chromatin questions are deliberately left open, each with a precise next experiment that would settle it. (i) The A-tract term w_at is not promoted. Regressed against the in-vivo MNase occupancy, the A-tract marginal effect after controlling for GC is essentially zero (partial correlation \(+0.007\)); aligned on A-tract centres, short tracts (\(\ge 6\)) show a mild depletion (\(0.81\times\)) but longer tracts are spuriously enriched (\(\ge 10\!:\!1.26\times\), \(\ge 15\!:\!2.68\times\), \(\ge 20\!:\!3.64\times\)), the opposite of exclusion and with the wrong dose-response. This is the signature of MNase’s A/T cleavage bias compounded by a handful of rare long-tract loci, not a refutation of the physics. w_at therefore stays grounded-in-literature. (ii) The \(\sim\!10\) bp positioning periodicity is present at the correct period in the WW power spectrum (\(10.14\) bp) but only \(1.14\times\) above the local baseline, and the dyad-aligned profile smears because in-vivo MNase peaks are not base-pair-precise. Both converge on the same need: a base-pair-precise chemical (Brogaard) nucleosome-dyad map in the same (R64/sacCer3) build as the sequence. With that single small file, both tests can be done cleanly; with in-vivo MNase they cannot.

The pipeline is built to catch its own mistakes, and four are recorded in the open rather than quietly fixed — which is how it stays trustworthy: a load normalisation artifact in the integrator; a naive “higher \(\gamma\) gives a sharper switch” claim (the true dependence is more robust, not steeper); a composition bug that divided GC by window length and let N-steps dilute the stacking average, spuriously tanking \(R^{2}\) on a gap-rich locus (mouse POU5F1, \(16\%\) N) and fixed to “composition over called bases”; and a loose-gate false pass in the periodicity test (raw autocorrelation), replaced by a power-spectrum prominence gate.

A Learning the method from zero — a curriculum

The body of this paper, and the Version-7 changelog, are written for a reader building on the framework. This appendix is the on-ramp for everyone else: a staged path from background knowledge to running the method and interpreting a locus. It is deliberately friendly — a plain idea first, the formal version second — because the single hardest idea (that size is in the genome yet not findable in any one place) is hard for experts too.

A.1 The one analogy to carry throughout

A genome is like a recipe that builds a specific cake. Where the layers and filling go is written explicitly — you can point to the line — and that is position / form (Layer 1). How big the cake comes out is also fully decided by the recipe, but not by one line: it is the sum of every amount and every baking time. That is size / quantity (Layer 2): in the recipe, but spread across the whole of it, not in a single line you can point to. Almost the entire method is making that one distinction precise and measurable.

A.2 Prerequisites, and how to use this

Four light backgrounds suffice: a little molecular biology (genes, transcription, enhancers, copy number); a little intuitive physics (phase separation / droplets, surface tension, a free-energy well, a bistable switch); basic Python (run a script; numpy, scipy, Biopython, matplotlib); and basic genomics (FASTA and GFF files, an accession, sequence alignment / percent identity). Do the stages in order. Each stage states a goal, what to read in this paper, what to run from the bundle, and a self-check.

A.3 The ten stages

  1. Stage 0 — Orientation. Install Python and the four libraries; fetch one small FASTA by accession and print its length. Ready when “enhancer,” “phase separation,” “FASTA,” and “run a script” are not scary.

  2. Stage 1 — The two layers. Read: the Introduction and “the two-layer proposition.” Self-check: for any feature, say whether it is position (Layer 1) or quantity (Layer 2) — e.g. which intron hosts an enhancer (1) vs how strongly it fires (2).

  3. Stage 2 — The mechanism. Read: Sections 4–4.1. Run: stiffness_switch.py (Section 38) and watch a bistable switch flip. Self-check: which knob does the sequence set (\(\gamma\)), which does the runtime set (\(\phi\)), and why does overfilling cause a switch rather than a gradual change?

  4. Stage 3 — From letters to structure. Read: the A4 arrangement, the stiffness signal, and Section 3.5. Self-check: from a sequence’s AT/GC pattern alone, predict roughly where soft/stiff shells and their boundaries fall.

  5. Stage 4 — The pipeline, hands-on. Read: Sections 6 and 7. Run: the core pipeline on E. coli and reproduce \(470\) shells / \(471\) anchors / \(4{,}506\) motors / \(9{,}012\) loops with all gates pass; read the Version-7 windowing fix and understand why short loci once broke it. Self-check: name the five steps and what a gate does.

  6. Stage 5 — Reproducibility. Read: Section 9. Do: verify one result end to end — hash the input against DATA_LOCK, run the script, confirm the gate, hash the output against MANIFEST. Self-check: why is “never tuned after the fact” the rule that gives the numbers their force?

  7. Stage 6 — Quantity, and why size is not in the letters. Read: Sections 11, 36, and the lineage-controlled tests 36.7–36.8. Run: dosage.py, crossspecies.py, decisive.py, and the let-7 count; watch the cat-vs-tiger control kill a tempting “size signal.” Self-check: why did every local search for size come back conserved, and what is the difference between “in the genome” and “localizable to one place”?

  8. Stage 7 — Form. Read: Sections 20, 27, and 35. Run: devo_morphogenesis.py and dna_to_animal.py; swap a hand-set limb coordinate for the Hox-read one and watch a deformity become a tetrapod. Self-check: why does reading the coordinate from the Hox arrangement fix the deformity, and what does the genome give cleanly (order/identity) versus not (absolute size)?

  9. Stage 8 — The synthesis. Read: Sections 37 and 38. Capstone: take a new locus, run the pipeline, read its A4, decide whether it is built as a switch or a rheostat, and then state — with reasons — why you cannot read its quantitative setting from the sequence, and what data you would need.

  10. Stage 9 — Mastery. Confirm the rubric below; the fastest check is to teach a newcomer Stages 1, 2, and 6 using only the analogy.

A.4 Common traps (kept visible because we hit them)

  • The lineage confound. A mouse-vs-elephant difference can look like “size” yet be lineage; always control within a family (cat vs tiger). This fooled us once with a clean-looking \(5/5\) signal (Section 36.7).

  • Hand-set coordinates \(\rightarrow\) deformity. Placing body parts at chosen numbers malforms the body; read coordinates from the genome’s position code (Section 35.2).

  • Equal-length Hamming artifact. Comparing two same-length but unaligned sequences gives fake low identity; always align first (GDF15/IRS4 looked divergent until aligned, then conserved).

  • Looking for a “size gene.” There is not one; size is global — read the whole or the runtime, not a single locus.

  • Truncated annotations. Predicted transcript models can cut a \(3'\)UTR and undercount sites; check sequence length before trusting a count.

  • Short-sequence pipeline crash. Loci shorter than the window broke the old pipeline; use the Version-7 fix (window clipped to length).

A.5 Mastery rubric

You have learned the method when you can: (1) run and reproduce the core pipeline and verify a result against DATA_LOCK/MANIFEST; (2) interpret a new region’s A4 with the five steps; (3) classify any feature as Layer-1 position or Layer-2 quantity, and a cis element as switch or rheostat; (4) design a within-family control for a claimed cross-species signal; (5) explain the size result without contradiction — in the genome, global, not localizable; and (6) recognize and avoid the six traps above.
