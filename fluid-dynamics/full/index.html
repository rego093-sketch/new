Abstract

Standard fluid mechanics treats several of its most basic facts as emergent or simply given: the size a vortex “chooses” for its core, the length scale that turbulence “selects,” and the dissipation that “refuses to vanish” as the viscosity is sent to zero. This whitepaper reframes those facts as necessary consequences of arrangement. We take the continuum to be a coarse-grained, jammed configuration of finite-volume particles—a volume-particle (VP) arrangement—and we argue, with reproducible evidence, that the form of fluid motion is configured: its governing equations, its selected lengths, and its dissipation are determined by the underlying arrangement rather than postulated. The argument rests on four independently reproducible pillars: (i) structural—an arrangement with mass and pairwise interaction forces the continuum balance laws as exact identities, with the Navier–Stokes closure following conditionally; (ii) geometric—a rotational core capacity identity fixes an effective core length exactly once the arrangement is specified, reproducing a published cross-regime audit to machine precision; (iii) dynamical—a binding versus penalty competition selects a length L⋆∝(σ/ε)1/2L_\star\propto(\sigma/\varepsilon)^{1/2} with an exact one-half control exponent, independent of the growth offset; and (iv) dissipative—rearrangement events carry a nonnegative dissipation measure whose steady-state budget saturates at the injection rate as viscosity vanishes (an Onsager-type anomaly). Every claim is graded LOCK (locked primitive), DERIVE (proved/derived), or GATE (falsifiable, conditional), and every computational claim ships with a runnable box that was executed to the stated tolerance. We respect the empirical phenomena exactly as standard fluid mechanics records them; what is new is the interpretation—that those phenomena are configured—together with the reproducibility scaffolding that makes the interpretation testable.

2 The reframing: from mystery to necessity

2.1 Three facts usually left as “emergent”

A great deal of fluid mechanics can be done without ever asking why certain forms appear. Three are emblematic.

  1. Core size. A tropical cyclone eye, a mesoscale ocean eddy, and a two-dimensional vortex each present a fairly definite radius. Operationally this radius is measured and used, but in a pure field description there is no a priori reason the flow should admit a single characteristic core length at all.

  2. Length selection. Under suitable forcing, a flow develops structure at a preferred scale. The preferred scale is reproducible, yet in the nonlinear field equations it is not obviously written anywhere; it appears to be “chosen” by the dynamics.

  3. Anomalous dissipation. As viscosity is reduced, the dissipation rate of a turbulent flow does not follow it to zero but tends to a finite limit. The standard field statement of this (the Onsager picture) is correct and empirically supported, but it is usually presented as a property of the inviscid limit rather than as a consequence of anything concrete in the medium.

None of these is a paradox. Each is, in the conventional account, an emergent feature: real, measured, and used, but not derived from a stated structure of the medium. The thesis of this whitepaper is that all three become necessary the moment one takes seriously that the medium has an arrangement.
